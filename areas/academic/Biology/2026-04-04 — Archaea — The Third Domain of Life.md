# Archaea — The Third Domain of Life

Archaea are one of the three domains of life — alongside Bacteria and Eukarya — among the most evolutionarily significant organisms on Earth, overlooked until the 1970s.

---

## Discovery and the Three-Domain Revolution

Until Carl Woese and George Fox's ribosomal RNA sequencing work (1977), life was divided into prokaryotes and eukaryotes. Archaea looked like bacteria — small, no nucleus. But 16S rRNA sequencing showed archaebacteria were as different from bacteria as bacteria are from us.

Tree of life revised:
- Bacteria: one branch
- Archaea + Eukarya: sister groups — more closely related to each other than either is to Bacteria

Implication: the eukaryotic cell likely arose from an archaeal ancestor that incorporated a bacterium (mitochondrion) by endosymbiosis.

---

## What Makes Archaea Distinctive

**Cell membrane — the key difference:**
- Bacterial/eukaryotic membranes: fatty acids + glycerol via **ester bonds**, D-configuration
- Archaeal membranes:
  - **Ether bonds** — more stable, resistant to hydrolysis at extreme temp/pH
  - **Isoprenoid chains** — branched hydrocarbons, not straight fatty acids
  - **L-glycerol** — mirror-image stereochemistry
  - Sometimes **tetraether lipids** — chains spanning the entire membrane as a monolayer. Extremely stable at high temperatures.

**Cell wall:** Have walls but lack **peptidoglycan** — the bacterial cell wall target of penicillin. Use pseudopeptidoglycan, S-layers, or other structures. Penicillin-class antibiotics are ineffective against archaea.

**Transcription/translation — eukaryotic character:**
- RNA polymerase resembles eukaryotic RNA Pol II
- Ribosomes are 70S (like bacteria) but proteins/rRNA sequences more eukaryotic
- Transcription factors TBP and TFB are archaeal homologs of eukaryotic TATA-binding protein and TFIIB

**DNA:**
- Use **histones** to package DNA (like eukaryotes) — structurally homologous, forming nucleosome-like structures. Bacteria don't use histones.
- Single circular chromosome (like bacteria)

## Major Phyla

**Euryarchaeota** — most diverse: methanogens, extreme halophiles (*Halobacterium*), thermoacidophiles

**Crenarchaeota** — hyperthermophiles: *Sulfolobus*, *Pyrolobus fumarii* (grows at 121°C — highest confirmed temperature for any organism), deep-sea vent communities

**Thaumarchaeota** — ammonia oxidizers: dominant in marine water columns, key in global nitrogen cycle, among the most abundant organisms on Earth

**Asgard superphylum** (discovered 2015+): found in deep-sea sediments near Loki's Castle (Greenland/Norway). Genomes contain eukaryotic signature genes: actin, tubulin homologs, ESCRT membrane-remodeling proteins, ubiquitin system. Current leading candidate for archaeal ancestor of eukaryotic cell. *Heimdallarchaeota* = closest known archaeal relative to eukaryotes.

## Methanogens

Strict anaerobes producing methane as metabolic byproduct: CO₂ + 4H₂ → CH₄ + 2H₂O

Sources: anaerobic sediments, wetlands, ruminant digestive tracts (~500L methane/day per cow), rice paddies, landfills.

Methane = potent greenhouse gas (~80× more warming than CO₂ over 20 years). Methanogen activity is a significant climate variable.

Likely among the most ancient metabolic strategies — possibly among the first energy-capturing pathways on early Earth, before oxygen.

---

## Archaea and the Origin of the Eukaryotic Cell

Current leading hypothesis: the eukaryotic cell is an archaeal cell that engulfed an alphaproteobacterium (the mitochondrial ancestor).

Evidence:
- Asgard archaea have eukaryotic signature proteins (actin, ubiquitin, ESCRT machinery)
- Phylogenomics places eukaryotes *within* Archaea, not as sister group
- "Two-domain" model (Bacteria vs. Archaea+Eukarya) has largely displaced three-domain model

Implication: you are a modified archaeon with a bacterial powerplant. Your transcription machinery, histones, membrane-remodeling proteins — archaeal. Your mitochondria, energy metabolism — bacterial.

---

## Archaea in Normal Environments

Early association with extremes was a sampling artifact:
- Abundant in ocean water (Thaumarchaeota among the most numerous organisms on Earth)
- Present in soil, freshwater, atmosphere
- In the human gut — methanogens (*Methanobrevibacter smithii*) dominant archaeal species, affecting caloric extraction from food
- On skin and in the mouth

-   **No confirmed archaeal pathogen.** Despite ubiquity, archaea appear unable to cause human disease. This stands in sharp contrast to the **Bacteria** domain, where structural features like the **Gram-negative** outer membrane and LPS endotoxins (see [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Biology/2026-04-21 — Gram-Negative Bacteria|Gram-Negative Bacteria]]) drive high pathogenicity and systemic inflammatory responses in humans.

## Philosophical Note

"Prokaryote" — defined as "cell without a nucleus" — describes a grade, not a clade. Bacteria and Archaea are both prokaryotes morphologically but are no more closely related to each other than either is to you. The category is **paraphyletic** — groups organisms by what they lack, not by common descent.

Archaea forced biologists to confront that intuitive morphological categories can be profoundly misleading about evolutionary history. The right unit for classification is shared ancestry, not shared appearance.
