# Mycorrhizal Networks

Mycorrhizal networks are fungal root systems connecting trees and plants underground — commonly called the "wood wide web." Individual plants are connected through fungal filaments (hyphae) that extend their root systems by orders of magnitude.

## What the Network Does

- Trees transfer sugars, water, phosphorus, and nitrogen to one another — including to seedlings that cannot yet photosynthesize adequately.
- "Mother trees" (large, old trees with extensive fungal connections) appear to preferentially support their own seedlings.
- When a tree is dying it can release large carbon pulses into the network, potentially feeding neighbours.
- Chemical distress signals propagate through the network, priming connected plants for pest or disease resistance before they are directly attacked.

## Why It Matters

**The unit of analysis problem.** Mycorrhizal networks radically complicate the Darwinian picture of individual competition. The "individual" in a forest ecosystem may not be the tree — it may be the network. Fitness operates at a different level of description than the organism.

This is a biological instance of the [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-03-22 — The Aggregation Problem|Aggregation Problem]]: model the forest at the organism level and you miss the dynamics entirely. The relevant unit of analysis is emergent from the interactions.

**Simple rules, complex behaviour.** Biochemical gradient-following by individual fungal filaments produces network-level resource distribution without any central coordination and without any blueprint. The network self-organises into resource-sharing infrastructure — a clear instance of [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Emergence/2026-03-21 — What Is Emergence|What Is Emergence]].

**Connection to social-scale findings.** Parallels the Dunbar Number insight: what appears to be a collection of individuals is actually a coordinated group with properties not predictable from any individual member.

## Important Caution

Some dramatic claims circulating in popular writing — deliberate sharing, "communication," intentionality — are contested. The fungal network is real and well-documented; the intentionality language is a projection. The mechanism is biochemical gradient-following, not decision-making. The network is genuinely remarkable without requiring anthropomorphism.

## Key Researcher

Suzanne Simard (UBC). Primary popular account: *Finding the Mother Tree* (2021). Peer-reviewed work published in *Nature* from 1997 onward.
