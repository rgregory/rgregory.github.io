# Stromatolites

Stromatolites are layered sedimentary structures built by microbial mats — primarily cyanobacteria — that trap and bind sediment, then precipitate carbonate minerals. They represent one of Earth's oldest biosignatures.

## Key Facts

- **Age**: Known from ~3.5 billion years ago (Archean); disputed traces may push to 3.7 Ga
- **Living examples**: Shark Bay (Western Australia), Lake Thetis, some hypersaline environments — rare because grazing invertebrates outcompete the mats in most modern ecosystems
- **Structure**: Laminated columns or domes; each layer represents a growth cycle of microbial mat + sediment trapping
- **Significance**: Cyanobacterial stromatolites were the primary oxygen producers during the Great Oxidation Event (~2.4 Ga), fundamentally transforming Earth's atmosphere and enabling complex aerobic life

## Why They Matter

Stromatolites are essentially a direct record of early biosphere–geosphere coupling. They demonstrate that life can reshape a planet's chemistry at a global scale — a fact with obvious implications for astrobiology and biosignature detection on other worlds.
