# Explosive Evolution Model — Evolution in Bursts, Not Gradients (2025)

Research published in 2025 strengthened the case for an **explosive (punctuated) model of evolution**, in which evolutionary change occurs in concentrated bursts during periods of ecological disruption, followed by long periods of relative stasis. This challenges the traditional Darwinian picture of gradual, continuous change.

---

## The Classical vs. Punctuated Model

**Classical (gradualist) Darwinism**: evolution proceeds at a roughly constant rate as natural selection continuously filters beneficial mutations. Given sufficient time, small changes accumulate into large ones. The fossil record's apparent gaps are artifacts of incomplete preservation.

**Punctuated equilibrium** (Gould & Eldredge, 1972): the fossil record's gaps are real. Species remain morphologically stable for long periods, then change rapidly at speciation events — "rapidly" on geological timescales meaning thousands to tens of thousands of years, not millions. Change is concentrated at transitions.

The 2025 work goes further, combining:
- **Paleogenomic data** (ancient DNA from fossil specimens across time points)
- **Phylogenetic rate analysis** (measuring substitution rates and morphological change rates at different branches and time periods)
- **Ecological co-occurrence data** (connecting evolutionary rate spikes to environmental disruptions)

---

## The 2025 Findings

**Rate heterogeneity is the rule, not the exception**: across taxonomic groups studied, evolutionary rates (both molecular substitution rates and morphological change rates) were strongly clustered — the vast majority of change occurs in a small fraction of the total lineage time.

**Environmental triggers**: the bursts correlate with:
- Mass extinction events and their aftermath (ecological release, niche vacancy)
- Climate oscillations (glacial cycles, aridification events)
- Key innovations (novel body plans, reproductive strategies, sensory systems) that open new adaptive zones

**Post-burst stasis is real**: after a burst of diversification, lineages enter periods of low rate that cannot be explained by lack of genetic variation — purifying selection and stabilizing selection actively constrain change during stable periods.

**Molecular clocks need recalibration**: standard molecular clock methods assume relatively constant substitution rates; the burst model implies these clocks systematically underestimate the tempo of recent rapid events and overestimate change during stable periods.

---

## Implications

**For the fossil record**: apparent morphological stasis is not evolutionary inactivity — genomes may be diverging even when morphology is stable (neutral evolution, regulatory rewiring without phenotypic change). The burst shows up in morphology when regulatory changes cross a phenotypic threshold.

**For speciation theory**: speciation may be more tightly coupled to ecological disruption than to gradual reproductive isolation. The "ecological speciation" framework gains support.

**For conservation biology**: if species are in evolutionary stasis between bursts, they may be more fragile to rapid environmental change than gradualist models predict — they are not continuously adapting; they are adapted to a stable state and vulnerable to disruptions that exceed the adjustment range.

**For the Cambrian Explosion**: the most dramatic burst in animal evolution (~540 Mya) fits naturally in the burst model — an ecological trigger (oxygenation, snowball Earth aftermath) unlocking rapid morphological diversification from a pre-adapted bilaterian ancestor.
