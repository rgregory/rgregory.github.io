# Retinoic Acid in Wound Healing — Clinical and Regenerative Implications

The 2024 Rockefeller University finding that retinoic acid (RA) governs the exit of hair follicle stem cells from lineage plasticity carries direct implications across three clinical domains: wound care, regenerative medicine, and oncology.

## Wound Healing

Impaired wound healing — chronic wounds, non-healing ulcers, excessive scarring — represents a large unmet clinical burden. The RA-plasticity axis offers a new mechanistic target:

- **Vitamin A deficiency** (common in elderly patients, those with malabsorption, or restricted diets) may directly impair the resolution phase of wound repair, leaving stem cells in an extended plastic state that fails to reconstitute the follicular architecture of normal skin.
- **Topical retinoic acid** is already in clinical use for skin conditions (e.g., tretinoin for photoaging and acne), but its role in the stem cell re-commitment phase during wound repair was not previously understood. This study establishes a specific mechanistic rationale for its wound-healing effects.
- The rheostat model implies that **dosing matters**: too little RA and plasticity is not resolved; too much RA applied too early may prematurely terminate plasticity before repair is complete.

## Regenerative Medicine

Lineage plasticity is a key enabling mechanism for regenerative strategies:

- **Stem cell therapies** depend on cells that can adopt new identities in response to tissue context. Understanding what controls the depth and duration of plasticity gives researchers a dial to tune.
- If RA drives re-commitment, **blocking RA signaling** could extend the plastic window, allowing stem cells to contribute more broadly to tissue repair before being locked back into their origin lineage.
- Conversely, applying RA could accelerate **re-maturation** of engineered tissues, helping grafted cells settle into stable identities more quickly.
- The finding that vitamin A operates as a systemic rheostat suggests that **nutritional status** is a covert variable in regenerative protocols — one that may confound clinical trial results if not controlled.

## Cancer Relevance

Lineage plasticity is not only a feature of wound repair — it is a hallmark of cancer progression:

- **Tumor plasticity** — the ability of cancer cells to switch identities, evade therapy, and repopulate a tumor — involves many of the same molecular pathways that govern normal stem cell plasticity.
- Retinoic acid derivatives are already used in oncology: **all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA)** is the frontline treatment for acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL), where it forces differentiation of arrested blast cells. The Rockefeller finding provides broader context: RA is a general re-commitment signal, not merely an APL-specific quirk.
- If RA normally constrains plasticity, **reduced RA signaling** in a tumor microenvironment could contribute to the extended plastic states that characterize aggressive, therapy-resistant cancers.
- Skin cancers in particular — squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma — arise from the same stem cell populations studied here. This study opens the question of whether RA signaling failure is a step in their pathogenesis.
