# On the Plurality of Worlds — David K. Lewis (1986)

> "As the realm of sets is for mathematicians, so logical space is a paradise for philosophers." — p. 4

**Lewis, David K. (1986). *On the Plurality of Worlds*. Oxford: Blackwell. ISBN 9780631224266.**

Delivered originally as the John Locke Lectures at Oxford, Trinity Term, 1984. This is Lewis's fullest and most systematic defence of Modal Realism — the thesis that there are infinitely many concrete, causally isolated, spatiotemporally extended universes, of which our world is only one.

---

## Executive Summary

Lewis argues that we should believe in a plurality of concrete possible worlds because the hypothesis is theoretically fruitful: it provides the ontological basis for unified and non-circular analyses of modality, counterfactuals, properties, propositions, causation, laws of nature, and mental content. He does not claim this is a conclusive argument — he claims the theoretical benefits are worth the ontological cost, just as the utility of set theory gives mathematicians good reason to believe in sets. The book has four chapters: (1) a defence of modal realism and its theoretical applications; (2) replies to paradoxes and objections; (3) a critique of ersatz alternatives; (4) an analysis of trans-world identity via counterpart theory.

## Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

### Preface (pp. vii–ix)

Lewis states the book's central thesis plainly: "the world we are part of is but one of a plurality of worlds." He sets out the method — reasons that favour the position over its rivals, not knock-down proofs — and explicitly distances himself from anti-realist or "ersatz" programmes. He notes the book grew from his 1984 John Locke Lectures at Oxford.

### Chapter 1 — A Philosophers' Paradise

#### 1.1 The Thesis of Plurality of Worlds (pp. 1–4)

The world is a maximally inclusive spatiotemporal system: everything connected to you spatiotemporally belongs to the same world. Other worlds exist in the same way ours does — they differ in what they contain, not in manner of existence. They are not nearby, not far away — they have no spatial or temporal relations to us at all. They are **causally and spatiotemporally isolated**.

The reason to believe: the hypothesis is serviceable. Just as mathematicians believe in sets because set theory unifies and simplifies mathematics, philosophers should believe in worlds because possible-worlds theory unifies and simplifies philosophy of modality, mind, language, and science.

> "I advocate a thesis of plurality of worlds, or modal realism, which holds that our world is but one world among many." — p. 2

#### 1.2 Modal Realism at Work: Modality (pp. 5–19)

Possibility = existential quantification over worlds. Necessity = universal quantification over worlds. The modal operators (diamond and box) are just restricted quantifiers.

- "Possibly P" iff, for some world W, P is the case at W.
- "Necessarily P" iff, for all worlds W, P is the case at W.
- "Contingently P" iff P is the case at some but not all worlds.

**Counterpart theory and de re modality.** For claims about what an individual could or must be, Lewis introduces quantification over **counterparts**: individuals at other worlds that resemble the original in relevant respects. Humphrey could have won the presidency iff some counterpart of Humphrey wins at some world. This avoids satisfaction *in absentia*.

**Supervenience.** Modal realism allows clean formulation of supervenience theses. "There could be no difference of sort A without difference of sort B" cannot be adequately captured by a sentential modal operator (diamond), which restricts quantifiers to a single world. Properly formulated supervenience requires quantification *across* worlds — exactly what modal realism provides directly.

- **Narrow psychophysical supervenience**: no two people in any world could differ mentally without differing physically.
- **Broad psychophysical supervenience**: no two people, whether in the same world or different worlds, could differ mentally without there being a physical difference somewhere.
- **Supervenience of laws**: two worlds could not differ in their laws without differing in their distribution of local qualitative character.

The sentential diamond collapses the cross-world quantification and makes the laws-supervenience thesis trivially true (there is only one world W in a statement about W). Modal realism avoids this distortion.

#### 1.3 Modal Realism at Work: Closeness (pp. 20–26)

Analysis of **counterfactuals** via similarity/closeness between worlds. "If A had been the case, C would have been the case" = in the closest worlds where A is the case, C is the case. The notion of closeness is not a primitive — it is grounded in overall qualitative similarity, weighted by what matters to the case in hand (nomological match, historical match, etc.).

#### 1.4 Modal Realism at Work: Content (pp. 27–49)

Possible worlds provide the basis for analysing **mental content** and **propositions**. A proposition = a set of possible worlds (the worlds where it is true). Belief, desire, and other propositional attitudes are relations to sets of worlds. This gives a unified treatment of intentionality without primitive intentional relations.

#### 1.5 Modal Realism at Work: Properties (pp. 50–68)

A **property** = a set of possible individuals (all actual and merely possible individuals that have the property). This treatment avoids treating properties as primitive universals or sui generis abstract objects. Properties are abundant: for every set of possibilia, there is a corresponding property. This plenitude is useful: it ensures that any meaningful predicate picks out some property.

#### 1.6 Isolation (pp. 69–80)

Worlds are **spatiotemporally isolated**: there are no spatiotemporal relations at all between things belonging to different worlds. Nor does anything at one world cause anything at another. This is what makes worlds genuinely distinct — they do not overlap spatiotemporally. Lewis defends this against island-universe objections.

#### 1.7 Concreteness (pp. 81–85)

Other worlds are **not abstract**. Lewis insists that possible worlds are things of the same ontological kind as the actual world — big physical objects (or whatever the actual world is). The difference between the actual world and other worlds is not a difference in abstractness or manner of existence but a difference in location or, rather, the absence of any locational relation at all.

#### 1.8 Plenitude (pp. 86–91)

**Every way a world could possibly be is a way that some world is.** This is not an additional axiom but a consequence of the modal realist analysis: if "possibly P" means "there is a world where P," then for every genuine possibility there must be a world. Lewis also requires plenitude for **parts** of worlds: every way a part of a world could be is a way that some part of some world is.

Alien properties and alien individuals — those not instantiated in the actual world — are accounted for: there are worlds containing them.

#### 1.9 Actuality (pp. 92–96)

**"Actual" is an indexical.** Like "here" or "now," it picks out the world the speaker inhabits. Every world is actual from the standpoint of its inhabitants. There is no world with privileged metaphysical actuality. This dissolves the apparent asymmetry between the actual and the merely possible — it is a perspectival difference, not an ontological one.

### Chapter 2 — Paradox in Paradise? (pp. 97–135)

Lewis replies to the main objections to modal realism:

#### 2.1 Everything is Actual? (pp. 97–100)
**Objection**: if all worlds are equally real, everything is actual. **Reply**: "actual" is indexical. Saying other-worldly things are real does not make them actual from our standpoint. Restricting quantification to the actual world is legitimate and common.

#### 2.2 All Worlds in One? (pp. 101–103)
**Objection**: modal realism collapses all worlds into one big world. **Reply**: worlds are individuated by the absence of any spatiotemporal relations between them. They are not parts of one inclusive world.

#### 2.3 More Worlds Than There Are? (pp. 104–107)
**Objection**: Cantorian paradoxes arise from quantifying over all worlds. **Reply**: Lewis is cautious; the plurality of worlds may not form a set. He is not committed to there being a set of all worlds.

#### 2.4 How Can We Know? (pp. 108–114)
**Objection**: worlds are causally isolated — modal knowledge is impossible. **Reply**: modal knowledge is like mathematical knowledge. We do not need causal access to know mathematical truths; similarly, we do not need causal access to possible worlds to have modal knowledge (a priori reflection suffices).

#### 2.5 A Road to Scepticism? (pp. 115–122)
**Objection**: modal realism generates sceptical scenarios (we might be in a deceptive world). **Reply**: Lewis rejects the premises needed to generate the sceptical argument within his framework.

#### 2.6 A Road to Indifference? (pp. 123–127)
**Objection**: if our counterparts suffer in other worlds regardless of what we do, morality becomes pointless. **Reply**: we are not identical to our counterparts. Our actions affect us and those in our world; what happens in other worlds is no concern of ours.

#### 2.7 Arbitrariness Lost? (pp. 128–132)
**Objection**: modal realism removes the brute contingency of our world — everything that could happen does. **Reply**: our world retains its particularity. The fact that other worlds have different laws does not make our laws any less ours, or any less contingent.

#### 2.8 The Incredulous Stare (pp. 133–135)
The most serious objection: modal realism is simply too ontologically extravagant. Lewis takes this seriously — it is a real cost. But he argues that the systematic theoretical benefits outweigh it, just as the theoretical benefits of set theory outweigh the ontological cost of an infinite hierarchy of sets.

### Chapter 3 — Paradise on the Cheap? (pp. 136–191)

A sustained critique of **Ersatz Modal Realism**: attempts to gain the benefits of possible-worlds semantics while replacing Lewis's concrete worlds with cheaper abstract entities.

#### 3.1 The Ersatzist Programme (pp. 136–141)
The motivation for ersatzism: preserve theoretical benefits while avoiding ontological extravagance. Lewis's counter-motivation: ersatzist programmes do not achieve genuine reduction of modal notions — they smuggle in primitive modality.

#### 3.2 Linguistic Ersatzism (pp. 142–164)
Worlds = maximal consistent sets of sentences (or propositions). **Objection**: "consistent" must be cashed out modally, or in terms of a model-theoretic notion that presupposes possible structures. Either way, primitive modality is not eliminated. Also, alien properties — properties not expressible in any actual language — cannot be represented linguistically.

#### 3.3 Pictorial Ersatzism (pp. 165–173)
Worlds = abstract structures that picture possible arrangements (cf. Armstrong's combinatorialism). **Objection**: the representation relation between abstract pictures and possibilities is unexplained. Alien properties — those not present in actuality — cannot be combined into ersatz worlds, because there is nothing to combine.

#### 3.4 Magical Ersatzism (pp. 174–191)
Worlds = abstract objects that simply "select" or "represent" ways things could be, by brute primitive selection. **Objection**: representation is an unexplained, "magical" primitive relation. No account is given of how an abstract object makes it true that things are a certain way. This is primitive modality under a new name.

Lewis's overarching verdict: the ersatzist must either reduce modal talk (in which case he needs something like modal realism) or take some modal notion as primitive (in which case he has not achieved the reduction he promised).

---

### Chapter 4 — Counterparts or Double Lives? (pp. 192–263)

The problem of **trans-world identity**: how do modal claims about individuals work?

#### 4.1 Good Questions and Bad (pp. 192–197)
Lewis separates genuine questions from confused ones. The genuine question is: what is the correct account of de re modality? The bad question is: "How does Humphrey exist in other worlds?" — because he doesn't.

#### 4.2 Against Overlap (pp. 198–209)
An individual cannot be a part of more than one world. If Humphrey were part of two worlds — one where he is bent, one where he is straight — he would be both bent and straight. Lewis rejects relativising properties to worlds as unacceptably complex and unmotivated. **Counterpart theory** is the solution: Humphrey has counterparts in other worlds, not a trans-world presence.

#### 4.3 Against Trans-World Individuals (pp. 210–219)
Against "trans-world individuals" — gerrymandered sums of parts from different worlds. Such entities would violate the isolation of worlds and create paradoxes about properties.

#### 4.4 Against Haecceitism (pp. 220–247)
**Haecceitism**: two worlds can differ in which individuals play which qualitative roles with no qualitative difference between the worlds — purely a difference of "thisness" (*haecceity*). Lewis argues this is metaphysically idle: there is no explanation of how purely qualitative duplicates can differ in which individual is which. The only respectable difference between worlds is qualitative difference.

#### 4.5 Against Constancy (pp. 248–263)
Against the view that an individual has the same intrinsic properties in every world it inhabits. If individuals exist in only one world (Lewis's view), constancy doesn't arise. If they exist in many (the rival view), constancy is either trivially true or false — neither result is satisfying.

## Key Arguments

1. **The Utility Argument** (Ch. 1): Modal realism is fruitful — it provides the best unified analysis of modality, counterfactuals, properties, propositions, causation, and mental content. Fruitfulness gives us good (not conclusive) reason to believe it true.

2. **The Indexicality of Actuality** (§1.9): "Actual" is an indexical — it picks out whichever world the speaker inhabits. This blocks the objection that conceding reality to other worlds makes everything actual.

3. **The Supervenience Argument** (§1.2): Supervenience theses require cross-world quantification. The sentential modal diamond cannot correctly formulate broad supervenience. Modal realism provides the correct formulation directly.

4. **The Anti-Ersatzist Argument** (Ch. 3): Every ersatzist programme either presupposes primitive modality (and thus achieves no reduction) or cannot represent alien possibilities. Modal realism achieves genuine reduction by analysing modal idioms as quantification over a domain of concrete entities.

5. **The Counterpart Argument** (Ch. 4): De re modal claims are best analysed via counterpart theory. Counterpart relations are context-sensitive similarity relations. This avoids trans-world identity, overlap, and haecceitism.

## Key Concepts and Definitions

| Term | Lewis's Definition |
|------|--------------------|
| **Possible world** | A maximal spatiotemporal system — a concrete universe, isolated from all others |
| **Actuality** | Indexical: the world the speaker inhabits; no world has privileged actuality |
| **Plenitude** | Every way a world could possibly be is a way that some world is |
| **Isolation** | No spatiotemporal or causal relations between things in different worlds |
| **Counterpart** | An individual at another world that resembles a given individual in contextually relevant respects |
| **Ersatzism** | Any programme replacing concrete worlds with abstract representations (linguistic, pictorial, or "magical") |
| **Haecceitism** | The view that worlds can differ in purely non-qualitative ways (which individual is which) — Lewis rejects this |
| **Humean Supervenience** | All facts supervene on the spatiotemporal distribution of local, intrinsic, qualitative properties |
| **De re modality** | Modal claims about what a particular individual could or must be or do |
| **Supervenience** | There could be no difference of sort A without a difference of sort B |

## Critical Reception and Significance

*On the Plurality of Worlds* is widely regarded as one of the most rigorous works of systematic metaphysics of the twentieth century. Its influence extends beyond those who accept Modal Realism:

- The **possible-worlds framework** is now standard across philosophy of language, logic, and metaphysics — even among philosophers who reject concreteness.
- **Counterpart theory** has been refined and extended by Allen Hazen, Michael Fara, Ted Sider, and others.
- Lewis's critique of **ersatzism** set the terms of debate about the nature of possible worlds for decades.
- **Humean Supervenience** remains a central reference point in debates about laws of nature, causation, and the mind.
- The **incredulous stare** became a standard dialectical move — and Lewis's response to it (theoretical utility over intuitive parsimony) became a model for how to handle radical ontological proposals.

Most philosophers reject Modal Realism. Most continue to work within a framework shaped by it.
