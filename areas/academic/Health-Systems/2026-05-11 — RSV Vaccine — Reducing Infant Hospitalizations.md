# RSV Vaccine — Reducing Infant Hospitalizations (2023)

**Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)** is the leading cause of lower respiratory tract infection in infants and young children worldwide. Before 2023, no approved vaccine existed to protect young infants. A convergence of approvals in 2023 changed this.

---

## The Interventions

Three distinct RSV prevention strategies reached approval in 2023:

### 1. Maternal Vaccine — Abrysvo (Pfizer)
Administered during weeks 24–36 of pregnancy. The mother's immune response generates antibodies that cross the placenta, providing passive immunity to the newborn from birth through the first 6 months of life — exactly the highest-risk window.

### 2. Monoclonal Antibody — Nirsevimab (Beyfortus, AstraZeneca/Sanofi)
A long-acting monoclonal antibody (not technically a vaccine) given to infants as a single injection at birth or at the start of RSV season. It directly provides pre-formed RSV-neutralizing antibodies, bypassing the need for an immune response. Half-life is engineered for ~5 months of protection. The CDC recommended it for all infants under 8 months entering their first RSV season.

### 3. Adult Vaccines — Abrysvo and Arexvy (GSK)
Approved for adults 60+, protecting older adults who are also at elevated RSV risk.

---

## Efficacy

- Maternal vaccination (Abrysvo): **~57% reduction in severe RSV lower respiratory tract illness** in infants through 6 months in trials; real-world uptake studies show higher effectiveness in adherent populations
- Nirsevimab: **~75–80% reduction in RSV hospitalizations** in infants in trial data; some real-world studies (Spain 2023–24 season) showed **~80–90% reduction** in severe disease and ICU admissions

These numbers represent among the most effective public health interventions deployed for infant respiratory disease.

---

## Why This Was Hard

RSV vaccine development had failed for 60 years after a 1966 formalin-inactivated RSV vaccine trial caused **vaccine-enhanced disease** — vaccinated infants who later contracted RSV developed more severe illness than unvaccinated children, and two died. This disaster halted RSV vaccine development for decades and imposed extreme regulatory caution.

The breakthrough came from structural biology: researchers at the NIH (Jason McLellan's group, then at Dartmouth/UT Austin) solved the structure of the RSV fusion protein in its **prefusion conformation** — the shape it adopts before it attaches to host cells, which exposes the most potent neutralizing epitopes. Prior vaccines had used the postfusion form, which is less immunogenic. **Stabilizing the prefusion conformation** was the key insight that unlocked efficacious RSV vaccines after six decades of failure.

---

## Public Health Impact

RSV causes approximately:
- 100,000–500,000 infant deaths annually worldwide (mostly in low/middle-income countries)
- 58,000–80,000 hospitalizations per year in US children under 5
- 177,000 hospitalizations in US adults 65+

Even modest penetration of these interventions represents tens of thousands of prevented hospitalizations in the first seasons of use. The 2023–24 RSV season in countries with high nirsevimab uptake showed measurable population-level reductions in infant hospitalization rates.

---

## Access Gap

As with Casgevy, the access problem is acute. Nirsevimab costs ~$495/dose in the US. Low-income countries where infant RSV mortality is highest have limited access. Gavi (the Vaccine Alliance) has begun negotiations for tiered pricing, but rollout in high-burden settings lags years behind high-income countries.
