# CRISPR Gene Therapy Approved — Casgevy (2023)

In November 2023, the FDA approved **Casgevy** (exagamglogene autotemcel, exa-cel) — the first CRISPR-based therapy approved for human use. It was developed by Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics for the treatment of **sickle cell disease (SCD)** and **transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia (TDT)**.

---

## What It Does

Casgevy works by editing the patient's own hematopoietic stem cells ex vivo:

1. Stem cells are harvested from the patient's bone marrow
2. CRISPR-Cas9 edits a specific site in the **BCL11A enhancer** — a regulatory region that suppresses fetal hemoglobin (HbF) production after birth
3. Disabling this suppressor reactivates fetal hemoglobin production
4. Edited cells are reinfused; they repopulate the bone marrow and produce functional hemoglobin

The logic is elegant: rather than correcting the defective adult hemoglobin gene directly, Casgevy bypasses it entirely by turning on a dormant developmental gene. Fetal hemoglobin does not sickle and can carry oxygen normally.

---

## Clinical Results

- In the Phase 3 SCD trial: **93.5% of patients** had no severe vaso-occlusive crises for at least 12 months post-treatment
- In the beta-thalassemia trial: **89% of patients** achieved transfusion independence
- These outcomes are durably maintained through follow-up periods extending beyond 2 years

---

## Significance

**First approved CRISPR therapy**: proof that CRISPR editing is safe and effective enough for regulatory approval in humans — a milestone that was speculative a decade earlier

**Ex vivo editing model**: avoids the challenges of in vivo delivery (targeting the correct cells in a living body); the edited cell approach is the current gold standard for safety

**Disease selection was strategic**: SCD and beta-thalassemia involve a single well-characterized gene with a clear therapeutic target, a population of stem cells that can be harvested and reinfused, and decades of prior bone marrow transplant precedent that established safety parameters

**Cost and access**: the therapy is priced at ~$2.2 million per patient, raising acute questions about equitable access — SCD disproportionately affects people of African, South Asian, and Mediterranean descent
