# Limb Regeneration Gene Set — Shared Across Axolotl, Zebrafish, and Mouse (2026)

Research published in 2026 identified a **conserved core gene set** underlying limb and appendage regeneration in axolotls, zebrafish, and mice — revealing that the molecular machinery for regeneration is not unique to specialist regenerators but is **broadly present across vertebrates**, including mammals.

---

## Background

Vertebrates vary dramatically in regenerative capacity:

- **Axolotls** (salamanders): can regenerate entire limbs, including bone, muscle, vasculature, and nerves, with perfect fidelity
- **Zebrafish**: regenerate fins, heart tissue, and spinal cord segments
- **Mice**: do not regenerate limbs; wounds heal by scarring. However, very young mice (neonates) can regenerate heart tissue and digit tips — a capacity lost in adults
- **Humans**: essentially no limb regeneration; partial digit tip regeneration in young children has been reported

The classical explanation: regeneration-capable species have special genes. The 2026 finding complicates this — the genes are not special to regenerators.

---

## The Finding

Using **comparative single-cell transcriptomics** across regenerating tissues in all three species (at multiple time points post-amputation), researchers identified a **shared regeneration gene module** — a co-expressed set of genes that activates at the wound site in all three species, even in mice where regeneration ultimately fails.

Key components of the shared module include:
- Genes encoding **blastema formation factors** — dedifferentiation signals that convert mature cells back toward a progenitor state
- **Axon guidance molecules** (nerve supply is required for regeneration in all three species)
- **Wnt and BMP pathway components** — pattern-forming signals that specify positional identity during regrowth
- **Immune modulators** — anti-inflammatory signals that suppress scar-promoting macrophage phenotypes

In mice, this module activates transiently after amputation — then **shuts down**. In axolotls, it is sustained. The failure of mammalian regeneration appears to be a **regulatory suppression**, not an absence of the underlying machinery.

---

## Implications

**Regeneration is ancestral**: the common ancestor of all vertebrates likely had significant regenerative capacity. Mammals did not lose the genes — they lost the regulatory permissiveness.

**The therapeutic target shifts**: instead of delivering regeneration genes (which are already present), the approach should be to **release the suppression** — identify what shuts down the module in mammals and inhibit that brake.

**Candidate suppressors**: the 2026 work identified several transcription factors and signaling pathways that are active during the shutdown phase in mice but not in axolotls — these are now priority targets for regenerative medicine research.
