# Quechua

Quechua (also *Runasimi*, "language of the people") is a family of closely related languages spoken across the Andean region of South America — primarily in Peru, Bolivia, and Ecuador, with communities in Argentina and Colombia. With an estimated 8–10 million speakers it is the most widely spoken indigenous language family in the Americas. It is not Altaic; Quechua is an independent language family with no established genetic relationship to other families, though it has been in long contact with Aymara. The language is agglutinative and verb-final (SOV), with highly productive suffixation.

## Key Facts

- **Evidential system — the classic three-way distinction**: Quechua is one of the most-studied evidential languages in the world, cited in virtually every typological survey. The system uses three clitics or suffixes (forms vary by dialect):
  - **-mi / -m** (direct evidence): the speaker personally witnessed the event. "Paymi hamurqa" — "He/she came" (I saw it).
  - **-si / -s** (reportative): the speaker was told by someone else. "Paysi hamurqa" — "He/she came" (I was told / they say).
  - **-chá** (inferential/conjectural): the speaker infers or guesses. "Paychá hamurqa" — "He/she must have come" (I infer from evidence).
  These clitics are sentence-level and attach to the focused element, not to the verb alone — making them different in structure from, say, Turkish verb suffixes.
- **Dialect variation**: Quechua is a dialect continuum, not a single language. Major branches include Quechua I (central Peru highlands) and Quechua II (which includes Cuzco Quechua, the prestige variety associated with the Inca, and Ecuadorian Kichwa). The evidential system is most robustly documented in Cuzco Quechua (Southern Quechua).
- **Contact phenomena**: Quechua has been in intense contact with Spanish for five centuries, producing significant lexical borrowing from Spanish. In some urban varieties, the evidential system is eroding under Spanish influence — a loss of obligatory grammatical marking in favor of lexical strategies, mirroring how English handles the same distinctions.

## Role in Typological Literature

Quechua is Aikhenvald's (2004) primary reference point for a three-term direct/inferential/reportative system and appears throughout the WALS chapter on evidentiality. Its documentation dates to early colonial grammars, making it one of the longest-attested evidential languages.
