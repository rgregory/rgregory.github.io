# Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptors (nAChRs)

## What They Are

Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors are **ligand-gated ion channels** — they open when a chemical binds, not when the membrane voltage changes. The endogenous ligand is acetylcholine (ACh); the receptor is called "nicotinic" because nicotine (an exogenous agonist from tobacco) also activates it. This distinguishes nAChRs from **muscarinic** acetylcholine receptors, which respond instead to muscarine (a compound from *Amanita muscaria*) and operate through G-protein-coupled signaling rather than direct ion gating.

Structurally, nAChRs are **pentamers**: five subunits arranged symmetrically around a central ion pore. When ACh binds (two binding sites, one at each α-subunit interface), the pore opens and allows Na⁺ and Ca²⁺ to flow inward, depolarizing the cell. The net effect is **excitatory**. The receptor family belongs to the **Cys-loop superfamily** — so named for a conserved disulfide bridge loop shared by all members — which also includes GABA-A receptors (inhibitory, Cl⁻ channel), glycine receptors (inhibitory), and 5-HT3 receptors (excitatory). This structural kinship with GABA-A receptors is pharmacologically significant: compounds that modulate one family member sometimes have off-target effects on others.

## Subunit Diversity

Mammals express **17 nAChR subunit genes**: α1–α10, β1–β4, γ, δ, and ε. Different subunit combinations produce receptor subtypes with distinct pharmacology, kinetics, and anatomical distribution. The main subtypes are:

**Muscle type — (α1)₂β1δε**
Located at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ). Mediates all voluntary skeletal muscle contraction. α1 is the subunit targeted by curare and by autoantibodies in myasthenia gravis.

**α4β2** (neuronal)
The most abundant nAChR in the brain. High-affinity binding site for nicotine — chronic nicotine exposure upregulates these receptors, a hallmark of addiction. Densely expressed in prefrontal cortex, thalamus, and striatum. Primary substrate for nicotine's effects on attention, working memory, and reward.

**α7** (neuronal)
Homomeric — all five subunits are α7. Unusually high Ca²⁺ permeability (higher than most other nAChRs), which makes it a potent activator of intracellular Ca²⁺-dependent signaling. Undergoes rapid desensitization with sustained agonist exposure. Critical for hippocampal synaptic plasticity, sensory gating, and the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway. Expressed on neurons and also on macrophages and microglia.

**α3β4** (neuronal)
Dominant subtype of autonomic ganglia (both sympathetic and parasympathetic). Governs peripheral autonomic function — heart rate modulation, glandular secretion, gut motility.

**α6β2** (neuronal)
Concentrated on dopaminergic neurons of the ventral tegmental area (VTA) and substantia nigra. Nicotine's activation of these receptors triggers dopamine release into the nucleus accumbens — the primary mechanism of nicotine reward and reinforcement.

## Functional Significance

### Neuromuscular Junction
Every voluntary movement requires NMJ nAChRs. Motor neurons release ACh; NMJ receptors depolarize the muscle end-plate, triggering contraction. Blocking these receptors with curare (tubocurarine) produces flaccid paralysis — which is why curare-derived compounds are used as surgical muscle relaxants. Myasthenia gravis is an autoimmune disease in which antibodies target muscle nAChRs (primarily the α1 subunit), reducing functional receptor density and producing progressive, fatigable muscle weakness.

### Cognition and Attention
The cholinergic system is a major regulator of cortical arousal, attention, and memory encoding. α4β2 receptors in the prefrontal cortex modulate the signal-to-noise ratio of cortical processing — essentially gating which sensory inputs get through. α7 receptors in the hippocampus are required for long-term potentiation (LTP) at certain synapses, linking them to memory consolidation. Loss of basal forebrain cholinergic neurons (nucleus basalis of Meynert) is a defining feature of Alzheimer's disease; this is why acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) are first-line pharmacotherapy — they increase synaptic ACh, partially compensating for receptor loss.

### Nicotine Addiction
Nicotine is a partial agonist at α4β2. It binds with high affinity, activates the receptor, but also drives rapid desensitization (the channel closes and becomes transiently unresponsive despite ligand still bound). With chronic exposure, cells upregulate nAChR expression to compensate — this is the molecular basis of tolerance. The "desensitization paradox" is that chronic smokers report enhanced cognition and mood regulation even though their receptors are largely in the desensitized state during smoking: the benefit appears to come from the *inter-cigarette interval*, when desensitized receptors recover and become briefly hypersensitive to endogenous ACh.

### Reward Circuitry
α6β2 nAChRs on VTA dopamine neurons are the proximate site of nicotine's rewarding effects. Nicotine → α6β2 activation → increased firing of DA neurons → dopamine release in nucleus accumbens → subjective reward and reinforcement. This is the same mesolimbic pathway engaged by other addictive drugs, though nicotine's mechanism of entry is specific to nAChRs.

### Cholinergic Anti-Inflammatory Pathway
α7 nAChRs expressed on macrophages and microglia are the terminal effector of the **cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex**. The vagus nerve, when stimulated by inflammatory signals, releases ACh in the spleen; ACh binds α7 on splenic macrophages and suppresses the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6). This is a fast, neural route for controlling systemic inflammation. Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS) exploits this pathway and is in clinical trials for rheumatoid arthritis and Crohn's disease.

### Embryonic Development
nAChRs guide axon pathfinding and synapse stabilization during fetal neurodevelopment. The cholinergic system is active in the developing brain before full circuit maturation. Prenatal nicotine exposure disrupts these developmental functions, contributing to increased risk of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS), altered autonomic regulation, and lasting cognitive deficits in exposed offspring.

## Pharmacology

**Agonists**
- Acetylcholine — endogenous full agonist
- Nicotine — partial agonist, high affinity at α4β2; low selectivity
- Varenicline (Chantix/Champix) — partial agonist at α4β2; first-line pharmacotherapy for smoking cessation; reduces withdrawal while producing submaximal reward
- Cytisine — naturally occurring partial agonist (from *Laburnum anagyroides* seeds); the oldest pharmacological smoking cessation aid; structural template for varenicline

**Antagonists**
- Tubocurarine (curare) — competitive antagonist at muscle-type nAChRs; surgical muscle relaxant
- Mecamylamine — non-competitive antagonist; crosses the blood-brain barrier; historically used for hypertension; research tool for neuronal nAChRs
- α-Bungarotoxin — peptide toxin from *Bungarus multicinctus* (banded krait) snake venom; irreversibly blocks muscle-type nAChRs and α7; primary tool for identifying and isolating these receptor subtypes in research

**Positive Allosteric Modulators (PAMs)**
- Galantamine — dual-action: weak AChE inhibitor plus positive allosteric modulator of nAChRs; used clinically for Alzheimer's disease
- PNU-120596 — potent α7-selective PAM; prevents α7 desensitization; research tool for studying α7 function; investigated as cognitive enhancer

**Desensitization Dynamics**
Sustained agonist exposure drives nAChRs into a high-affinity, channel-closed desensitized state. This is a defining feature of nAChR pharmacology and distinguishes them from many other ligand-gated channels. Recovery from desensitization is time-dependent and subtype-specific — α7 desensitizes fastest, α4β2 recovers more slowly. The pharmacological consequence is that simple agonism is not sufficient for sustained efficacy; PAMs (which prevent or reverse desensitization) are of major therapeutic interest.

The structural homology is not incidental: both receptor types share the pentameric architecture, the extracellular ligand-binding domain geometry, and the Cys-loop disulfide motif. Their pharmacological contrast (inhibitory vs. excitatory) maps onto GABA-A's Cl⁻ selectivity vs. nAChR's Na⁺/Ca²⁺ selectivity. The "nicotinic" name itself comes from the parallel with muscarinic receptors, named for the muscarine in *Amanita muscaria*.

The Alzheimer's disease angle links both notes — cholinergic neuron loss is the pathological substrate that both NGF supplementation and AChE inhibition attempt to address.
