# RSV Vaccine — 80–90% Reduction in Infant Hospitalization (2023)

In 2023, two significant interventions against **respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)** were approved, together achieving an 80–90% reduction in severe infant RSV disease and hospitalization:

1. **Nirsevimab (Beyfortus)** — a long-acting monoclonal antibody for infants (not technically a vaccine, but functions prophylactically like one)
2. **Abrysvo** (Pfizer) — a maternal vaccine given during pregnancy; maternal antibodies cross the placenta and protect the newborn

RSV is the **leading cause of infant hospitalization** in the United States and globally, responsible for ~2.1 million outpatient visits and 58,000–80,000 hospitalizations in children under 5 annually in the US alone.

---

## Why RSV Was So Hard to Prevent

RSV has resisted vaccine development for decades. A catastrophic failure in the 1960s — a formalin-inactivated RSV vaccine that caused **enhanced respiratory disease (ERD)** and led to two infant deaths — created a lasting shadow over RSV vaccine research. The failure was attributed to a skewed immune response (Th2-biased, not Th1) that produced non-neutralizing antibodies and immune complex deposition in lung tissue.

The 2023 breakthroughs depended on a key structural insight: RSV's **fusion protein (F protein)** exists in two conformations — a **prefusion form** (stable, highly immunogenic) and a **postfusion form** (less immunogenic). Earlier vaccines used the postfusion form. The modern approaches stabilize the prefusion conformation using engineered disulfide bonds, eliciting antibodies that neutralize the virus before it enters cells.

---

## The Nirsevimab (Beyfortus) Approach

Nirsevimab is a monoclonal antibody — not a traditional vaccine. It provides **passive immunity** by delivering pre-formed antibodies directly to the infant:

- Single intramuscular injection at birth or entry into RSV season
- Half-life extended by Fc engineering to last through a full RSV season (~5 months)
- Phase 3 trial (MELODY): **74.5% efficacy** against RSV-associated lower respiratory tract infection (LRTI) leading to medical attention; **79.5% efficacy** against hospitalization in first RSV season
- In high-risk infants: efficacy exceeded 90%

---

## The Maternal Vaccine (Abrysvo) Approach

Abrysvo is given to pregnant women at 32–36 weeks gestation:

- Maternal antibodies cross the placenta, protecting the newborn from birth
- Phase 3 trial: **81.8% efficacy** against severe RSV-LRTI in infants through 90 days of age
- The protection window aligns with the highest-risk period (first 3 months)
