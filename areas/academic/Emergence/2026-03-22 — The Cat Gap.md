# The Cat Gap

The cat gap is a ~6-million-year window in the North American fossil record — approximately 25 to 18.5 million years ago, spanning the Oligocene-Miocene boundary — during which no cat-like carnivores appear at all.

## What Came Before

North America had *nimravids* — so-called "false sabre-toothed cats." They resembled modern cats anatomically and occupied the same apex predator niche, but they were a separate evolutionary lineage entirely. They disappear from the fossil record around 26 million years ago.

## The Gap

For roughly 6 million years, the ecological niche occupied by cat-like ambush hunters is essentially empty in North America. Other carnivores were present — early dogs, bear-dogs — but the specific role of the solitary ambush predator appears to have gone largely unfilled. This is not a gap in the fossil record due to preservation bias; it is a genuine ecological vacancy sustained across geologically significant time.

## What Came After

True felids — the lineage containing all modern cats — migrated into North America from Eurasia via the Bering land bridge around 18.5 million years ago and rapidly diversified to fill the vacant niche.

## Why It Matters

- **Ecological vacancy is real and persistent.** A niche can go unfilled for millions of years. The assumption that evolution continuously optimizes to fill all available niches does not hold.
- **Niche-filling does not require relatedness.** The animals that eventually fill a vacated niche need not be closely related to their predecessors. Nimravids and true felids are not closely related; they converged independently on similar forms.
- **Ecosystems do not restore to prior configurations.** After disruption — here, likely driven by climate cooling and habitat restructuring — complex systems may reach new equilibria rather than snapping back.
- **Cause is debated.** Climate cooling and drying during this period likely restructured habitats and prey communities, but the precise mechanism is unresolved.
