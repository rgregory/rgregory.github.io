# George Orwell — Writer and Political Thinker

Eric Arthur Blair (1903–1950), who wrote as George Orwell, occupies a unique position in twentieth-century intellectual history: he was a novelist whose political thinking was indistinguishable from his literary practice, and a political thinker whose ideas require literary form to be fully articulated. He resists easy classification. He was a democratic socialist who despised the British Left's romance with Stalinist Russia. He was an anti-imperialist who served the British Empire as a colonial police officer in Burma. These contradictions were not hypocrisies — they were the raw material of his thought.

## Formation: Burma, Spain, and the Road

The biographical arc matters. Orwell's time in the Imperial Police in Burma (1922–1927) gave him firsthand experience of how power infantilizes both the oppressor and the oppressed — the subject of his essay "Shooting an Elephant" and his novel *Burmese Days*. His years of voluntary poverty in Paris and London (*Down and Out in Paris and London*, 1933) gave him the body knowledge of what economic precarity actually feels like, not what it looks like from a distance.

The decisive event was the Spanish Civil War. Orwell went to Spain in 1936 to fight against Franco, joining the POUM (Partido Obrero de Unificación Marxista), a dissident Marxist militia. What he witnessed was not just the war against fascism, but the Stalinist Communist Party's systematic destruction of the independent left — show trials, disappearances, the murder of anarchists and Trotskyists by their nominal allies. He recorded this in *Homage to Catalonia* (1938), one of the most important political documents of the century and almost entirely ignored on publication.

Spain taught him the central lesson: **the greatest threat to socialism was not capitalism but the authoritarian version of socialism itself**. The betrayal of the revolution by those who claimed to be leading it became the animating concern of his two great fables.

## "Politics and the English Language" (1946)

This essay is arguably Orwell's most direct contribution to political philosophy — a theory of language as a site of power and resistance.

His argument: political language has become systematically degraded. Abstract, passive, latinate constructions allow writers to assert things without taking responsibility for them. "Pacification" describes the bombing of villages. "Transfer of population" describes ethnic cleansing. "Elimination of unreliable elements" describes murder. The passive voice and abstract nouns perform a specific function: they disappear the agent. There is no one doing anything; things merely happen.

Orwell's prescription is simultaneously aesthetic and political: use concrete nouns, active verbs, short words, images that you can actually picture. Clarity of expression is not a stylistic preference — it is a form of intellectual honesty, and intellectual honesty is a form of political resistance.

This argument runs through all his work. Squealer in *Animal Farm* does not lie in the crude sense — he *obfuscates*, adjusting the language of the commandments so gradually that the animals lose the ability to know whether things have changed. In *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, Newspeak is engineered to make thoughtcrime literally unthinkable by eliminating the vocabulary in which dissenting thoughts could be formed.

Compare: [[2026-04-04 — Michel Foucault — Philosopher]] theorizes discourse as the medium through which power constitutes truth and subjectivity. Orwell's analysis is less systematic but more urgent: he names specific abuses and identifies practical countermeasures. Where Foucault asks "how does power work through language?", Orwell asks "how do we resist it?"

Also compare: [[2026-03-21 — Chomsky on Systems of Power]], which develops a structural "propaganda model" of how mass media manufactures consent — a more institutional analysis that complements Orwell's focus on individual linguistic choices.

## Democratic Socialism and Anti-Totalitarianism

Orwell never abandoned socialism, but he defined it in a way that put him at odds with most of the British Left. For him, socialism meant economic equality, political liberty, and the absence of hereditary privilege — all three simultaneously. The Soviet Union failed all three tests. The mistake of his contemporaries, he argued, was that they were so horrified by capitalism that they lost the ability to criticize anything that called itself socialist, however much it resembled the thing it claimed to oppose.

His critique is captured in the famous conclusion of *Animal Farm*: the pigs have become the farmers. The revolution has not merely been betrayed; it has reproduced the original hierarchy, now with an ideological justification that makes it harder to name.

This connects to the concept of [[2026-06-24 — Totalitarianism]], which Orwell dramatizes without ever quite philosophizing. Hannah Arendt provides the philosophical scaffolding that Orwell's work demands.

## Relevance to Teaching Philosophy

Orwell is an extraordinarily effective teaching instrument for several reasons:
- He makes abstract political concepts viscerally concrete (the allegory mode)
- He demonstrates that philosophical clarity and literary form are not opposed
- He forces students to examine the relationship between language and thought
- He raises the question of whether it is possible to think outside the categories one's language provides — a classic bridge to Wittgenstein and the later philosophy of language
