# Pilobolus — Dictyocaulus, Biomimetics, and the Meaning of Purposeful

## I. Dictyocaulus — The Parasite on the Parasite's Taxi

*Dictyocaulus* is a nematode lungworm living in the bronchi and trachea of ungulates. Larvae (L3 stage) are chemotactically attracted to Pilobolus sporangiophores. They crawl up the stalk, cluster around the subsporangial vesicle, and are launched along with the sporangium — traveling up to 2.5 meters to land on grass at grazing height.

Neither organism planned this. Pilobolus evolved its ballistic system for spore dispersal. *Dictyocaulus* evolved chemotaxis toward the fungus independently. No mutualism — Pilobolus gets nothing. Pure exploitation: the parasite evolved to treat another organism's engineering as infrastructure.

Result: **two independently optimized solutions to the same dispersal problem, one hijacking the other**, with no communication or cooperation between them.

## II. Biomimetics

**Hydraulic Pressure Accumulation**
The vesicle is a biological capacitor: slow energy input (osmotic charging over hours), fast energy output (rupture in milliseconds). Applications: soft robotics, pressure-triggered drug delivery microcapsules, passive-charging micro-UAV launch.

**Phototrophic Aiming — Analog Computation**
No sensor, no processor, no motor. A curved fluid-filled lens focuses light to a point; growth geometry encodes the answer. The aim direction is a feature of the physical structure, not a computation. Applications: passive optical tracking for solar collectors, self-orienting microstructures, swarm robotics using simple physical rules.

**Whole-Payload Launch**
Container serves as both vehicle and landing adhesive — packaging that doubles as delivery mechanism. Applications: targeted drug delivery, seed dispersal drones.

## III. The Philosophy — What Does "Purposeful" Mean?

### Three Positions

**1. Eliminativism**
All teleological language about Pilobolus is a useful fiction. There is no purpose — only chemistry, osmosis, phototropic growth, hydraulics. Problem: works as metaphysics but fails as explanation. To explain *why* the sporangiophore orients toward light (not just *how*), you need to say what the orientation is *for*.

**2. Weak Teleology — Functional Explanation Without Intention**
(Aristotle, Wright, Millikan, Godfrey-Smith): Teleological language is legitimate for evolved traits because natural selection gives content to "what something is for."

Larry Wright's formulation: X is there *in order to* do Y if:
1. X does Y
2. X is there *because* it does Y (selection history)

**Purpose is real; intention is not required.** Purpose is a feature of the trait's history, not the organism's mind.

**3. Strong Teleology — Aristotelian Natural Norms**
(Foot, Thompson, Koons): Living things have natural norms built into their nature. A sporangiophore that fails to orient toward light is *defective* — not just different, but failing to be what it is. The biological kind defines a standard of proper function.

### Derived vs. Original Intentionality (Searle)

- **Original intentionality**: mental states about things intrinsically
- **Derived intentionality**: representations about things because *we* interpret them that way

Missing third category: **biological intentionality** — purpose produced by natural selection. Not original (no mind), not merely derived (not just our interpretation — the trait really was selected for this effect). **Purpose without a purposer.**

Ruth Millikan's term: **"proper function"** — the function a trait was selected for, independent of any observer.

### The Dictyocaulus Twist

The larvae exhibit goal-directed behavior — active chemotaxis, deliberate climbing. But the goal they're "pursuing" (ballistic dispersal) wasn't part of their evolutionary history with that mechanism. The behavior looks directed at a goal the organism cannot represent.

**The spectrum:**
- Pilobolus: no NS, pure physics/chemistry
- *Dictyocaulus* larvae: primitive NS, reflexive chemotaxis
- Insect: NS, fixed action patterns, no representation
- Vertebrate: NS, flexible behavior, possible representation
- Human: NS, language, explicit intentionality

At every level we want to say the organism is "doing something for a reason." The *kind* of purposiveness differs at each step.

## Teaching Applications

Pilobolus as case study for philosophy of biology:

1. **Unambiguous** — nobody thinks it has intentions; discuss functional purpose without consciousness debate
2. **Forces the how/why distinction** — mechanism vs. function, two non-reducible explanatory levels
3. **Tests intuitions** about teleological language — is it *true* that the sporangiophore *aims*? What work is "aim" doing?
4. **Connects to design arguments** — looks designed, isn't; gap between appearance of design and its absence is where Darwinian explanation does its most important philosophical work
5. **Dictyocaulus extension** — what changes when a nervous system enters? Does intentionality come in degrees? Where does "real" purpose begin — and is that the right question?
