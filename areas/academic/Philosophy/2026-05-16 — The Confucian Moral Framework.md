# The Confucian Moral Framework

Confucianism's ethical system rests on five constants (*wuchang*, 五常) that together define what it means to be a fully human moral agent. These are not independent virtues — they form an integrated hierarchy in which each constant supports and presupposes the others.

---

## The Five Constants

### Rén (仁) — Benevolence / Humaneness

The master virtue. Often translated as "benevolence," "humaneness," or "human-heartedness." *Rén* is the capacity for sympathetic fellow-feeling — the recognition that what matters for others matters morally. Confucius in the *Analects* is frustratingly indirect about its definition, suggesting that *rén* is easier to recognize in practice than to define abstractly.

> "The man of *rén*, wishing to be established himself, seeks also to establish others." (*Analects* 6.30)

*Rén* is the motivational core. The other constants give it structure and expression.

### Lǐ (禮) — Ritual Propriety

*Lǐ* covers the entire domain of correct social form: ritual conduct, ceremony, protocol, and propriety. It is how *rén* is externalized in social life. Where *rén* is internal disposition, *lǐ* is its outward practice.

The *Analects* famously state: "Restraining oneself and returning to *lǐ* — this is *rén*" (12.1). The two concepts are nearly co-constitutive: *rén* without *lǐ* is sentiment without discipline; *lǐ* without *rén* is empty formalism.

*Lǐ* encompasses:
- Family rituals (mourning, ancestral rites)
- Political ceremony (court protocol, investiture)
- Social norms governing relationships at every level

### Xiào (孝) — Filial Piety

The concrete first expression of *rén* in the family. *Xiào* is the reverence and care owed to parents and ancestors. It is the foundation of Confucian social structure because the family is the prototype of all social relationships. The son who cannot honour his parents cannot be trusted to honour the ruler; the ruler who cannot honour Heaven cannot be trusted to govern the people.

Filial piety is not mere obedience. The *Classic of Filial Piety* (*Xiaojing*) distinguishes remonstrance (correcting a parent's wrongdoing out of love) from mere compliance. Blind obedience is not *xiào*.

See also: [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — The Imperial Examination System|The Imperial Examination System]] — the examination curriculum foregrounded *xiào* as the moral foundation for civil service.

### Yì (義) — Righteousness / Moral Rightness

*Yì* is the sense of what is right — the ability to discern appropriate action independent of self-interest. Where *rén* is sympathetic motivation and *lǐ* is correct form, *yì* is the moral judgment that determines what is fitting in a given situation.

Mencius uses *yì* as a counterpart to *rén*: "Humaneness (*rén*) is the human mind; righteousness (*yì*) is the human path." (*Mencius* 6A.11). To violate *yì* for personal gain is corruption in the most fundamental sense.

### Zhì (智) — Wisdom / Moral Knowledge

The cognitive dimension: the capacity to distinguish right from wrong, to understand *lǐ*, and to know how *rén* and *yì* apply in complex situations. *Zhì* is practical wisdom, not abstract intellect. It is what allows the morally cultivated person to navigate situations where the constants appear to conflict.

## Human Nature: Mencius vs. Xunzi

The most important internal debate in early Confucianism concerns the ground of these virtues:

**Mencius (孟子, ca. 372–289 BCE)** argued that human nature (*xing*, 性) is inherently good. The four virtues — *rén, yì, lǐ, zhì* — are not imposed from outside but are natural moral sprouts (*duān*, 端) that all humans possess. Education and moral cultivation water these sprouts; evil arises from neglect, deprivation, and poor environment, not from nature.

> "The feeling of commiseration is the beginning of humaneness. The feeling of shame and dislike is the beginning of righteousness. The feeling of modesty and yielding is the beginning of ritual propriety. The feeling of approval and disapproval is the beginning of wisdom." (*Mencius* 2A.6)

**Xunzi (荀子, ca. 310–235 BCE)** disagreed sharply: human nature is bad (*xing è*, 性惡). Left uncultivated, humans pursue self-interest and desire, creating conflict. Ritual propriety (*lǐ*) and the moral standards (*yì*) that teachers and sage-kings created are precisely the *correctives* to nature — not its expression. Virtue must be constructed, not uncovered.

This is not merely an academic dispute. Xunzi's position is structurally closer to Legalism (see [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — Legalism vs Confucianism|Legalism vs. Confucianism]]) — if humans are naturally bad, you need institutions and law to contain them, not just moral exhortation. Paradoxically, Xunzi's two most famous students were Han Feizi and Li Si — the architects of Legalism.

## The Analects as Primary Source

The *Lunyu* (論語, *Analects*) is a compilation of Confucius's sayings and conversations, assembled by his students after his death (479 BCE). It is the foundational text of the Confucian tradition. Confucius does not present a systematic treatise; the *Analects* is a collection of contextual judgments, which is itself a pedagogical point — moral wisdom is practical and situational, not algorithmic.

The *Analects* belongs to the Four Books of the Confucian canon alongside the *Mencius*, the *Great Learning*, and the *Doctrine of the Mean*. See [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — The Four Books and Five Classics|The Four Books and Five Classics]].

## The Rectification of Names (Zhèngmíng, 正名)

A crucial but often overlooked element: Confucius insisted that social order depends on names accurately reflecting realities. "If names are not correct, speech will not accord with truth; if speech does not accord with truth, affairs cannot be accomplished" (*Analects* 13.3). A ruler who does not act like a ruler, a father who does not act like a father — both violate *zhèngmíng* and unravel the social fabric. This connects directly to *lǐ*: ritual propriety only functions if the roles it governs are genuinely occupied.
