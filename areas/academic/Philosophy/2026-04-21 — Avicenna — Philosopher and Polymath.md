# Avicenna — Philosopher and Polymath

Avicenna (Ibn Sina, c. 980–1037) was a Persian polymath and the most influential philosopher of the Islamic Golden Age. He constructed a comprehensive philosophical system that synthesized Aristotelianism and Neoplatonism with Islamic theology, providing the metaphysical and medical foundations for both the Islamic world and medieval European Scholasticism.

## Metaphysics: The Necessary Existent

Avicenna's most famous contribution to metaphysics is the **Proof of the Truthful** (*Burhan al-Siddiqin*), an argument for the existence of God based on the nature of being itself.

- **Contingent Beings**: Objects whose essence does not include existence (e.g., humans, trees, planets). They *could* not exist; their existence requires an external cause.
- **The Necessary Existent**: To avoid an infinite regress of causes, there must be a being whose essence *is* existence. This being is necessary in itself and is the source of all contingent existence. Avicenna identifies this being as God.

## Essence and Existence

Avicenna introduced a sharp distinction between **essence** (what a thing is) and **existence** (the fact that it is).
- In all created things, essence and existence are accidentally joined; "horseness" does not imply the existence of a horse.
- In the Necessary Existent alone, essence and existence are identical.
This distinction was later central to the work of **Thomas Aquinas** and the development of Western ontology.

## The Floating Man Thought Experiment

To prove the immateriality and self-awareness of the soul, Avicenna proposed the **Floating Man** experiment:
- Imagine a person created instantaneously in mid-air, with their sight veiled and limbs splayed so they feel no contact with their own body or the environment.
- Avicenna argues that even in this state of total sensory deprivation, the person would still be certain of their own existence.
- **Conclusion**: The "I" (self-awareness) is not dependent on the body or sensory data. This anticipates **Descartes'** *cogito* by six centuries.

## The Canon of Medicine

As a physician, Avicenna wrote ***The Canon of Medicine*** (*Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb*), a five-volume encyclopedia that systematized Greek, Persian, and Indian medical knowledge. It served as the standard medical textbook in Europe and the Islamic world until the 17th century.

## Key Works

- ***The Book of Healing (Kitab al-Shifa)*** — A massive philosophical and scientific encyclopedia covering logic, physics, mathematics, and metaphysics.
- ***The Canon of Medicine (Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb)*** — The authoritative medical text of the medieval period.
- ***Pointers and Reminders (al-Isharat wa-l-Tanbihat)*** — His final, most mature philosophical work.

## In Relation To

**[[05-People/Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz|Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz]]** — Avicenna's distinction between necessity and contingency is a direct ancestor of Leibniz's Principle of Sufficient Reason and his modal metaphysics.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-03-21 — Hume An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding|David Hume]]** — Avicenna's "Necessary Existent" is precisely the kind of metaphysical claim that Hume's "flames" would consume; where Avicenna sees a logical necessity for a First Cause, Hume sees only a habit of mind projecting a regularity it cannot prove.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-19 — A. J. Ayer — Philosopher|A. J. Ayer]]** — Ayer dismisses Avicenna's entire metaphysical project as "literally meaningless" because the existence of a "Necessary Existent" is neither analytic nor empirically verifiable.
