# Derrida's Criticism of Searle — Iterability, Intention, and the Limits of Speech Act Theory

## The Exchange

The debate unfolds across three texts:

1. **Jacques Derrida, "Signature Event Context"** (1972, published in *Marges de la philosophie*) — a paper originally delivered at a philosophy conference on communication. Derrida targets Austin's speech act theory and, more broadly, the assumption that communication transmits a self-identical meaning from sender to receiver.

2. **John Searle, "Reiterating the Differences: A Reply to Derrida"** (1977, *Glyph* 1) — a polemical response in which Searle defends Austin and accuses Derrida of misreading him. Searle's tone is dismissive; he treats Derrida's arguments as based on misunderstanding rather than genuine philosophical disagreement.

3. **Jacques Derrida, "Limited Inc a b c..."** (1977, *Glyph* 2) — Derrida's rejoinder, running to book length, in which he systematically works through Searle's reply. Eventually published as *Limited Inc* (1988) with an afterword. The title is a joke: Searle had co-authored with two colleagues, so Derrida addressed his response to "Sarl" — a French corporate abbreviation (Société à Responsabilité Limitée).

After "Limited Inc", Searle refused to engage further. He later said, in effect, that Derrida was not doing philosophy but producing something closer to literary wordplay. The exchange became one of the most cited and most divisive in late-twentieth-century philosophy.

---

## What Derrida Actually Argues: Iterability

Derrida's central concept in "Signature Event Context" is **iterability** (from Latin *iter*, again, and Sanskrit *itara*, other). The argument runs roughly as follows:

For any sign — word, sentence, written text, gesture — to function as a sign at all, it must be **repeatable**. A mark that could only occur once, in one context, for one receiver, would not be intelligible as a sign. Meaning requires a structure of repetition.

But repetition always involves alteration. Every re-use of a sign occurs in a different context: a different reader, a different time, a different surrounding text, a different institutional frame. The sign can therefore always be **cited**, **quoted**, **grafted** into new contexts, made to function in ways its original "author" did not intend and could not have anticipated.

This is not a pathological feature of language. It is a **structural condition** of signification itself. A sign that could not be separated from its originating context and intention would not be a sign at all — it would be a private event, incommunicable by definition.

The consequence: **meaning cannot be fixed by intention alone**. The author's intention is one contextual factor among others. Once a sign is produced, it enters a field of possible re-uses that outruns any intention to anchor it.

This applies with particular force to **writing**, which, as Derrida notes following Austin and Husserl, is designed to function in the absence of its author. A written text survives the death of its writer. But Derrida's point is that the same iterability structure is at work in all language — writing simply makes it more visible.

## How This Challenges Searle's Speech Act Theory

Searle's theory of speech acts (developed in *Speech Acts*, 1969, and refined through *Expression and Meaning*, 1979) holds that:

- Linguistic meaning is fundamentally a matter of **speaker's intention** — specifically, the intention to produce a certain effect in a hearer by means of the hearer's recognition of that intention (a Gricean framework Searle modified and deepened).
- Successful communication occurs when the speaker's illocutionary intent is correctly identified. The conventions of language encode and transmit these intentions reliably under normal conditions.
- **Context** is specifiable: the rules for speech acts determine what counts as a sincere assertion, a promise, a question, a directive, etc. Context is bounded, and within those bounds meaning is determinate.

Derrida's argument cuts at the root of this account. If iterability is constitutive of signification, then:

1. **No context is ever fully saturable** — there is always a remainder, always a possibility that the sign is taken up differently than intended. Searle's appeal to "context" to fix meaning assumes that context can be sealed off. But every context can itself be re-contextualized.
2. **Intention is itself a sign** and therefore subject to iterability. The speaker's intention must be expressed, recognized, interpreted — all operations that involve the same iterable structures. Intention cannot stand outside language and anchor it from a position of pure interiority.
3. **The speaker's meaning** (what Searle carefully distinguishes from sentence meaning) is not a self-transparent mental state that language merely encodes. It is produced through the same iterative-differential structure as any other sign.

Derrida is not claiming that communication fails or that meaning is impossible. He is claiming that the **condition of possibility** for communication is simultaneously the condition of its openness to drift — to misreading, repurposing, and citation in unintended contexts. These are not accidental breakdowns but structural features.

## Derrida's Reading of Austin — The Problem of "Parasitic" Utterances

Searle was defending Austin against Derrida, but Derrida argues that his reading of Austin is more faithful than Searle's.

In *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), Austin distinguishes between **serious** and **parasitic** (or "etiolated") uses of language. Serious uses are normal performatives and constatives in ordinary communicative contexts. Parasitic uses are utterances in theater, fiction, poetry, play, or quotation — cases where the speaker does not genuinely perform the act the utterance names. If an actor says "I promise" on stage, he is not really promising. Austin excludes these cases from his theory and proposes to handle them separately.

Searle reproduces this exclusion and treats it as a reasonable methodological bracketing.

Derrida's counter-argument is that the exclusion is not methodologically innocent. The "parasitic" cases — citation, quotation, theater, fiction — are possible precisely because of iterability. The actor can say "I promise" on stage because the words "I promise" are iterable — they can be detached from the sincere context and grafted into a different one. But this is the same property that allows "I promise" to function in ordinary life at all.

Austin's excluded cases do not sit safely at the margin. They reveal the **general structure** of language. The normal case depends on the same iterability that makes the parasitic case possible. The exception is not marginal; it is constitutive.

Derrida therefore concludes that Austin's theory, taken seriously and followed to its own internal implications, undermines the intention-anchored model of speech acts that both Austin and Searle are trying to construct.

## The Philosophical Stakes

The exchange raises fundamental questions:

**Does context ever fully determine meaning?**
Searle's position requires that meaning is, in principle, fully determinable given sufficient specification of context and intention. Derrida's position is that context is in principle open — not because we lack information, but because iterability makes every context re-contextualizable. This is not skepticism about meaning; it is a different ontology of meaning.

**Can intention anchor a sign?**
Searle treats speaker's intention as the fundamental determinant of linguistic meaning. Derrida does not deny that intentions exist, but argues that intentions are themselves expressed through iterable marks and cannot therefore step outside the play of iterability to fix meaning from a position of pure interiority. Intention is always already mediated.

**What is the relationship between "normal" and "deviant" language uses?**
The debate crystallizes a deeper disagreement about how to do philosophy of language. Searle follows a broadly Austinian method: start from clear, paradigm cases of ordinary language use and explain the deviant cases by derivation or analogy. Derrida inverts this: the deviant or "parasitic" case is structurally primary — it reveals what the normal case depends on.

This connects to a broader disagreement about **the relationship between system and example** in philosophy of language, and about whether idealization (bracketing citation, theater, irony) is a harmless tool or a move that systematically distorts the object of study.

## Why the Exchange Became Famous — and Infamous

The exchange is famous partly for its intellectual content and partly for its sociology.

Searle's tone in "Reiterating the Differences" is contemptuous. He accuses Derrida of misreading Austin and of pursuing a style of argument incompatible with analytic standards of clarity and rigor. He does not engage with the concept of iterability at any sustained depth.

Derrida's "Limited Inc" responds in kind — but with wit. By addressing his reply to "Sarl" and running through Searle's objections with forensic patience, Derrida makes visible that Searle has often failed to read the actual argument in "Signature Event Context." The rejoinder is simultaneously a piece of philosophy and a demonstration that the author (Searle) cannot fully control how his text is read — which is itself an enactment of the iterability thesis.

The exchange landed in the middle of the "theory wars" in American academia during the 1970s–1990s, when the divide between analytic and continental philosophy was at its widest and most institutionally charged. Searle represented analytic clarity; Derrida represented continental opacity. Each side used the exchange to confirm its priors about the other.

After "Limited Inc", Searle declined to respond further, stating that Derrida was not engaged in philosophy in any recognizable sense. This refusal itself became a data point: for Derrida's supporters, it confirmed that the analytic tradition could not engage with the argument; for Searle's supporters, it confirmed that the argument was not worth engaging.

## What Remains Genuinely Productive

Despite the polemics, the exchange has genuine philosophical yield:

1. **Iterability is a real and underexplored structural feature of language.** Whatever one thinks of Derrida's broader conclusions, the observation that signs must be repeatable and are therefore always detachable from original context is correct and important. No adequate philosophy of language can ignore it.

2. **The exclusion of "parasitic" language is a genuine problem for Austin's program.** The question of how to handle irony, citation, fiction, and play within a speech act theory has been debated extensively since (see Recanati, Bezuidenhout, Bach and Harnish). Derrida was not wrong to press on it.

3. **The relationship between intention and meaning is genuinely contested.** Gricean intention-based semantics is powerful but faces serious objections from multiple directions — from contextualism (Sperber and Wilson, Carston), from social externalism (Burge), and from Derrida's direction. The territory is not settled.

4. **The question of whether context can be "saturated"** — whether there is always a residue of openness — connects directly to contemporary debates in formal pragmatics, indexicality, and the semantics/pragmatics interface.

5. **The exchange models both the productive and unproductive dynamics of cross-tradition philosophical debate.** Its breakdown is itself philosophically instructive: what conditions allow genuine engagement across methodological divides?
