# Casgevy — First FDA-Approved CRISPR Therapy (2023)

In December 2023, the FDA approved **Casgevy** (exa-cel, developed by Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics) for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia. It is the first CRISPR-based therapeutic approved for clinical use anywhere in the world.

---

## What It Does

Casgevy uses CRISPR-Cas9 to edit a patient's own hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) ex vivo — outside the body. The edit targets the **BCL11A enhancer**, a repressor gene that normally suppresses fetal hemoglobin (HbF) production after birth. By disabling this repressor, the therapy reactivates HbF in red blood cells. Fetal hemoglobin does not sickle and can compensate functionally for defective adult hemoglobin in both conditions.

The process:
1. Stem cells are harvested from the patient's bone marrow via apheresis
2. CRISPR edits are made in culture (ex vivo)
3. Patient undergoes myeloablative conditioning (chemotherapy to clear existing bone marrow)
4. Edited cells are infused back
5. Cells engraft and produce HbF-expressing red blood cells for the patient's lifetime

---

## Clinical Results

- In the pivotal trial, **29 of 29 sickle cell patients** were free of severe vaso-occlusive crises for at least 12 months post-treatment
- **39 of 42 beta-thalassemia patients** achieved transfusion independence
- HbF levels rose to therapeutically meaningful concentrations and were sustained
- Follow-up continues — the therapy is expected to be a one-time, potentially curative intervention

---

## Significance

The approval is a landmark for several reasons:

**First in class**: Casgevy is not merely the first CRISPR therapy; it validates the entire ex vivo CRISPR editing platform as clinically viable. Every future CRISPR therapeutic benefits from the regulatory and manufacturing pathway it opens.

**Target choice**: The BCL11A enhancer strategy is elegant — rather than correcting the defective HBB gene directly (technically harder), it reactivates an alternative protein the body already knows how to make. It is a workaround that exploits developmental biology.

**Disease burden addressed**: Sickle cell disease affects approximately 100,000 Americans and millions worldwide, disproportionately in sub-Saharan Africa. Beta-thalassemia is similarly prevalent across Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and South/Southeast Asian populations.

**Cost and access**: The therapy is priced at approximately $2.2 million per patient, raising immediate equity concerns. Delivery requires specialized centers with bone marrow transplant infrastructure. Global access, especially in high-burden low-resource countries, remains an unsolved problem.

---

## What Comes Next (2024–2026)

- In vivo delivery approaches are in development — eliminating the need for ex vivo editing would dramatically simplify treatment and reduce cost
- Base editing and prime editing variants (avoiding double-strand breaks) are in early trials for hemoglobinopathies
- Casgevy's approval opened the door to a wave of CRISPR IND filings in 2024–2026 across oncology, metabolic disease, and rare genetic disorders
