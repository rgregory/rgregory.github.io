# Connections — Sima Qian, Hayek, and the Impossibility of Central Knowledge

## The Parallel

Hayek's 1945 argument: no central authority can possess the distributed, local, tacit knowledge dispersed across millions of actors. The information needed to coordinate society doesn't exist in a form that *can* be centralized.

Sima Qian's *Shiji* (~94 BCE): a 2,100-year empirical record of exactly this failure, repeated across every dynasty he documents.

## The pattern in the Shiji

Every dynasty Sima Qian records follows the same arc:

1. **Founding**: a leader with local, contextual knowledge of conditions (often a rebel, outsider, or frontier commander) overthrows a regime that has lost touch
2. **Consolidation**: the new dynasty builds administrative apparatus — the well-field system, taxation, census, corvée — to render the empire legible and controllable from the center
3. **Overreach**: the apparatus grows; the center attempts to manage what only the periphery can know (local conditions, seasonal variations, social trust networks)
4. **Collapse**: the periphery defects, revolts, or simply stops cooperating. The center's model of reality diverges fatally from actual conditions

This is not a moral fable about tyranny. It's a *structural* argument: the task is impossible at scale regardless of the ruler's virtue.

## What each illuminates about the other

**Hayek illuminates Sima Qian**: The recurring dynastic cycle is not cultural destiny or moral decline — it's an information-theoretic constraint. Hayek gives the mechanism: prices (or their absence) cannot transmit the right signals when the system is too centralized. The *Shiji* becomes evidence for a universal claim, not just Chinese history.

**Sima Qian illuminates Hayek**: Hayek's argument can seem abstract, even ideological. The *Shiji* gives it 2,600 years of concrete cases — each with named actors, specific institutions, particular failures. It also shows what Hayek underemphasizes: the *moral* dimension. Sima Qian's praise-and-blame historiography judges rulers not just for incompetence but for *hubris* — the belief that they could know what cannot be known.

## The Scott connection

James C. Scott's *Seeing Like a State* (1998) is the modern bridge: high-modernist states fail for exactly the same reason as Sima Qian's dynasties. Scott's concept of *mētis* (local, tacit, practical knowledge) is what the Chinese periphery possesses and the center cannot extract.

The *Shiji*'s five-part classification scheme (Annals, Tables, Treatises, Hereditary Houses, Biographies) is itself an act of legibility — Sima Qian *is* the state apparatus, making history visible and evaluable. But he adds what Scott says states always strip away: thick moral evaluation. The *Shiji* is legibility *with judgment*.
