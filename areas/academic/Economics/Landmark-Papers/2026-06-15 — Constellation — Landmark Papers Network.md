# Constellation — Landmark Economics Papers Network

**Purpose**: Map the intellectual relationships among 20 landmark papers (2016–2026) and show how they connect to philosophy, complexity, and social science.

---

## The Five Core Constellations

### 1. Inequality Cascade
**Central insight**: Inequality is not one mechanism but a cascade of reinforcing loops across wealth, skills, markets, and status.

**Papers**:
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Capital in the Twenty-First Century (Piketty)|Piketty]] — r > g wealth concentration (foundational)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — The Race Between Education and Technology (Goldin & Katz)|Goldin & Katz]] — education premium drives inequality via skill-tech mismatch
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Superstar Inequality (Kaplan & Rauh)|Kaplan & Rauh]] — winner-take-all rents (compensation mechanisms)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — The Sum of Small Things (Pugh)|Pugh]] — aspirational consumption cascades (status amplifies inequality)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Trade and Inequality (Grossman et al)|Grossman et al.]] — globalization winners & losers

**Philosophical grounding**: 
- Rawlsian justice (difference principle: when is inequality justified?)
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-17 — Jean-François Lyotard — The Postmodern Condition|Lyotard on grand narratives]] — the legitimacy crisis of capitalism when inequality becomes visible

**Key insight**: These five papers show inequality is overdetermined — multiple, independent mechanisms all push toward concentration. Education can't offset wealth concentration; trade displacement can't be absorbed by market adjustment. Policy must intervene at multiple points.

---

### 2. Technology-Labor Nexus
**Central insight**: Technological change is a choice, not a destiny. Policy and institutions shape whether tech complements or displaces labor.

**Papers**:
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — The Race Between Education and Technology (Goldin & Katz)|Goldin & Katz]] — long-run education-tech race (supply constraint)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Pricing of Skills and Technological Change (Acemoglu & Restrepo)|Acemoglu & Restrepo]] — short-run displacement effects (Berlitz case)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Artificial Intelligence, Automation, and Work (Acemoglu & Johnson)|Acemoglu & Johnson]] — modern AI era; automation vs. complementarity as institutional choice
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Machine Learning Methods (Mullainathan & Spiess)|Mullainathan & Spiess]] — technical capabilities vs. social wisdom (prediction ≠ causation)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Monopsony Power in Higher Education (Laband & Lentz)|Laband & Lentz]] — institutional labor market power shapes wage outcomes

**Philosophical grounding**:
- [[Philosophy of Technology]] — determinism vs. agency: are we building tech that enslaves or augments?
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-17 — Jürgen Habermas — Philosopher|Habermas on instrumental vs. communicative rationality]] — what's lost when technology becomes purely productive?
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-18 — Statistics as Philosophy|Statistics as Philosophy]] — can we causally infer the right policy from observational data?

**Key insight**: Acemoglu & Johnson's central claim reframes the entire discussion: **technology adoption is not determined by comparative advantage but by deliberate institutional design**. Education, innovation policy, and labor regulation all shape which technologies get adopted.

### 3. Institutions & Development
**Central insight**: Long-run divergence in prosperity is driven by institutional choices (inclusive vs. extractive), not geography or culture.

**Papers**:
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Why Nations Fail (Acemoglu & Robinson)|Acemoglu & Robinson]] — institutional foundations (foundational)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Inclusive Growth and Development (Banerjee et al)|Banerjee et al.]] — inclusive institutions + distributional concerns
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Air Pollution and Mortality in China (Chen)|Chen et al.]] — environmental regulation as institutional choice
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Measuring Well-Being and Progress (Stiglitz, Sen, Fitoussi)|Stiglitz, Sen, Fitoussi]] — institutional quality enables well-being, not just GDP

**Philosophical grounding**:
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-17 — Jürgen Habermas — Philosopher|Habermas on legitimate institutions]] — communicative rationality and procedural fairness
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Economics/2026-04-16 — Hayek's Knowledge Problem|Hayek's Knowledge Problem]] — distributed knowledge and why centralized extractive institutions fail epistemically
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-17 — Jean-François Lyotard — The Postmodern Condition|Lyotard on power-knowledge]] — institutions as legitimating narratives; loss of master narratives

**Key insight**: Institutions shape everything downstream (inequality, technology adoption, environmental externalities, well-being). **The causal arrow runs from institutions → outcomes**, not vice versa. This makes institutional design the highest-leverage policy lever.

### 4. Measurement & Epistemology
**Central insight**: What we measure shapes what we optimize for. Measuring beyond GDP, and moving from prediction to causation, are both measurements questions with profound philosophical stakes.

**Papers**:
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Measuring Well-Being and Progress (Stiglitz, Sen, Fitoussi)|Stiglitz, Sen, Fitoussi]] — beyond GDP (foundational)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Machine Learning Methods (Mullainathan & Spiess)|Mullainathan & Spiess]] — prediction vs. causation distinction
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Cost of Living in America (Lees & Philips)|Lees & Philips]] — historical real wage measurement
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Air Pollution and Mortality in China (Chen)|Chen et al.]] — causal inference methods

**Philosophical grounding**:
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-18 — Statistics as Philosophy|Statistics as Philosophy]] — measurement as epistemology; what counts as knowledge?
- **Goodhart's Law** — when a metric becomes a target, it ceases to be a good metric
- **Incommensurability** — can health, education, environmental quality, and democracy be reduced to a single metric (happiness, capabilities)?

**Key insight**: Measurement problems are not technical—they're philosophical. Every choice to measure (or not measure) something carries political implications. Lyotard's insight applies: **measuring well-being is a question of whose narrative gets privileged**.

### 5. Behavioral & Policy Realism
**Central insight**: Humans depart from rational-choice assumptions in systematic, predictable ways. Policy design must account for actual behavior, not idealized rationality.

**Papers**:
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Behavioral Economics and the Standard Model (Thaler)|Thaler]] — behavioral foundations (foundational)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Behavioral Responses to Wealth Taxes (Brülhart)|Brülhart]] — behavioral response to policy (migration, avoidance)
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — The Sum of Small Things (Pugh)|Pugh]] — status anxiety and consumption behavior
- [[2026-06-15 — Landmark Paper — Superstar Inequality (Kaplan & Rauh)|Kaplan & Rauh]] — behavioral exec compensation (overconfidence, anchoring)

**Philosophical grounding**:
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Cognitive-Science/|Bounded rationality]] — Simon's critique of rational choice
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Economics/2026-04-16 — Prospect Theory|Prospect Theory]] — loss aversion and reference dependence
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-18 — Statistics as Philosophy|Statistics as Philosophy]] — empiricism: actual behavior over assumptions

**Key insight**: Behavioral insights have profound policy implications. Wealth taxes fail not because the economic logic is wrong, but because behavioral responses (migration) change the incidence. Nudge theory and choice architecture matter because context shapes decisions.

## Cross-Constellation Connections

### Inequality → Technology → Institutions Loop

The papers reveal a reinforcing loop:
- **Institutions** shape whether technology complements or displaces labor (Acemoglu & Johnson)
- **Technology displacement** (without complementarity) drives inequality (Acemoglu & Restrepo, Goldin & Katz)
- **Wealth concentration** (Piketty) captures rents and shapes next-generation technology adoption
- **Extractive institutions** amplify this loop; **inclusive institutions** interrupt it

### Well-Being → Measurement → Legitimacy Crisis

Another reinforcing loop:
- **Measurement** (GDP) ignores distribution and externalities (Stiglitz/Sen/Fitoussi)
- **Mismeasurement** allows inequality and environmental damage to grow invisible
- **Visible inequality** creates legitimacy crises (Lyotard, Habermas)
- **Institutional design** must rebuild legitimacy through inclusive, transparent measurement

### Behavioral Anchors

Behavioral insights cut across all constellations:
- Status anxiety (Pugh) amplifies inequality perception
- Loss aversion (Thaler) makes redistribution politically difficult
- Overconfidence (Kaplan & Rauh) inflates executive compensation
- Bounded rationality (Simon) limits policy-makers' ability to design optimal institutions

## Philosophy & Emergence Bridges

### Core Philosophical Tensions

1. **Determinism vs. Agency** — Are outcomes determined by technology/markets, or chosen by institutions?
   - Acemoglu & Johnson argue: **chosen** (agency wins)
   - but Piketty & Gordon suggest limits to what policy can do (path dependency, compound growth)

2. **Universalism vs. Particularity** — Do mechanisms (r > g, skill-biased tech) apply universally, or are they context-dependent?
   - The papers suggest: universal mechanisms, but institutional **responses** are particular

3. **Measurement vs. Reality** — Can inequality, well-being, progress be meaningfully measured?
   - Stiglitz/Sen/Fitoussi say: yes, but multidimensionally
   - Lyotard warns: commensuration risks reducing incommensurable values to a single metric

### Emergence & Complex Systems

Inequality and technology-labor dynamics exhibit emergence properties:
- **Self-organization** — superstar inequality emerges from positive feedback (success breeds visibility breeds more success)
- **Criticality** — small institutional changes (education investment, tech innovation policy) can trigger large cascades
- **Path dependence** — early technology choices (automation vs. complementarity) shape long-run outcomes
- **Power laws** — superstar income distribution follows power laws, not normal distribution (characteristic of complex systems)

## Methodological Contributions

The 20 papers exemplify diverse research methods:

| Method | Papers | Why It Matters |
|--------|--------|--------|
| **Long-run historical analysis** | Piketty, Goldin & Katz, Gordon, Acemoglu & Robinson | Escapes short-term volatility; reveals structural patterns |
| **Natural experiments** | Chen (Huai River policy), Acemoglu & Restrepo (Berlitz collapse), Brülhart (cantonal variation) | Enables causal inference without randomized trials |
| **Comparative international analysis** | Acemoglu & Robinson, Piketty, Stiglitz/Sen/Fitoussi | Shows variation in outcomes; tests universality of mechanisms |
| **Machine learning + causal inference** | Mullainathan & Spiess | Bridges prediction and causal explanation |
| **Empirical behavioral observation** | Thaler, Pugh, Kaplan & Rauh | Grounds theory in actual human behavior |

**Unified insight**: All methods grapple with the same fundamental problem: **inferring causation from imperfect data**. No method is sufficient alone; triangulation across methods strengthens confidence.

## Questions for Further Exploration

1. **Can institutions truly reshape technology adoption?** Or are there deep economic constraints on which technologies succeed?
   - Acemoglu & Johnson say institutions matter; test by examining why some nations adopted complementary tech (education-augmenting) and others pure automation

2. **Is inequality overdetermined (multiple independent causes) or recursive (each cause amplifies others)?**
   - The five papers suggest the latter: Piketty's r > g sets the stage, tech-labor mismatch amplifies it, institutions allow both, behavior cascades further

3. **What would a well-being-maximizing economy look like?** And is it achievable without sacrificing growth?
   - Stiglitz/Sen/Fitoussi and Banerjee et al. suggest "inclusive growth" reconciles both; test by examining which institutions enable this trade-off

4. **Can machine learning contribute to causal understanding, or only prediction?**
   - Mullainathan & Spiess provide a bridge; explore how high-dimensional data can hint at causal mechanisms without proving them

5. **What makes institutions stable or changeable?** Why do some nations transition from extractive to inclusive institutions, while others lock in?
   - Acemoglu & Robinson identify "critical junctures"; but what triggers them?

## Methodological Epilogue

These 20 papers collectively argue for **methodological pluralism**:
- Historical analysis reveals long-run patterns
- Natural experiments enable causal inference
- Behavioral observation grounds theory in reality
- Machine learning reveals patterns in high-dimensional data
- Philosophical reflection ensures we're asking the right questions

**No single method suffices.** The best economics uses all of them, triangulating to truth.
