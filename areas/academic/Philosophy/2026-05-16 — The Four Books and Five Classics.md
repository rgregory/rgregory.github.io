# The Four Books and Five Classics

The Confucian canon — the *sìshū wǔjīng* (四書五經) — is the body of texts that defined Chinese intellectual life for roughly 2,000 years. Together these nine texts formed the curriculum of the imperial examination system, the educational standard for the literati class, and the moral-philosophical framework of Chinese civilization from the Han Dynasty through the fall of the Qing in 1912.

---

## The Four Books (四書, *Sìshū*)

Zhu Xi (朱熹, 1130–1200), the great Neo-Confucian synthesizer of the Song Dynasty, elevated these four texts above the Five Classics as the essential entry point into Confucian learning. From the Yuan Dynasty onward, the Four Books became the primary curriculum for the imperial examinations.

### 1. The Analects (論語, *Lúnyǔ*)

Sayings and conversations of Confucius (551–479 BCE), compiled by his students after his death. The foundational text: it establishes *rén* (benevolence), *lǐ* (ritual propriety), and the rectification of names as organizing concepts. Not a systematic treatise — deliberately situational and contextual. See [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — The Confucian Moral Framework|The Confucian Moral Framework]] for its content.

### 2. The Mencius (孟子, *Mèngzǐ*)

The collected teachings of Mencius (ca. 372–289 BCE), Confucius's most important intellectual heir. Extended arguments for the innate goodness of human nature, the primacy of the people over the ruler, and the theory of benevolent government (*rénzhèng*, 仁政). Mencius argues that a ruler who fails the people loses the moral right to rule — a politically charged position that gives philosophical grounding to the Mandate of Heaven. See [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — The Mandate of Heaven|The Mandate of Heaven]].

### 3. The Great Learning (大學, *Dàxué*)

Originally a chapter of the *Book of Rites*, extracted and elevated by Zhu Xi. A short text articulating the "eight steps" of Confucian self-cultivation, from the investigation of things (*gewu*, 格物) through the ordering of the state to bringing peace under heaven (*tianxia*). The famous passage:

> "The ancients who wished to illuminate illustrious virtue throughout the world first regulated their states; wishing to regulate their states, they first aligned their families; wishing to align their families, they first cultivated themselves..."

This cascade — self → family → state → world — is the structural logic of Confucian political philosophy.

### 4. The Doctrine of the Mean (中庸, *Zhōngyōng*)

Also originally a chapter of the *Book of Rites*. A philosophical treatment of moral equilibrium (*zhong*, 中) and constancy (*yong*, 庸). Argues that the highest moral achievement is the unwavering enactment of virtue regardless of circumstance — not a compromise between extremes but the steady maintenance of the correct. Associated with Zisi, Confucius's grandson. Philosophically dense; more cosmological in tone than the other Three Books, gesturing toward Heaven (*tian*, 天) as the ground of moral order.

## The Five Classics (五經, *Wǔjīng*)

The Five Classics predate the Four Books. They are the ancient texts that Confucius himself reportedly edited, transmitted, or studied. Their canonization occurred progressively through the Han Dynasty.

### 1. The I Ching / Book of Changes (易經, *Yìjīng*)

The oldest of the five. Originally a divination manual organized around 64 hexagrams, each made of six broken or unbroken lines, representing states of change. The *Yijing* accumulated philosophical commentary over centuries (the "Ten Wings"), transforming it into a cosmological treatise on the patterns underlying change in nature and human affairs. It influenced virtually every Chinese intellectual tradition — Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist alike.

### 2. The Book of Odes / Classic of Poetry (詩經, *Shījīng*)

An anthology of 305 poems from the Western Zhou period (1046–771 BCE): folk songs, court hymns, and ritual songs. Confucius reputedly said that reading the *Odes* teaches one how to think, how to observe, how to maintain social bonds, and how to express remonstrance (*Analects* 17.9). The *Odes* were used in diplomacy, court ritual, and moral instruction. Mastery required knowing which poem to quote in which situation.

### 3. The Book of Rites (禮記, *Lǐjì*)

A compilation of texts on ritual propriety, social norms, and philosophical reflection compiled during the Han Dynasty. The *Lǐjì* is vast and heterogeneous — it covers ancestral rites, mourning procedures, court ceremony, music, and political philosophy. Two of its chapters became the *Great Learning* and *Doctrine of the Mean* (the third and fourth of the Four Books). The *Lǐjì* is the authoritative reference for *lǐ* (ritual propriety) as a concept and practice.

*Note*: There were originally three Rites texts (*Yili*, *Zhouli*, *Liji*); only the *Lǐjì* was canonized in the standard Five Classics list.

### 4. The Book of Documents / Classic of History (書經, *Shūjīng*)

Purportedly a collection of speeches and proclamations from ancient sage-kings (Yao, Shun, Yu) and early Zhou rulers. The most politically significant of the five: it records the rhetoric of legitimate governance, including the earliest formulation of the Mandate of Heaven as a concept. Heavily used in political argumentation — citing the *Shujing* was citing precedent from antiquity. Portions are now considered forgeries, but their influence was real regardless.

### 5. The Spring and Autumn Annals (春秋, *Chūnqiū*)

A terse chronicle of the state of Lu (Confucius's home state) from 722 to 481 BCE, traditionally attributed to Confucius himself as editor. The text is famously laconic — recording events in minimal language. But the Confucian tradition read enormous moral significance into every word choice (*zhèngmíng* — rectification of names — at the level of historical writing). The *Zuo Commentary* (*Zuozhuan*), written as an expanded narrative companion, provides the historical flesh; the *Spring and Autumn* provides the moral skeleton.

## The Canon and the Imperial Examinations

From the Han Dynasty onward, mastery of these texts was the credential for government service. The *jǔrén* system of the Han gave way to the fully developed *kējǔ* (科舉) examination system of the Sui and Tang Dynasties (see [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-05-16 — The Imperial Examination System|The Imperial Examination System]]). From the Song Dynasty through the Ming, the Four Books in particular — especially in Zhu Xi's commentaries — were the examination curriculum's core.

This created a feedback loop: the state certified mastery of these texts as the qualification for power; students spent decades memorizing and internalizing them; officials were selected from those who had; those officials then governed in the conceptual framework the texts provided.

---

## Neo-Confucianism and the Canon

Zhu Xi's Song Dynasty synthesis (*lǐxué*, 理學 — School of Principle) rearranged the canon's centre of gravity toward the Four Books. His commentaries on all four became nearly as canonical as the texts themselves — during the Ming and Qing Dynasties, examination candidates were expected to interpret the Four Books through Zhu Xi's readings. This created its own problem of ossification: by the late Ming, the *baguwen* (eight-legged essay) examination format required such rigid formal compliance that genuine philosophical creativity was structurally suppressed.
