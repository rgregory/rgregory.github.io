# Gram-Negative Bacteria

Gram-negative bacteria are a class of bacteria characterized by their cell wall structure, which does not retain the crystal violet stain used in the Gram staining protocol (appearing pink/red under a microscope). This structural distinction has profound implications for their survival, pathogenesis, and resistance to medical treatment.

## Structural Architecture

The defining feature of Gram-negative bacteria is their **double-layered membrane system**:

1.  **Inner Cytoplasmic Membrane**: Standard phospholipid bilayer.
2.  **Periplasmic Space**: A gap containing a thin layer of **peptidoglycan** (much thinner than in Gram-positive bacteria) and high concentrations of enzymes.
3.  **Outer Membrane**: A unique asymmetrical bilayer.
    *   **Inner Leaflet**: Phospholipids.
    *   **Outer Leaflet**: **Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)**.
    *   **Porins**: Channel-forming proteins that allow the passage of small molecules but act as barriers to larger ones (like many antibiotics).

## Clinical Significance: The LPS Endotoxin

The LPS in the outer membrane is a potent **endotoxin**. When the bacteria die or the cell wall is disrupted:
- **Lipid A** (the toxic component of LPS) is released into the bloodstream.
- This triggers a massive inflammatory response by the host's immune system.
- Can lead to **septic shock**, characterized by systemic inflammation, hypotension, and multi-organ failure.

## Antibiotic Resistance Mechanisms

Gram-negative bacteria are inherently more difficult to treat than Gram-positive bacteria due to several "built-in" defenses:

-   **Physical Barrier**: The outer membrane prevents large, hydrophilic antibiotics (e.g., Vancomycin) from reaching their targets.
-   **Efflux Pumps**: Specialized protein complexes that actively pump antibiotics out of the cell.
-   **Enzymatic Degradation**: Beta-lactamase enzymes (which destroy penicillins) are concentrated in the periplasmic space, intercepting drugs before they reach the cell membrane.
