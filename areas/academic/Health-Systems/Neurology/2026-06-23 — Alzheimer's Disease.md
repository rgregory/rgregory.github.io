# Alzheimer's Disease

The most common cause of dementia, accounting for **~60–70%** of all dementia cases worldwide. A progressive neurodegenerative disorder defined pathologically by extracellular **amyloid-beta plaques** and intraneuronal **tau neurofibrillary tangles**, clinically by an insidious, progressive amnestic syndrome that expands to global cognitive failure. First described by Alois Alzheimer in 1906 (patient Auguste Deter). Now reframed (2024) as a biologically defined disease detectable years before symptoms.

---

## Pathophysiology

### Amyloid-beta (Aβ) plaques
- **APP processing**: Amyloid Precursor Protein is cleaved by either the non-amyloidogenic pathway (**α-secretase** → no Aβ) or the amyloidogenic pathway (**β-secretase / BACE1** then **γ-secretase**), the latter liberating Aβ peptides.
- **Aβ42** (42 amino acids) is more hydrophobic and aggregation-prone than Aβ40; an elevated **Aβ42:Aβ40 ratio shift** drives oligomerization → protofibrils → fibrils → insoluble extracellular **senile (neuritic) plaques**. Soluble **Aβ oligomers** are now considered the most synaptotoxic species.

### Tau neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs)
- Tau is a microtubule-stabilizing protein. In AD it becomes **hyperphosphorylated**, detaches from microtubules, and self-aggregates into **paired helical filaments** → intraneuronal NFTs. Microtubule destabilization impairs axonal transport.
- **Braak staging** of tau spread: **I–II** transentorhinal/entorhinal cortex (preclinical) → **III–IV** limbic (hippocampus, amygdala; MCI/early dementia) → **V–VI** neocortex/isocortex (established dementia). Tau burden and topography correlate with cognitive decline far better than amyloid burden.

### Amyloid cascade hypothesis
The dominant pathogenic model: **Aβ accumulation is the initiating upstream event**, triggering downstream tau pathology, neuroinflammation, synaptic loss, and neurodegeneration. Supported by autosomal-dominant mutations (all increase Aβ42 production/aggregation) and Down syndrome. Criticized because amyloid load correlates poorly with cognition and decades of amyloid-only drugs failed — but partly vindicated by lecanemab/donanemab showing clinical benefit from amyloid removal. Current view: amyloid is necessary-but-not-sufficient; **tau is the proximate driver** of symptoms.

### Downstream pathology
- **Synaptic and neuronal loss**: synapse loss is the strongest structural correlate of cognitive decline. Gross atrophy is earliest/most severe in the **medial temporal lobe (hippocampus, entorhinal cortex)**, spreading to temporoparietal and frontal association cortex; ventricular enlargement.
- **Cholinergic deficit**: degeneration of the **nucleus basalis of Meynert** depletes acetylcholine in cortex/hippocampus — the basis of the "cholinergic hypothesis" and rationale for cholinesterase inhibitors.
- **Neuroinflammation**: chronic activation of **microglia** and astrocytes around plaques; **TREM2** and **CD33** risk variants implicate innate immunity. Inflammation can be both protective (clearance) and harmful (amplifying neurodegeneration).

## Genetics

| Gene | Type | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **APOE ε4** | Sporadic (late-onset) **risk** gene — most important | **Heterozygote (1 copy): ~3× risk**; **Homozygote (2 copies): ~12–15× risk** & earlier onset. ε2 is protective; ε3 neutral. Not deterministic. |
| **APP** (chr 21) | Autosomal-dominant early-onset familial AD (EOFAD) | Mutations increase Aβ42 production/aggregation |
| **PSEN1** (chr 14) | Autosomal-dominant EOFAD — most common cause | Component of γ-secretase; most aggressive, earliest onset (30s–50s) |
| **PSEN2** (chr 1) | Autosomal-dominant EOFAD — rarest | γ-secretase component; more variable onset |

- **Sporadic (late-onset, >65)** accounts for **>95%** of cases; multifactorial (APOE + lifestyle + age). **Autosomal-dominant familial AD is <1%** but causes early onset and is fully penetrant.
- **Down syndrome / trisomy 21**: an extra copy of the **APP gene** (on chr 21) causes near-universal Aβ deposition by age 40 and dementia in the majority by their 50s–60s — strong human evidence for the amyloid cascade.

## Risk Factors

### Modifiable — 2020 Lancet Commission (12 factors, ~40% of dementia potentially preventable)
Less education (early life); **hypertension, obesity, hearing loss** (midlife); **smoking, depression, physical inactivity, diabetes, low social contact, excessive alcohol, traumatic brain injury, air pollution** (later life). (The 2024 update added **untreated vision loss** and **high LDL cholesterol** → 14 factors, ~45%.)

### Non-modifiable
- **Age** — the dominant risk factor (prevalence roughly doubles every ~5 years after 65)
- **Sex** — women have higher lifetime risk (partly greater longevity)
- **Family history / genetics** (APOE ε4, autosomal-dominant mutations)
- **Down syndrome**

## Clinical Stages & Cognitive Trajectory

1. **Preclinical AD** — biomarker-positive (amyloid+), cognitively normal; can precede symptoms by **15–20 years**.
2. **MCI due to AD** — measurable cognitive decline (typically episodic memory), function largely preserved; ~10–15%/yr progress to dementia.
3. **Mild dementia** — memory loss interferes with daily life; word-finding, navigation difficulties.
4. **Moderate dementia** — pronounced deficits; needs ADL assistance; behavioral/psychiatric symptoms (agitation, wandering, sundowning).
5. **Severe dementia** — minimal language, non-ambulatory, incontinent, fully dependent; death often from aspiration pneumonia.

**Typical cognitive cascade**: **episodic memory** (hippocampal — recent events, repetition) → **language & visuospatial** (temporoparietal — anomia, getting lost) → **executive function** (frontal — judgment, planning) → **global** cognitive and functional collapse. Procedural memory and remote memories are relatively spared until late.

## Atypical Variants

- **Posterior Cortical Atrophy (PCA)** — "visual variant"; occipitoparietal atrophy → visuospatial/visuoperceptual deficits (Bálint/Gerstmann syndromes), reading difficulty, with relatively preserved memory early. Younger onset.
- **Logopenic variant Primary Progressive Aphasia (lvPPA)** — left temporoparietal; impaired word retrieval and sentence repetition with preserved single-word comprehension/grammar. Most lvPPA is AD pathology.
- **Frontal / behavioral (dysexecutive) variant** — prominent executive dysfunction or behavioral change; mimics bvFTD but has AD pathology.
- **Early-Onset AD (EOAD, <65)** — ~5–10% of cases; more often atypical (PCA, lvPPA, dysexecutive), faster progression, less APOE-driven, enriched for autosomal-dominant mutations.

## Diagnosis

- **DSM-5**: "Major (or Mild) Neurocognitive Disorder due to Alzheimer's Disease."
- **NIA-AA 2011**: introduced preclinical / MCI / dementia stages; incorporated biomarkers into research criteria.
- **NIA-AA 2018 — ATN research framework**: defines AD *biologically* by biomarkers, independent of symptoms:
  - **A** = **A**myloid (amyloid PET or CSF Aβ42↓)
  - **T** = **T**au (tau PET or CSF p-tau↑)
  - **N** = **N**eurodegeneration (MRI atrophy, FDG-PET, CSF total-tau↑)
  - AD requires **A+T+** ("Alzheimer's continuum" = A+ alone).
- **NIA-AA 2024 revision**: AD defined as a **biological entity** diagnosable in living patients via biomarkers; expands categories to **A, T1/T2 (core vs spreading tau), N, I (inflammation), V (vascular), S (synuclein co-pathology)**; explicitly endorses **plasma biomarkers** (e.g., p-tau217) as core diagnostic tools. (Controversial for labeling asymptomatic biomarker-positive people as "having" AD.)

## Biomarkers

| Biomarker | Class | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Amyloid PET** (florbetapir, flutemetamol, florbetaben) | A | Visualizes fibrillar plaque; turns positive earliest |
| **CSF Aβ42** (or **Aβ42:Aβ40 ratio**) | A | **Decreased** (sequestered in plaque); ratio corrects for production |
| **CSF p-tau181** | T | **Increased** — specific for AD tau pathology |
| **CSF total-tau (t-tau)** | N | **Increased** — marks neuronal injury (non-specific) |
| **Plasma p-tau217** | A/T | **Recent breakthrough** — blood test with **~90%+ accuracy** vs PET/CSF; scalable, low-cost screening; the most promising plasma marker. (p-tau181, GFAP, NfL also used.) |
| **Tau PET** (flortaucipir) | T | Maps NFT burden/topography; correlates strongly with symptoms & Braak stage |
| **MRI** | N | **Hippocampal & medial temporal atrophy**; temporoparietal volume loss; rules out vascular/structural causes |

## Treatment — Symptomatic

### Cholinesterase inhibitors (mild–moderate; rivastigmine also severe)
- **Donepezil** (Aricept) — once daily; all stages.
- **Rivastigmine** (Exelon) — oral or **transdermal patch**; also inhibits butyrylcholinesterase.
- **Galantamine** (Razadyne) — also modulates nicotinic receptors.
- Modest symptomatic benefit; do not alter underlying course. Side effects: GI (nausea, diarrhea), bradycardia.

### NMDA receptor antagonist (moderate–severe)
- **Memantine** (Namenda) — blocks glutamate excitotoxicity; often **combined with donepezil** (Namzaric). Well tolerated.

## Treatment — Disease-Modifying (anti-amyloid monoclonal antibodies)

| Drug | Brand | Pivotal trial | Slowing of decline | Target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lecanemab** | Leqembi | **CLARITY-AD** | **~27%** (CDR-SB over 18 mo) | Soluble Aβ **protofibrils** |
| **Donanemab** | Kisunla | **TRAILBLAZER-ALZ 2** | **~35%** (iADRS; greater in low-tau subgroup) | **Deposited plaque** (pyroglutamate Aβ) |
| **Aducanumab** | Aduhelm | EMERGE/ENGAGE | Mixed/contested | Aggregated Aβ |

- **Indication**: early symptomatic AD (MCI or mild dementia) with **confirmed amyloid positivity**. Lecanemab dosed IV q2 weeks; donanemab IV q4 weeks with the option to **stop once plaques are cleared**.
- **Mechanism distinction**: lecanemab preferentially binds **soluble protofibrils**; donanemab targets **established/deposited plaque** (pyroglutamate-modified Aβ).
- **ARIA (Amyloid-Related Imaging Abnormalities)** — the key safety risk: **ARIA-E** (edema/effusion) and **ARIA-H** (microhemorrhage/hemosiderin). Usually asymptomatic but can be serious/fatal. Risk is **strongly elevated in APOE ε4 carriers, especially homozygotes** → genotyping recommended before treatment; requires serial MRI monitoring. Avoid/caution with anticoagulants.
- **Aducanumab** — first anti-amyloid approved (FDA accelerated approval, 2021) amid major controversy over efficacy; **withdrawn by Biogen in 2024** (development discontinued).
- Benefits are **modest (slowing, not reversal/halting)**; significant cost, infusion burden, and ARIA risk drive ongoing debate about clinical meaningfulness.

## Prognosis & Survival

- **Mean survival ~4–8 (up to 10) years** from diagnosis; varies with age at onset and comorbidity. Younger-onset patients may survive longer in absolute terms but with faster decline.
- Inexorably progressive; no cure. Common terminal events: **aspiration pneumonia**, sepsis, malnutrition, complications of immobility. AD is a leading cause of death in older adults.

---

## Epidemiology

- **~60–70% of all dementia** (most common cause).
- **~7 million** Americans living with AD (2024); **~55 million** people with dementia worldwide, projected to ~139M by 2050.
- **Age prevalence** rises sharply: roughly **~5% at 65–74**, **~13% at 75–84**, **~33%+ at 85+**. Prevalence approximately doubles every 5 years after 65.
- Women disproportionately affected (~2/3 of US cases).

---

## Current Research

- **Tau-targeting therapies** — anti-tau monoclonal antibodies, tau aggregation inhibitors, and **antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs)** to lower tau production; rationale that tau tracks symptoms better than amyloid.
- **Blood-based screening** — **plasma p-tau217** (and p-tau181, GFAP, NfL, Aβ42:40) poised to transform diagnosis/triage in primary care, replacing invasive LP/expensive PET.
- **Prevention / pre-symptomatic trials** — **FINGER** (multidomain lifestyle: diet, exercise, cognitive training, vascular control — showed cognitive benefit; **U.S. POINTER** replication); anti-amyloid in **preclinical/asymptomatic** populations (**AHEAD 3-45** with lecanemab; **DIAN-TU** in autosomal-dominant carriers; A4 study).
- **Combination approaches** — anti-amyloid + anti-tau; secretase modulation; **active/passive vaccines**; targeting neuroinflammation (microglia/TREM2), and the shift toward **earliest-possible intervention** before irreversible neurodegeneration.
