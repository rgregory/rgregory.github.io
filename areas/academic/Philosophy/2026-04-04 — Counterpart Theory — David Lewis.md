# Counterpart Theory — David Lewis

Counterpart Theory is Lewis's account of how to make sense of modal claims within Modal Realism. It replaces the idea that *you yourself* exist in other possible worlds.

---

## The Problem It Solves

Standard possible worlds semantics (Kripke): modal claims = quantification over worlds.
- "You could have been taller" = there is a world where *you* are taller

Problem: if you are a concrete physical object, you cannot be in two places at once. You are world-bound. How can the very same you exist with a different height, different history?

**Transworld identity problem**: what makes an individual in world W₂ *the same individual* as one in W₁?

Lewis's solution: **you don't exist in other worlds. What exists there is your counterpart.**

---

## The Core Idea

Your **counterpart** in another world is an individual who:
- Exists in that world
- Resembles you in relevant respects
- Is more similar to you than any other individual in that world

The counterpart relation is **not identity** — it is similarity.

Modal translations:
- "You could have been taller" = some world contains a counterpart of you who is taller
- "You could not have been a poached egg" = no world contains a counterpart of you that is a poached egg

---

## Why Similarity, Not Identity?

The counterpart relation is **context-sensitive**. Depending on which features are salient, different individuals in other worlds count as your counterpart.

Consequences:
- You may have **multiple** counterparts in a single world
- You may have **no** counterpart in some worlds
- Which individual counts as your counterpart varies with the modal question

"Could I have been a brain surgeon?" — emphasizes intellectual/career features
"Could I have had a different mother?" — emphasizes genetic/developmental facts
These may pick out different counterparts.

---

## Formal Theory (1968)

Primitive predicates: Wx (x is a world), Ixy (x is in y), Ax (x is actual), Cxy (x is counterpart of y)

Key axioms:
- Nothing is in two worlds
- Counterparts are in distinct worlds from what they are counterparts of

Translation scheme:
- ◇φ(a) = ∃x∃w(Iwx ∧ Cxa ∧ φ(x)) — some counterpart of a satisfies φ in some world
- □φ(a) = ∀x∀w(Iwx ∧ Cxa → φ(x)) — all counterparts of a satisfy φ in all worlds

---

## Key Consequences

**De re modality without essentialism**
What's "essential" to you = what all your counterparts share. Essences are derivative, not primitive.

**The Humphrey Objection (Kripke)**
"Could Humphrey have won?" — we mean *Humphrey himself*, not a similar individual.
Lewis's response: the counterpart relation tracks Humphrey. His counterpart *represents* him. The claim is genuinely about Humphrey; truth conditions are cashed out via representation, not identity.

**Variable counterpart relations**
Same sentence can have different truth values depending on context. Feature (captures flexibility of modal talk) or bug (makes modal truth unstable) — contested.

**Temporal analogy**
You are one person at different times with different properties (not contradictory — relativized to times). Similarly, counterparts have different properties in different worlds — but they are distinct individuals, not you at different temporal stages.

---

## Counterpart Theory vs. Transworld Identity

| | Counterpart Theory | Transworld Identity |
|---|---|---|
| Same individual across worlds? | No — distinct counterparts | Yes — same individual |
| Grounds de re modality | Similarity relation | Haecceity / essence |
| Humphrey's fate | His counterpart wins | He himself wins |
| Intuitive match | Less intuitive, more coherent | More intuitive, less coherent |

**Plantinga's haecceities**: "thisness" properties like *being Aristotle* as primitive necessary properties tracking individuals across worlds. Lewis: this is transworld identity renamed without explanation.

---

## Connection to Modal Realism

Counterpart Theory only makes full sense within **Modal Realism** — possible worlds as genuine, concrete, spatiotemporally isolated universes. The concreteness of worlds is why individuals are world-bound, which is why counterparts are needed instead of transworld identity.

If worlds are merely abstract (ersatz realism), transworld identity becomes less problematic — you could exist in multiple abstract structures without being in two concrete places.

---

## The Philosophical Payoff

1. **Deflates transworld identity** — removes mysterious relation between numerically identical individuals in different concrete universes
2. **Makes modal claims precise** — rigorous first-order semantics for de re modal claims
3. **Captures context-sensitivity** — explains why "could have been different" varies with context
4. **Grounds essentialism empirically** — what's essential = what counterparts invariably share

**The cost**: you must accept that when you say "I might have been otherwise," this is true in virtue of a *similar individual* elsewhere, not you yourself. Many find this deeply counterintuitive.

Lewis's reply: the intuition that *you* are in other worlds is generated by confusion. Like thinking the morning star and evening star are different objects — it reflects how we think, not what things are.
