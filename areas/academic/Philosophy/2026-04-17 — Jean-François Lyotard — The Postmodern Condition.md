# Jean-François Lyotard — The Postmodern Condition

French philosopher (1924–1998). Best known for *The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge* (1979), commissioned by the Quebec government as a study of the status of knowledge in contemporary society. Defines postmodernity through the collapse of **metanarratives**.

---

## The Postmodern Condition: Core Argument

**Modernity** was legitimated by grand narratives (metanarratives):
1. **The Enlightenment narrative**: science and reason progressively liberate humanity
2. **The Hegelian narrative**: history progresses toward the self-realization of Spirit
3. **The Marxist narrative**: history moves toward the emancipation of the proletariat

**Postmodernity** = **incredulity toward metanarratives**. These legitimating stories have lost their credibility. We no longer believe in History with a capital H, or Progress, or a single rational foundation.

---

## Language Games

Following Wittgenstein, Lyotard holds that different domains (science, ethics, art, politics) operate according to different and **incommensurable** language games. There is no meta-language that can adjudicate between them.

The aspiration to a single legitimating discourse — Habermas's ideal of consensus — is for Lyotard **terroristic**: it suppresses the heterogeneity of language games by forcing everything into one.

---

## Paralogy vs. Consensus

- **Habermas**: the goal of discourse is rational consensus under ideal conditions
- **Lyotard**: consensus is the wrong goal — **paralogy** (the production of new moves, instabilities, and dissent within and between language games) is how knowledge actually advances

Science progresses not by consensus but by challenging existing rules — producing paradoxes, anomalies, and novel frameworks.

---

## The Differend

In *The Differend* (1983), Lyotard develops the concept of a situation where a conflict cannot be equitably resolved because the parties operate under different rules, and applying one party's rules silences the other. The differend is the injustice that cannot be articulated within the prevailing idiom.
