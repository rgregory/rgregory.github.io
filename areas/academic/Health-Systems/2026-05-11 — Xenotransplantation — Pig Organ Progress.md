# Xenotransplantation — Pig Organ Progress (2025)

Xenotransplantation — transplanting animal organs into humans — advanced significantly in 2024–2025, with multiple landmark cases demonstrating longer survival times and building the evidence base for clinical trials.

---

## Background

The global shortage of transplantable human organs is acute:
- ~100,000 people on the US transplant waitlist at any time
- ~20 die each day waiting for a kidney
- Demand will only increase with aging populations and chronic disease

Pigs have long been the preferred xenotransplantation donor species: similar organ size, well-characterized genetics, and existing breeding infrastructure. The challenge is **rejection** — human immune systems aggressively attack pig tissue — and **physiological compatibility**.

---

## The Genetic Engineering Approach

Modern pig organs used in xenotransplantation are from gene-edited animals. The editing removes pig genes that trigger human immune responses and adds human genes that help the organ evade rejection:

**Knockouts (remove)**:
- *GGTA1* — encodes α-Gal, a pig sugar antigen that human antibodies immediately attack
- *CMAH*, *β4GalNT2* — additional pig carbohydrate antigens
- *SLA* class II genes — reduce T-cell-mediated rejection
- Porcine endogenous retroviruses (PERVs) — viral sequences embedded in the pig genome that could potentially infect human cells (14 PERV loci knocked out by eGenesis)

**Knock-ins (add)**:
- *CD46*, *CD55*, *CD59* — human complement regulatory proteins
- *CD47* — "don't eat me" signal that protects against macrophage phagocytosis
- *HLA-E* — reduces NK cell killing
- *TBM*, *EPCR*, *CD39* — human coagulation regulators (pig and human coagulation systems are not fully compatible)

The current generation of genetically engineered pigs carries 10+ simultaneous edits.

---

## 2024–2025 Milestones

**Kidney transplants (2024)**: Massachusetts General Hospital performed the first genetically engineered pig kidney transplant into a living human patient (March 2024) — 62-year-old patient, 69-day survival before death from an unrelated cause; kidney continued to function until the patient's death. A second patient received a pig kidney in November 2024; that patient remained alive into 2025 with functioning graft.

**Heart transplants**: the first pig heart transplant (2022, Lawrence Faucette and David Bennett Sr.) demonstrated feasibility; the 2024 Duke case used improved multi-gene-edited pigs and achieved longer survival

**Liver and lung**: earlier stage; pig livers have been used as temporary "bridges" in patients awaiting human livers; pig lungs transplanted in brain-dead humans showed gas exchange

**Immunosuppression refinement**: the 2025 cases use more targeted immunosuppression (co-stimulation blockade: anti-CD40L, belatacept) reducing the toxic side effects of broad immunosuppression while achieving better rejection control

---

## Outstanding Challenges

- **Chronic rejection** remains a barrier; even with strong initial function, long-term outcomes are not established
- **Physiological mismatches**: pig kidneys produce slightly different quantities of EPO, renin, etc.; long-term metabolic consequences are unknown
- **Infectious risk**: PERVs are inactivated in current animals, but novel zoonotic transmission remains a theoretical concern
- **Regulatory pathway**: the FDA is developing a framework; current cases are under compassionate use and expanded access protocols, not standard approval
