# Heuristics — Fast Rules for Good-Enough Decisions

## What They Are

Heuristics are mental shortcuts — simplified decision rules that sacrifice optimality for speed and low cognitive load. The term comes from the Greek *heuriskein* (to find, to discover).

Two major research traditions offer conflicting interpretations of the same phenomenon.

---

## The Kahneman-Tversky Tradition: Heuristics as Biases

Kahneman and Tversky (1970s–80s) identified heuristics as systematic cognitive biases — predictable departures from rational Bayesian reasoning:

- **Availability heuristic**: judge probability by how easily examples come to mind → overestimate dramatic or recent events
- **Representativeness heuristic**: judge probability by resemblance to a prototype → base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy
- **Anchoring and adjustment**: start from an initial value, adjust insufficiently → anchored to irrelevant starting points
- **Affect heuristic**: judge risk/benefit by emotional response → "feels safe" ≠ is safe

---

## The Gigerenzen Tradition: Heuristics as Adaptive Tools

Gigerenzen argues heuristics are not bugs — they are features: fast-and-frugal rules that perform well in real-world environments with limited information and time. The "less-is-more" effect: simple heuristics sometimes outperform complex optimization models in noisy, real-world conditions.

- **Recognition heuristic**: if one option is recognized and the other isn't, infer the recognized one ranks higher
- **Take-the-best**: use the single most predictive cue; ignore the rest
- **Tallying**: count the number of cues favoring each option; don't weight them

**The core debate**: are heuristics cognitive errors relative to a Bayesian standard, or ecologically rational strategies that match the structure of real environments?

---

## Domain Applications

**Medical diagnosis**: heuristics explain both expert intuition (illness scripts) and diagnostic errors (anchoring, premature closure).

**Legal judgment**: heuristics influence verdicts — availability bias inflates perceived crime rates; affect heuristic produces harsher sentences for emotionally salient crimes.

**Financial decisions**: sunk cost fallacy, status quo bias, and loss aversion are all heuristic-driven departures from expected utility reasoning.
