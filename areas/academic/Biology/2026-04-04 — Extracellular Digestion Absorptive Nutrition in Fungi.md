# Extracellular Digestion — Absorptive Nutrition in Fungi

## The Mechanism

Fungi cannot ingest food. Instead, they secrete digestive enzymes directly into their substrate and absorb the resulting small molecules through their cell walls. This mode of feeding is called **absorptive nutrition** — heterotrophic and wholly external.

Contrast with other kingdoms:
- **Animals**: ingestive nutrition — food is taken inside the body and digested internally
- **Plants**: autotrophic — energy derived from photosynthesis, no external digestion
- **Bacteria**: also absorptive, but single-celled and structurally very different from fungi

## Enzyme Arsenal

Fungi secrete a substrate-dependent cocktail of digestive enzymes. The mix is regulated based on available carbon sources in the environment:

| Enzyme Class | Target Substrate |
|---|---|
| Cellulases / Hemicellulases | Plant cell wall polysaccharides (cellulose, hemicellulose) |
| Ligninases (lignin peroxidases, laccases) | Lignin — the most recalcitrant plant polymer |
| Proteases | Proteins |
| Lipases | Fats |
| Amylases | Starch |

Enzyme secretion is inducible: fungi sense the available carbon source and upregulate the appropriate enzyme pathway. This is metabolic efficiency at the cellular level.

## Wood Rot — Two Strategies

Lignocellulose (wood) is the most abundant polymer on land. Fungi have evolved two distinct strategies for breaking it down:

**White rot fungi** (e.g., oyster mushroom *Pleurotus ostreatus*, turkey tail *Trametes versicolor*)
- Degrade both cellulose AND lignin
- Leave pale, fibrous residue
- Have the most complete lignocellulosic toolkit; capable of full mineralization

**Brown rot fungi** (e.g., many bracket fungi)
- Degrade cellulose but leave lignin intact
- Leave brown, crumbly residue
- Brown rot lignin is chemically modified but structurally persistent — it remains in soil for centuries
- This makes brown rot a significant **carbon sequestration pathway** at the ecosystem level

## Ecological Significance

Fungi are the primary decomposers of lignocellulosic material in most terrestrial ecosystems. Without extracellular digestion by fungi, dead wood and leaf litter would accumulate indefinitely — nutrient cycling would halt, and terrestrial ecosystems would collapse.

The global carbon cycle depends on this process. Brown rot lignin persisting in soil is one mechanism by which terrestrial ecosystems act as long-term carbon sinks — directly relevant to climate and energy discussions.
