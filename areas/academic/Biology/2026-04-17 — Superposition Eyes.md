# Superposition Eyes

The **superposition eye** is the optical design of nocturnal and crepuscular insects — moths, fireflies, many beetles — and also decapod crustaceans (shrimp, lobster). Unlike the [[2026-04-17 — Apposition Eyes|apposition eye]] where each ommatidium is optically isolated, the superposition eye allows light from many facets to converge onto a single receptor.

This produces a **bright, unified image** on the retinal layer — at the cost of resolution — making it highly adapted for low-light environments.

---

## Optical Mechanism

A "clear zone" between the optics and the retina allows light rays entering through many facets to be refracted or reflected (depending on subtype) to a common focal point on the retina. The result: each photoreceptor receives light from many facets simultaneously, multiplying sensitivity.

### Subtypes

| Subtype | Mechanism | Example taxa |
|---|---|---|
| Refracting superposition | Graded-index lens system | Moths, butterflies |
| Reflecting superposition | Mirror optics (square facets) | Decapod crustaceans |
| Parabolic superposition | Parabolic mirror | Some beetles |

---

## Sensitivity vs. Resolution Trade-off

Sensitivity gain ≈ (diameter of superposition aperture)² / (single facet diameter)² — can be 1,000× more sensitive than an equivalent apposition eye.

Resolution is reduced correspondingly: the superposition image is effectively blurred by the number of facets contributing.
