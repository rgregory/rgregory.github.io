# Memory Decay and Cognitive Decline

Bridges the cognitive science of **forgetting** (how memory decays in normal brains) with the neurology of **cognitive decline** (how it deteriorates in aging and dementia). The two illuminate each other — the same taxonomy of forgetting that explains everyday memory loss also separates benign aging from Alzheimer's.

---

## Part A — The Science of Forgetting

### Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve (1885)
Hermann Ebbinghaus, self-experimenting with ~2,300 nonsense syllables (*Über das Gedächtnis*), produced the first quantitative law of forgetting.
- **Shape**: sharp drop then flattening — negatively accelerated, roughly **exponential** decay. Most loss in the first hours (~58% retained at 20 min, ~33% at 1 day, ~21% at 31 days).
- **Math**: modern fits use `R = e^(−t/S)` (exponential) or `R = a·t^(−b)` (power). **Power-law vs. exponential debate** persists — power fits aggregate data better but may be an *averaging artifact* (Wixted & Ebbesen 1991).
- **Savings**: even "forgotten" material is **relearned faster** — proof traces persist below conscious recall.
- **Spacing effect**: distributed practice beats massed — the engine behind spaced repetition. Replicated by Murre & Dros (2015).

### Decay vs. Interference — Why Do We Forget?
The central 20th-century debate: passive fading vs. active competition.
- **Decay theory** (Thorndike's "law of disuse"): traces erode as a function of *time* if unrehearsed.
- **Interference theory**: other learning, not time, causes forgetting.
  - **Retroactive interference**: new learning impairs old memories (B → forgets A)
  - **Proactive interference**: old learning impairs new (A → harder to recall B). Underwood (1957) showed most list-learning "forgetting" was PI, not decay.
- **Decisive evidence — Jenkins & Dallenbach (1924)**: subjects who **slept** during the retention interval forgot far less than those awake, despite equal elapsed time. Time alone isn't the driver.
- **Consensus**: interference dominates long-term forgetting; pure time-decay is hard to demonstrate cleanly. Decay survives mainly at the seconds scale (working memory).

### McGeoch's Critique of Trace Decay (1932)
Foundational attack on decay theory:
1. **Time per se causes nothing** — "iron rusts over time, but oxidation causes rust, not time." What matters is *what happens during* the interval.
2. **Spontaneous recovery / reminiscence** — forgotten material sometimes returns *stronger*; pure decay can't explain that.
3. **Cue-dependent retrieval** — the right cue resurrects "decayed" memories, implying the trace was intact but inaccessible (foreshadows Tulving).

### Consolidation & Reconsolidation
Newly encoded memories are **labile** and must stabilize.
- **Synaptic consolidation** (minutes–hours): LTP, protein synthesis (CREB).
- **Systems consolidation** (days–years): **Standard Model** (Squire & Alvarez) — memories start **hippocampus**-dependent; the hippocampus "trains" the **neocortex** until memories become cortically distributed and hippocampus-independent (cf. Complementary Learning Systems, McClelland et al. 1995). Competing **Multiple Trace Theory** (Nadel & Moscovitch): rich episodic memories *always* keep a hippocampal component.
- **Sleep**: **slow-wave sleep** consolidates declarative memory via hippocampal-neocortical replay (sharp-wave ripples); REM linked to procedural/emotional.
- **Ribot's Law (1881)**: in retrograde amnesia, **recent memories are most vulnerable**, remote ones preserved — a *temporal gradient*. Recent memories decay faster because they haven't completed systems consolidation. (Seen in H.M. and in Alzheimer's — see Part B.)
- **Reconsolidation** (Nader, Schafe & LeDoux 2000): retrieval *reactivates* a memory into a labile state that must re-stabilize — during which it can be updated, strengthened, or disrupted. **Memory is reconstructive, not a fixed recording** — every recall can rewrite the trace.

### Three Loci of Forgetting
- **Encoding failure** — never entered LTM (you can't draw a coin you've seen 1000×; Nickerson & Adams 1979). Cf. **levels of processing** (Craik & Lockhart 1972) — deep semantic encoding outlasts shallow.
- **Storage decay** — trace degrades/overwritten after encoding (the Ebbinghaus curve).
- **Retrieval failure** — memory **intact but inaccessible** (the dominant modern explanation). Evidence: tip-of-the-tongue, savings, successful cued recall.

### Cue-Dependent Forgetting (Tulving)
Most everyday forgetting is a **retrieval** failure.
- **Encoding specificity** (Tulving & Thomson 1973): a cue works only if it was encoded *with* the target.
- **Availability vs. accessibility** (Tulving & Pearlstone 1966): items unrecallable by free recall are still recognized — available but inaccessible.
- **Context-dependent** (Godden & Baddeley 1975): divers recalled ~40% better when test environment (underwater/land) matched encoding.
- **State-dependent / mood-congruent** (Bower 1981): internal state match aids recall.

### Countermeasures to Decay
- **Spaced repetition**: expanding intervals "reset" decay (Landauer & Bjork 1978). Algorithms: Leitner, SuperMemo SM-2, Anki, FSRS.
- **Testing effect / retrieval practice** (Roediger & Karpicke 2006): *retrieving* beats re-reading — study-then-test retained ~61% at one week vs. ~40% for restudy, despite restudiers feeling *more* confident (metacognitive illusion).
- **Desirable difficulties** (R. Bjork): spacing, interleaving, testing slow acquisition but boost retention. **New Theory of Disuse**: independent **storage strength** (durable) vs. **retrieval strength** (current access); successful effortful retrieval raises storage strength.

### Working Memory Decay (seconds-scale)
A different timescale, likely a different mechanism.
- **Brown–Peterson (1959)**: with rehearsal blocked, recall fell to ~0 by ~18 s — interpreted as decay. But **Keppel & Underwood (1962)** showed it was largely **proactive interference**.
- **Capacity**: Miller's **7±2** (1956); Cowan's revised **~4 chunks**.
- **Models**: Baddeley & Hitch (central executive + phonological loop + sketchpad); **TBRS** (Barrouillet & Camos — decay + attentional refreshing, load-dependent); interference-based models (Oberauer) deny time-decay entirely.
- **Bottom line**: decay is most defensible at the **seconds scale**; long-term, interference + retrieval failure dominate.

## Part B — Cognitive Decline in Aging and Dementia

### Normal Age-Related Decline: What Goes vs. What Stays
**Declines**: **processing speed** (earliest/steepest, from 20s–30s), fluid intelligence (Gf), working memory, episodic memory (esp. free recall, source memory), executive function, prospective memory.
**Preserved**: **crystallized intelligence** (Gc — vocabulary often *rises* into 60s–70s), semantic memory (though naming slows), procedural memory, recognition memory (cueing compensates), emotional regulation (often improves — positivity effect).

**Processing-speed theory** (Salthouse 1996): age-related **slowing mediates most age differences** in other domains — control for processing speed and much of the age variance in memory/reasoning disappears. Recasts much "memory decline" as downstream of slowing. This is the classic **mechanics (decline) vs. pragmatics (maintained)** crossover (Baltes; Cattell–Horn Gf/Gc).

### Trajectory, Variability, Reserve
- **Onset controversy**: Salthouse (cross-sectional) finds decline in the **20s–30s**; Schaie's **Seattle Longitudinal Study** finds reliable decline only **after ~60**. The gap = practice + cohort effects confounding cross-sectional data.
- **Rate**: gradual/linear in midlife, **accelerating after ~70**, steeper after 80.
- **Variability increases with age** — older cohorts far more heterogeneous; "**SuperAgers**" retain youthful memory.
- **Cognitive reserve** (Stern): capacity to *tolerate* pathology before symptoms appear, built by education, occupational complexity, engagement, bilingualism, exercise. Distinguish **brain reserve** (hardware) vs. **cognitive reserve** (software). High-reserve people show symptoms *later* but then decline *faster* (pathology has accumulated behind the reserve).

### The Continuum: SCD → MCI → Dementia
1. **Subjective Cognitive Decline (SCD)** — self-perceived worsening, normal testing; ~2× conversion risk but heterogeneous.
2. **Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI)** — objective impairment beyond age expectation, **function preserved** (Petersen). **Amnestic** (memory-led, higher AD risk) vs. non-amnestic.
3. **Dementia** — decline severe enough to **impair daily independence**.

**Conversion rates**:
- **MCI → dementia: ~10–15%/yr** (clinic), ~5–10%/yr (community); cumulative ~33–50%+ over 5 yrs for amnestic MCI
- **MCI reverts to normal in ~15–20% (up to 30%)** — reversible causes (depression, meds, sleep, thyroid — see [[2026-06-23 — Reversible Dementia Mimics]])
- **Amnestic MCI + amyloid-positive**: often **>50%** conversion over a few years

### Pathological (AD) vs. Normal Aging — the Discriminating Signature
| Feature | Normal aging ("benign senescent forgetfulness," Kral 1962) | Alzheimer's |
|---|---|---|
| **Forgetting type** | *Retrieval* slowing — **cues rescue it** | *Encoding/storage* failure — **cues don't help** |
| **Trajectory** | Gradual, near-linear, plateau-like | Progressive, **accelerating** (a "knee" then steepening) |
| **Episodic memory** | Mild decline, recognition preserved | Severe/early; recognition also fails |
| **Forgetting rate** | Normal | **Accelerated** long-term forgetting (lost over hours/days) |
| **Other domains** | Spared semantic/procedural | Language, visuospatial, executive all erode |
| **Insight** | Retained | Often lost (anosognosia) |

> **The clinical discriminator**: in normal aging a **cue rescues** the memory (retrieval problem); in amnestic AD the memory was never consolidated, so **no cue helps** — operationalized in the **Free and Cued Selective Reminding Test** (Grober & Buschke). This maps to **entorhinal → hippocampal** pathology hit earliest (cf. Ribot's law). Tau **Braak staging**: I–II transentorhinal → III–IV limbic (symptom onset) → V–VI neocortex (severe). See [[2026-06-23 — Alzheimer's Disease]].

### Measurement & The Preclinical Window
- **Cross-sectional overestimates early decline** (cohort effects) and **underestimates late** (selective attrition); **longitudinal suffers practice effects**. Best estimates triangulate (Seattle, Betula studies).
- **Preclinical AD window ~15–20 years** (charted by **DIAN** — autosomal-dominant carriers with known onset):
  - CSF Aβ42 declines **~20–25 yrs** before symptoms
  - Amyloid PET positive **~15 yrs** before
  - Tau / atrophy / hypometabolism **~10–15 yrs** before
  - Subtle episodic-memory decline **~10 yrs** before; **MCI ~2–5 yrs** before diagnosis
- **Jack et al. A/T/N model**: ordered sigmoid cascade — **Aβ → tau → neurodegeneration → memory → global cognition**.
- **Terminal drop** (Kleemeier 1962): accelerated decline in the final ~3–5 years before death, across many older adults regardless of dementia.

## How Part A Illuminates Part B
- **Ribot's law = the AD memory gradient**: recent memories fall first in both, because both implicate hippocampus-dependent, not-yet-consolidated traces.
- **Three loci discriminate aging from AD**: normal aging = retrieval failure (cues rescue); AD = encoding/storage failure (cues don't). Same taxonomy separates benign from pathological.
- **Sleep & consolidation**: disrupted slow-wave sleep impairs consolidation *and* is implicated in amyloid clearance (glymphatic system) — a mechanistic link between everyday forgetting and AD risk.
- **Desirable difficulties → cognitive reserve**: retrieval practice and engagement plausibly build the reserve that delays clinical dementia onset.
