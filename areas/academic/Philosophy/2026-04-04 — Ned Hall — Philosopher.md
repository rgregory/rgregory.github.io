# Ned Hall — Philosopher

Ned Hall is an analytic philosopher at Harvard University, working primarily in metaphysics and the philosophy of science. He is best known for his argument that the concept of causation is **irreducibly dual** — that there are two fundamentally distinct causal concepts which Lewis's counterfactual theory conflates, and that no single unified analysis can capture both.

## Overview

Hall's most influential contribution is the distinction between **causation as counterfactual dependence** and **causation as production** — a distinction that challenges the core of David Lewis's counterfactual analysis of causation and has reshaped the philosophy of causation since the early 2000s.

He has also contributed to the theory of laws of nature, probability, and the metaphysics of science more broadly.

## Key Works

- "Two Concepts of Causation" (2004) — in *Causation and Counterfactuals* (ed. Collins, Hall, Paul) — the canonical statement of the dual-concept view
- "Causation and the Price of Transitivity" (2000) — *Journal of Philosophy* — challenges transitivity of causation
- "Intrinsic" (with Lewisian co-author L. A. Paul, 2001) — on the notion of intrinsic properties
- *Causation and Counterfactuals* (2004, co-edited with John Collins and L. A. Paul) — the central anthology on counterfactual theories of causation
- Work on Humean laws, chance, and the Principal Principle

## Core Arguments

### Two Concepts of Causation

Hall argues that ordinary causal talk and causal reasoning deploy **two distinct concepts**, both called "causation":

**Concept 1 — Dependence causation:**
- C causes E when E counterfactually depends on C
- "If C had not happened, E would not have happened"
- This is Lewis's analysis
- Captures the idea that causes make a difference to their effects

**Concept 2 — Production causation:**
- C causes E when C produces E through a connected causal process
- There is a spatiotemporally continuous chain linking C to E
- Captures the idea that causes bring about their effects through an unbroken mechanism

Hall's argument is that these two concepts come apart in specific cases:

**Preemption cases** (dependence without production):
- Billy throws a rock. Suzy also throws a rock. Suzy's rock hits the bottle first and breaks it. Billy's throw would have broken it if Suzy's hadn't.
- Suzy's throw is the cause by production (the connected process). But E does not counterfactually depend on C — the bottle would have broken even without Suzy's throw (Billy's would have done it).
- So production causation is present, counterfactual dependence is absent.

**Overdetermination and trumping cases** show further divergences.

**Late preemption** cases create further problems for Lewis's analysis that Hall examines in detail, arguing that no refinement of the counterfactual account can handle all cases without importing production-style thinking through the back door.

### Against the Unified Analysis

Lewis's final theory (in "Causation as Influence," 2000) tries to handle problem cases by analyzing causation as a pattern of counterfactual influence across a range of small variations in C. Hall argues this still does not bridge the gap — there are cases where influence varies while the production relation stays stable, and vice versa.

Hall's conclusion: we should **accept the plurality** of causal concepts rather than forcing a unified account. This is philosophically honest but theoretically unsatisfying for those who want a single reductive analysis.

### On Transitivity

Hall also challenges the widely assumed transitivity of causation. In "Causation and the Price of Transitivity" he presents cases where:

- A causes B
- B causes C
- But intuitively A does not cause C

These cases arise with double-prevention (where a prevention of a prevention counts as a cause under the counterfactual analysis). Hall argues that transitivity must be given up, or the analysis generates deeply counterintuitive results.

## Relationship to David Lewis's Work

Hall's career has been substantially shaped by engagement with Lewis:

1. **The counterfactual analysis**: Hall accepts the Lewisian framework of possible worlds and counterfactual dependence as the starting point, but argues it is insufficient on its own. He does not reject Modal Realism but operates within the Lewisian semantic framework.

2. **Philosophical respect**: Hall has described Lewis as the philosopher whose work he engages with most seriously. The tone of "Two Concepts of Causation" is one of internal critique — working within Lewis's programme to show its limits — rather than external rejection.

3. **Lewis's response**: Lewis in his late work ("Causation as Influence," 2000) explicitly attempted to address the cases Hall and others raised. Hall's paper in part is a response to whether Lewis succeeded. His verdict: not quite.

4. **Humean Supervenience**: Hall's work on production causation raises a question for Humean Supervenience. If production causation involves something over and above patterns of counterfactual dependence — if there really are causal processes in the world, not merely counterfactual co-variation — then Humean Supervenience is under pressure. The world may not reduce to local qualitative facts plus patterns; it may require primitive causal structure.

5. **Laws and chance**: Hall has also worked on Lewis's **Best System Analysis** of laws of nature and the **Principal Principle** (Lewis's account of the relationship between objective chance and rational credence). He raises technical and conceptual difficulties for both.

## Key Terms

| Term | Hall's use |
|------|-----------|
| **Dependence causation** | Causation as counterfactual dependence — Lewis's analysis |
| **Production causation** | Causation as a connected process from cause to effect |
| **Preemption** | Cases where a backup cause is present but preempted by the actual cause |
| **Transitivity** | The claim that causal chains are transitive; Hall argues this fails |
| **Influence** | Lewis's refinement: causation as a pattern of influence across variations |
