# Primordial Black Holes

## What They Are

Primordial black holes (PBHs) are black holes formed in the first second after the Big Bang — not from stellar collapse, but from density fluctuations in the early universe itself. They were first proposed by Zel'dovich and Novikov (1966) and independently developed by Hawking (1971).

Their possible mass range is extraordinary: from the Planck mass (~22 micrograms, the smallest physically meaningful object) up to thousands of solar masses. The formation mechanism is direct: in the hot, dense plasma of the early universe, regions where the local density exceeded a critical threshold collapsed gravitationally into black holes before any stars, galaxies, or large-scale structure existed. No stellar evolution required — just general relativity applied to the primordial density field.

This makes PBHs a fundamentally different class of object from astrophysical black holes. They are cosmological fossils: remnants of the structure of the early universe, not of any particular star's death.

## What Problems They Solve

PBHs are scientifically interesting precisely because they sit at the intersection of multiple open problems in physics and cosmology. No other single object addresses so many simultaneously.

### 1. Dark Matter Candidate

The dominant problem in cosmology is that roughly 27% of the universe's energy content is in an unknown form — dark matter — that interacts gravitationally but not electromagnetically. The leading particle candidates (WIMPs, axions) require physics beyond the Standard Model and have not been detected despite decades of experiments.

PBHs in certain mass windows require no new physics whatsoever. They are predictions of general relativity applied to known early-universe conditions. If PBHs formed in the asteroid-mass range (roughly $10^{17}$–$10^{22}$ g, i.e., from the mass of a small asteroid to a dwarf planet) in sufficient abundance, they could constitute all or part of dark matter.

The MACHO and EROS microlensing surveys have ruled out PBHs as the dominant dark matter component in the stellar-mass range (~$0.1$–$100 M_\odot$). But the asteroid-mass window is not ruled out by microlensing (because the lensing signal is too weak to detect at this scale), and remains open as a viable dark matter candidate as of 2025.

### 2. Supermassive Black Hole Seeds

JWST and pre-JWST observations reveal quasars powered by black holes of $10^9$–$10^{10}$ solar masses at redshifts $z > 7$ — less than 700 million years after the Big Bang. The problem: you cannot grow a stellar-mass black hole (a few to a few tens of solar masses) to this size by standard gas accretion in 700 million years. The Eddington limit caps the rate at which a black hole can accrete mass before radiation pressure halts the infalling gas; even at the Eddington limit continuously, growth is exponential but the exponent is too slow.

Massive PBHs born in the early universe — with initial masses of $10^3$–$10^5 M_\odot$ — provide seeds that had a head start. They did not need to form from stars first. This is currently one of the most active areas of PBH research, driven by unexpected JWST observations of massive, bright galaxies at high redshift.

### 3. LIGO/Virgo Mass Gap Events

Stellar evolution theory predicts a "pair-instability supernova gap": for progenitor stars in roughly the 80–130 $M_\odot$ range, pulsational pair-instability supernovae disrupt the star entirely before a black hole can form. The predicted gap in the black hole mass function runs from approximately 50–130 $M_\odot$ — no stellar-collapse black holes should exist here.

Yet LIGO/Virgo have detected gravitational wave events involving black holes in this range. GW190521, the most striking example, involved black holes of ~66 and ~85 $M_\odot$ — squarely in the gap. Primordial origin is the cleanest explanation: PBHs are not subject to stellar evolution constraints and can form at any mass.

### 4. Baryogenesis Connection

The universe contains matter but almost no antimatter, despite the expectation from symmetric initial conditions. This matter-antimatter asymmetry is one of the deepest unsolved problems in physics (Sakharov conditions must be satisfied; the Standard Model does so only weakly).

PBH evaporation via Hawking radiation in the early universe could have produced the observed asymmetry. Evaporating PBHs radiate particles and antiparticles, but CPT-violating effects at high temperature near a Planck-scale object can produce a net excess of baryons. Baumann (2007) and subsequent work have developed concrete models of [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Physics/2026-05-15 — Baryogenesis|baryogenesis via PBH evaporation]], though no model is yet confirmed: as a PBH's mass drops and its Hawking temperature climbs toward GUT energies, it satisfies all three Sakharov conditions simultaneously (baryon number violation, CP violation, and inherently non-equilibrium evaporation).

### 5. Hawking Radiation Laboratory

Hawking radiation — the thermal radiation emitted by black holes due to quantum effects at the event horizon — has never been directly detected. For astrophysical black holes (stellar mass and above), the Hawking temperature is far too low to be observable ($T_H \sim 60$ nK for a solar-mass black hole, compared to the $\sim 2.7$ K CMB).

Small PBHs are different. Hawking temperature scales inversely with mass:

$$T_H = \frac{\hbar c^3}{8\pi G M k_B}$$

A PBH with initial mass $M_0 \sim 5 \times 10^{11}$ kg ($\sim 10^{-18} M_\odot$) would be completing its evaporation today, with its final moments producing a gamma-ray burst detectable in principle by instruments like Fermi-LAT. Detection would be the first direct confirmation of Hawking radiation — and would probe physics at the intersection of quantum mechanics and general relativity. So far, no confirmed detection.

PBHs with mass below $\sim 10^{12}$ kg would have already evaporated; those with mass above $\sim 10^{15}$ g ($10^{12}$ kg) are still evaporating on cosmological timescales.

## Current Status

No PBH has been confirmed as of 2025. The field is active on multiple fronts:

- **JWST** is finding unexpected massive galaxies and black holes at high redshift, keeping the seeding problem live
- **NANOGrav** and other pulsar timing arrays detected a gravitational wave background in 2023; one interpretation is a stochastic background from PBH formation in the early universe (though other explanations are viable)
- **Microlensing surveys** (Subaru HSC, Roman Space Telescope upcoming) are probing the asteroid-mass window with increasing sensitivity
- **Fermi-LAT** constrains the evaporating PBH abundance from the absence of a diffuse gamma-ray background from Hawking radiation

The connection to inflationary cosmology is significant: the density fluctuations that would seed PBHs are predicted by inflationary models. If PBHs exist in the asteroid-mass window, they would be indirect evidence for specific inflationary spectra — linking cosmological observations at the largest scales to Planck-scale physics.
