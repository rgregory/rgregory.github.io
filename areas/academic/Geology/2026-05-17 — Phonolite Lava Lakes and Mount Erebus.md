# Phonolite Lava Lakes and Mount Erebus

## What Is a Phonolite Lava Lake?

A lava lake is a persistent body of molten rock (magma) that sits at or near the surface inside a volcanic crater or pit, remaining liquid over timescales of years to decades. They are windows into the interior of active magmatic systems — rare, scientifically invaluable, and physically extreme.

Most of the world's lava lakes are **basaltic**: low-silica (~45–52 wt% SiO₂), low-viscosity, iron- and magnesium-rich melts that behave like a very hot, slow fluid. Basalt's low viscosity allows gas to escape relatively peacefully, which is why the melt can sit open at the surface without tearing itself apart through explosive degassing.

**Phonolite** is something entirely different. It is a silica-undersaturated (typically 52–60 wt% SiO₂) alkaline volcanic rock dominated by alkali feldspar (specifically anorthoclase and sanidine) and feldspathoid minerals — nepheline being the defining signature. Where basalt is the volcanic equivalent of iron-rich ocean crust, phonolite is chemically evolved, sodium- and potassium-rich, and far less common. The name derives from the ringing sound slabs of the rock produce when struck ("clinker" or "soundstone").

A **phonolite lava lake** — an open, persistent body of phonolitic melt at the surface — is extraordinarily rare. The geochemical conditions required to produce phonolitic magma, sustain it in a near-surface reservoir, and maintain a stable open lake rather than an explosive eruption represent a highly specific and improbable confluence of factors.

## Why Phonolite Lava Lakes Are So Rare

The rarity stems from a fundamental tension in volcanology: phonolite is **not supposed to do this**.

**The viscosity problem.** Silicic/intermediate melts (more silica, more polymerized silicate chains) are more viscous than basalt. Higher viscosity inhibits bubble nucleation and growth — dissolved gases (primarily H₂O, CO₂, SO₂) cannot escape smoothly. The classic result is explosive volcanism: the pressure builds until the magma fragments violently. Phonolite is more viscous than basalt but less viscous than rhyolite — it sits in a middle zone where open lakes are theoretically possible but practically unlikely.

**The degassing problem.** Lava lake stability depends on a balance: magma rises (carrying dissolved volatiles), gases degas at the surface, and cooled, denser, degassed magma sinks. This convective overturn cycle keeps the lake alive. If the melt is too viscous, this convection stalls and the lake crusts over or explodes. If gas content is too high, the surface becomes unstable. Phonolite has relatively high alkali content, which somewhat reduces viscosity compared to pure silicic melts — but the margin for a stable lake is narrow.

**The compositional rarity problem.** Phonolite forms through **extreme fractional crystallization** of a mafic parent melt: as basaltic magma cools, it differentiates, with early-crystallizing minerals (olivine, pyroxene, plagioclase) removing iron, magnesium, and calcium. What remains is progressively enriched in sodium, potassium, and silica. To reach phonolitic composition, a magma system must sustain this differentiation over long timescales in a thermally buffered crustal reservoir — the right tectonic setting, the right heat flux, the right magma supply rate. Intraplate oceanic islands (like those near Antarctica) and rift zones can provide this, but the full journey to phonolite is a geochemical rarity.

## Mount Erebus: The Only Known Persistent Phonolite Lava Lake

### Location and Basics

Mount Erebus sits on **Ross Island**, Antarctica, at approximately 77.5°S, 167.2°E. At 3,794 meters (12,448 feet), it is the highest active volcano in Antarctica and the southernmost active volcano on Earth. The summit hosts the **Inner Crater**, roughly 500 meters wide, within which sits the lava lake — a convecting body of phonolitic melt approximately 40–60 meters across.

Erebus has been continuously active since at least **1972**, when systematic scientific observation began. It is almost certainly older in its persistent activity — historical accounts from the first human expeditions to Antarctica (Shackleton's 1908 Nimrod expedition, which reached the summit) describe active lava in the crater. The volcano itself has been active for roughly 1.3 million years.

### The Volcanic System

Erebus is the surface expression of the **West Antarctic Rift System**, a region of lithospheric extension broadly analogous (in tectonic setting) to the East African Rift. Thinned crust over a hot mantle allows alkaline magmas to rise from depth. The specific phonolitic composition of Erebus's lava lake is thought to arise from long-residence fractional crystallization in a crustal magma reservoir at roughly 5–10 km depth.

### Strombolian Activity and "Erebus Crystals"

The lake is not quiescent. Erebus displays persistent **Strombolian eruptions** — intermittent bursts of gas and lava from the lake surface, occurring roughly every few hours under normal conditions. These eruptions can be violent enough to hurl lava bombs hundreds of meters from the crater rim.

Among the most remarkable features of Erebus eruptive products are **anorthoclase phenocrysts** — large, well-formed crystals of alkali feldspar (anorthoclase is the Na-K feldspar solid solution near the Na end), sometimes exceeding 10 cm in length, found in ejected lava bombs. These "Erebus crystals" are iconic among volcanologists and mineralogists: the slow cooling in the deep magma reservoir allows these crystals to grow to exceptional size before being incorporated into lava bombs and flung from the crater. Their large size and pristine morphology make them valuable petrological samples — each one records the conditions (temperature, pressure, melt composition) of the deep reservoir.

### Infrasound and Seismic Monitoring

Erebus has been intensively monitored since the 1970s (the Mt. Erebus Volcano Observatory, MEVO, has operated there for decades). The Strombolian eruptions generate distinctive **infrasound signatures** — very-low-frequency pressure waves that travel enormous distances through the atmosphere. Long-period seismic events at Erebus have been used to constrain the geometry and dynamics of the plumbing system. The combination of real-time seismic, infrasound, and gas flux monitoring makes Erebus one of the best-instrumented active volcanoes on Earth, despite its extreme location.

## Volcanology of Lava Lake Persistence

A lava lake persists when a dynamic equilibrium is maintained:

1. **Magma supply from depth** replenishes the lake at a rate sufficient to offset surface cooling.
2. **Convective overturn** continuously brings hot, volatile-rich magma to the surface and returns cooled, degassed magma to depth. This is the critical mechanism — a stagnant lake would solidify.
3. **Degassing rate** must be high enough to release accumulated gas pressure without triggering explosive fragmentation.
4. **Thermal balance**: the lake surface loses heat by radiation (at ~1000°C, radiative flux is enormous) and by conduction through the thin crust that forms at the margins. Magma supply must match this heat loss.

The convective overturn in a lava lake is a direct geological instance of **Rayleigh-Bénard convection**: a fluid heated from below and cooled at the surface develops organized convective cells. In the lake, this is visible as slow overturn patterns and the periodic renewal of fresh, incandescent melt at the surface.

At Erebus specifically, the convective cycle is modulated by the Strombolian bursts — each eruption partially disrupts the lake surface, accelerating degassing and resetting the thermal profile before the lake surface re-establishes.

## Phonolite Geochemistry

Phonolite's defining mineralogy:

- **Nepheline** (NaAlSiO₄): a feldspathoid — silica-undersaturated. Its presence means the melt was not saturated in quartz. In fact, quartz and nepheline cannot coexist in equilibrium; if you see nepheline, there is no free silica.
- **Anorthoclase / Alkali feldspar**: (Na,K)AlSi₃O₈ — the dominant framework silicate. Provides the bulk structure of the rock and the iconic "Erebus crystals."
- **Aegirine / aegirine-augite**: sodium-rich pyroxene, common in alkaline rocks.
- **Amphiboles** (e.g., edenite, katophorite): complex hydrous silicates, indicating some water activity in the melt.
- **Accessory minerals**: titanite, apatite, magnetite, and in some Erebus samples, wollastonite.

Major element chemistry (approximate, varies by sample):
- SiO₂: 54–58 wt%
- Al₂O₃: 19–21 wt%
- Na₂O: 7–9 wt% (very high — this is what drives the alkaline character)
- K₂O: 4–5 wt%
- FeO(T): 4–6 wt%
- CaO: 2–4 wt%
- MgO: < 1 wt% (almost entirely differentiated away)

The high Na+K, low Mg, and nepheline-normative composition are the geochemical signature. Phonolite plots in the TAS (Total Alkali vs. Silica) diagram in the upper-left of the silicic field — a position reached only by highly evolved alkaline magmas.

## Scientific Significance

**Window into magma chamber dynamics.** Most volcanic systems are observed only during eruptions — brief, violent events. Erebus provides a continuous, real-time view of an active magmatic system: convection, degassing, crystallization, and melt evolution can be monitored over years. Gas flux measurements (SO₂, CO₂, halogens) from the lake give direct constraints on volatile budgets and degassing pathways.

**Rare petrological constraints.** Because phonolite is so rare as a stable lake composition, Erebus provides unique data for thermodynamic models of alkaline magma differentiation. The anorthoclase crystals are especially valuable — their compositions record the temperature and melt composition at depth during crystal growth, allowing researchers to reconstruct the magma's thermal history.

**Volatile degassing models.** The continuous gas output from Erebus (roughly 1,000–4,000 tonnes/day of SO₂ and CO₂ combined) constrains models of how much volcanic gas planetary atmospheres receive from this type of system. This feeds into both atmospheric chemistry models and paleovolcanic reconstructions.

**Astrobiological analogue.** Extreme environments around lava lakes (high heat, sulfurous gases, ice proximity at Erebus) are studied as analogues for potentially habitable extreme environments on other bodies — particularly volcanic hotspots on icy moons like Europa or Enceladus.

## A Second Phonolite Lava Lake? (Epistemic Caveat)

As of my knowledge cutoff (August 2025), **Mount Erebus remains the only confirmed persistent phonolite lava lake on Earth.** There have been occasional reports and discussions in the volcanological literature about whether other alkaline volcanic systems (notably some in the East African Rift, which also produces evolved alkaline magmas including phonolite) might host or have hosted phonolite-composition lakes, but no second confirmed example had been established.

**Nyiragongo** (Democratic Republic of Congo) is sometimes mentioned in this context — it hosts the world's largest persistent lava lake — but its composition is **melilite nephelinite / carbonatite-influenced** mafic melt, not phonolite. It is alkaline but not phonolitic.

If a second phonolite lava lake has been discovered or confirmed after August 2025, that would be genuinely extraordinary news in volcanology. Any such report should be verified against primary literature (Journal of Volcanology and Geothermal Research, Geophysical Research Letters) before treating it as confirmed.
