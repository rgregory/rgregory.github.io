# Chromhidrosis — Colored Sweat

## What It Is

Chromhidrosis is a rare condition characterized by the secretion of colored sweat. It is distinct from pseudochromhidrosis, where normal sweat becomes colored only after external contact.

## Three Types

### 1. Apocrine Chromhidrosis (true chromhidrosis)

- Apocrine glands (axillae, areolae, face, genitalia) secrete sweat containing lipofuscin — a pigment that accumulates in apocrine secretory cells
- Lipofuscin in higher oxidation states produces darker colors: yellow → green → blue → black
- The specific color depends on the degree of lipofuscin oxidation
- Rare but well-documented; more common in people of African descent
- Treatment: topical capsaicin (depletes substance P → reduces apocrine secretion), botulinum toxin injections, topical retinoids

### 2. Eccrine Chromhidrosis (very rare)

- Colored substances ingested or absorbed enter eccrine sweat
- Examples: rifampicin (orange-red), beta-carotene (yellow-orange), copper (blue-green in Wilson's disease), methylene blue
- Almost always drug- or diet-induced

### 3. Pseudochromhidrosis

- Normal (colorless) sweat becomes colored after contact with chromogenic bacteria (e.g., *Bacillus subtilis* → red/orange), fungi, or dyes on the skin
- Not a glandular disorder

## Lipofuscin — The Underlying Pigment

Lipofuscin is the "wear and tear" pigment — an insoluble polymer of oxidized lipids and proteins that accumulates in post-mitotic cells (neurons, cardiac muscle, apocrine gland cells) as a byproduct of incomplete lysosomal digestion. It is also a marker of cellular aging and oxidative stress. In chromhidrosis, unusually high concentrations build up in apocrine secretory cells.
