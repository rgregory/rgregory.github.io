# Evidentiality: Biology vs. Linguistics

## 1. The Biological Mandate: Direct Evidence
In biology, "evidentiality" is a structural necessity. Biological systems operate on feedback loops where a signal must be physically corroborated by a result.

* **Receptor-Ligand Interaction:** A cell requires the physical binding of a molecule to a receptor. This is the biological equivalent of an eyewitness account.
* **Replication and Proofreading:** DNA polymerase acts as a biological "fact-checker," physically sensing structural distortions to prevent mutations.
* **The Evolutionary Filter:** Natural selection is the ultimate enforcer. If a biological organism "hallucinates" a threat or a resource, the cost is often death.

## 2. The Linguistic Flexibility: Communicative Intent
In linguistics, "evidentiality" refers to how languages encode the source of information. While some languages make this grammatically mandatory, many do not.

* **Arbitrariness of the Sign:** Language allows us to speak about things that don't exist (unicorns) or things that might happen (the future).
* **Social Coordination:** The goal of language isn't always to transmit "proven facts"; it's often to coordinate social behavior or express emotion.
* **Pragmatics vs. Syntax:** We move the burden of proof from the *structure* of the sentence to the *context* of the conversation using "hedges" like *"I think"* or *"Supposedly."*

## 3. Comparison of Constraints

| Feature | Biology | Linguistics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Consequence of Falsehood** | System failure or death. | Social misunderstanding. |
| **Verification Method** | Chemical/Physical binding. | Contextual clues/Reputation. |
| **Primary Goal** | Survival and reproduction. | Information transfer and bonding. |
