# Metacognition — Thinking About Thinking

## What It Is

Metacognition is cognition about cognition — awareness and regulation of one's own mental processes. The term was coined by developmental psychologist John Flavell (1979), who distinguished three components:

- **Metacognitive knowledge**: what you know about how cognition works — your own processes, those of others, and cognitive tasks in general
- **Metacognitive monitoring**: tracking the current state of your own cognitive processes ("Am I understanding this? Am I confident?")
- **Metacognitive control / regulation**: adjusting cognitive strategies based on monitoring ("Slow down, re-read, switch approaches")

## Why It Matters

Metacognition is the difference between knowing something and knowing that you know it — and knowing the limits of what you know.

Poor metacognition produces the Dunning-Kruger effect: incompetent performance combined with an inability to recognize the incompetence, because recognizing incompetence requires the very skills being measured.

Conversely, high metacognitive skill enables calibration: knowing when to trust your intuitions and when to distrust them.

## In Educational Contexts

Metacognitive awareness is among the highest-impact interventions in learning research (Hattie's synthesis). Students who monitor comprehension, identify gaps, and self-quiz outperform those who re-read passively.

Study strategies that feel easier — re-reading, highlighting — are often metacognitively misleading. They produce familiarity, which the learner confuses for understanding.

## The Regress Problem

Who monitors the monitor? Metacognitive processes are themselves cognitive processes that can be flawed. This creates a theoretical regress. In practice, however, first-order metacognition is already rare enough that the regress is not the pressing problem.
