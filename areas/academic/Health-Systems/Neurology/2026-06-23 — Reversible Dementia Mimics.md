# Reversible Dementia Mimics

The treatable conditions that look like dementia but can be partly or fully corrected. **Every standard dementia workup exists primarily to exclude these before committing to an irreversible neurodegenerative diagnosis** ([[2026-06-23 — Alzheimer's Disease|AD]], [[2026-06-23 — Lewy Body Dementia|LBD]], [[2026-06-23 — Vascular Dementia|VaD]]).

## The Clinical Point

The dementia workup is not designed to *confirm* Alzheimer's — it's designed to *catch the reversible mimics*. Standard panel (AAN practice parameter): **CBC, TSH, serum B12, comprehensive metabolic panel** (electrolytes, Ca²⁺, glucose, renal, hepatic) + **structural neuroimaging (MRI preferred)** at least once. Second-tier when indicated: folate, HIV, RPR/treponemal, ESR/CRP, autoimmune/paraneoplastic panel, CSF, EEG.

**How much is actually reversible?** The honest numbers (Clarfield meta-analyses): ~13% "potentially reversible" (1988) → **9% potentially reversible but only ~0.6% fully reversed** (2003, *Arch Intern Med*). So full reversal is *rare* — but the tests are cheap and the cost of missing one is severe. **Yield is highest in younger patients, rapid/atypical course, focal signs, or fluctuation** — exactly why these matter most in early-onset (<65) workups.

## 1. Vitamin B12 Deficiency (Cobalamin)

### Mechanism
B12 is a cofactor for two reactions:
- **Methionine synthase**: homocysteine → methionine → **SAM** (universal methyl donor for myelin, phospholipids, neurotransmitters). Deficiency → impaired methylation → **demyelination** + ↑homocysteine.
- **Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase**: deficiency → **methylmalonic acid (MMA)** buildup → abnormal odd-chain fatty acids in myelin.

> **Key teaching point**: neurologic/neuropsychiatric injury can occur **before and independent of anemia**. Folate supplementation masks the hematologic signs while neurologic damage progresses.

### Causes
Pernicious anemia (autoimmune, anti-intrinsic factor — most common severe cause in elderly); **metformin** (>4 yrs); **PPIs/H2 blockers**; vegan diet (animal-source only; years to deplete); malabsorption (atrophic gastritis, ileal disease/resection, bariatric surgery, nitrous oxide abuse → acute SCD).

### Presentation
- **Cognitive/neuropsychiatric**: memory loss, slowed processing, confusion, depression, psychosis ("megaloblastic madness")
- **Subacute combined degeneration (SCD)**: dorsal column (loss of vibration/proprioception, sensory ataxia, +Romberg) + corticospinal (spastic weakness, hyperreflexia, extensor plantar) signs
- Hematologic: macrocytic anemia, hypersegmented neutrophils, ↑LDH

### Diagnosis
- **Serum B12**: <200 pg/mL deficient; **200–300 pg/mL gray zone** (unreliable — confirm with metabolic markers)
- **MMA + homocysteine** are the sensitive functional markers (rise before serum B12 falls):
  - **B12 deficiency = ↑MMA + ↑Hcy**
  - **Folate deficiency = normal MMA + ↑Hcy** ← the discriminator
- Anti-intrinsic factor / parietal cell antibodies if pernicious anemia suspected

### Treatment & Reversibility
- **Oral 1000–2000 µg/day is equally effective as IM** even in pernicious anemia (~1% absorbed passively, intrinsic-factor-independent). IM (1000 µg) for severe/neurologic cases.
- **Hematologic recovery reliable**; **neurologic/cognitive recovery variable and incomplete** — predicted by **short symptom duration**. <3 months → good recovery; >6–12 months → often residual deficits. **Among the more genuinely reversible mimics if caught early.**

## 2. Hypothyroidism

### Mechanism & Presentation
Thyroid hormone regulates CNS metabolic rate, neurotransmission, myelin. Deficiency slows global cerebral metabolism.
- **Cognitive**: psychomotor slowing, poor concentration, memory complaints — mimics depression or early dementia
- **"Myxedema madness"**: psychosis, paranoia, agitation in severe/long-standing cases
- Systemic: fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, **delayed (hung-up) reflexes**, bradycardia. Extreme: myxedema coma

### Diagnosis
- **Primary hypothyroidism: ↑TSH + ↓free T4** (TSH is the primary screen)
- **Subclinical: ↑TSH (4.5–10) with normal free T4**
- Anti-TPO if Hashimoto's suspected

### Treatment & Reversibility
- **Levothyroxine** titrated to normal TSH; start low (25–50 µg) in elderly/cardiac to avoid arrhythmia; recheck TSH at ~6 weeks
- **Overt hypothyroidism**: cognitive/psychiatric symptoms generally **reverse substantially**
- **Subclinical debate**: treating subclinical hypothyroidism does **NOT** reliably improve cognition — the **TRUST trial** (*NEJM 2017*) found no cognitive/QoL benefit in older adults. So isolated subclinical hypothyroidism is an unreliable explanation for dementia; **overt** disease is the relevant target.

## 3. Normal Pressure Hydrocephalus (NPH)

### The Triad — "Wet, Wobbly, Wacky"
1. **Gait apraxia (wobbly)** — earliest, most characteristic, most shunt-responsive. **"Magnetic gait"**: broad-based, short shuffling steps, feet stuck to floor, en-bloc turning. Lower-body parkinsonian/apractic.
2. **Cognitive impairment (wacky)** — **subcortical/frontal** pattern: psychomotor slowing, executive/attention deficits, apathy, retrieval-type memory loss (cf. cortical amnestic AD)
3. **Urinary incontinence (wet)** — urgency → frank incontinence

Triad often incomplete; **gait is usually first and the cardinal feature**.

### Pathophysiology
**Communicating hydrocephalus** — impaired CSF reabsorption → **ventriculomegaly with normal opening pressure**. Ventricular enlargement stretches periventricular fibers (medial motor fibers to legs + bladder run periventricularly → leg-predominant gait + bladder involvement). Often idiopathic; secondary to prior SAH, meningitis, trauma.

### Diagnosis
- **MRI**: ventriculomegaly **out of proportion to sulcal atrophy**; **Evans index >0.3**; **DESH** (tight high-convexity sulci + dilated Sylvian fissures); transependymal flow
- **Tap test**: remove **30–50 mL CSF**, assess gait before/after — **gait improvement predicts shunt response** (specific, modestly sensitive; negative tap doesn't exclude)
- Extended lumbar drainage (more sensitive); CSF infusion test (outflow resistance)

### Treatment & Reversibility
- **Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt** (programmable valve). Complications: subdural hematoma/hygroma, infection, over-drainage, obstruction
- **Gait responds best/earliest; urinary intermediate; cognition responds least and least predictably** (esp. with comorbid AD/vascular pathology — common in this elderly group)
- **Best candidates**: gait-predominant, short duration, positive tap, classic DESH imaging, minimal vascular disease, MCI not established dementia
- The paradigmatic **surgically reversible dementia** — but durable cognitive reversal is modest; selection is everything

## Other Reversible / Treatable Mimics

- **Depression ("pseudodementia")**: prominent "I don't know" answers, complaints exceeding deficits, abrupt onset. Improves with antidepressants. *Caveat*: late-life depression with cognitive impairment is also a dementia **prodrome/risk marker**.
- **Medication effects** (often the single most common reversible contributor): **anticholinergics** (diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, TCAs — Beers criteria), **benzodiazepines/Z-drugs**, opioids, gabapentinoids, sedating antihistamines, muscle relaxants. Deprescribing is therapeutic.
- **Alcohol-related** — direct neurotoxicity ± thiamine deficiency; partially reversible with abstinence + nutrition
- **Obstructive sleep apnea** — intermittent hypoxia/fragmentation → attention/executive/memory deficits; improves with **CPAP**
- **Metabolic/endocrine**: hypercalcemia ("stones, bones, groans, psychiatric overtones"), hyponatremia, hepatic encephalopathy (↑ammonia), uremia, hypo/hyperglycemia, Cushing's
- **CNS infections**: **neurosyphilis** (general paresis — RPR + CSF VDRL, treat with penicillin), **HIV (HAND** — improves with ART), Lyme neuroborreliosis, chronic meningitis, Whipple
- **Autoimmune/limbic encephalitis** (treatable, often subacute, younger patients):
  - **Anti-NMDA receptor** — young women, ovarian teratoma; psychiatric symptoms, seizures, dyskinesias
  - **Anti-LGI1** — older adults; **faciobrachial dystonic seizures**, hyponatremia, amnesia
  - Treat with immunotherapy (steroids, IVIG, PLEX, rituximab) ± tumor removal. Send panels in rapidly progressive/atypical dementia.
- **Structural**: **chronic subdural hematoma** (elderly, anticoagulated, falls — evacuable); **brain tumor** (frontal meningioma — resectable)
- **Other vitamin deficiencies**:
  - **Thiamine (B1) — Wernicke-Korsakoff**: Wernicke triad = **confusion + ophthalmoplegia + ataxia** (emergency — **thiamine before glucose**); Korsakoff = anterograde + retrograde amnesia with confabulation (often only partially reversible)
  - **Folate** — megaloblastic anemia, ↑Hcy with **normal MMA**; treat B12 first to avoid masking SCD
  - **Niacin (B3) — pellagra**: "3 D's" — dermatitis, diarrhea, dementia

> **Rapidly progressive dementia red flags** (escalate workup — many treatable): subacute weeks–months course, seizures, movement disorder, focal signs, autonomic instability → autoimmune/paraneoplastic, infectious, metabolic (and CJD, which must be distinguished though untreatable).
