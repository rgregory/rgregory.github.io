# Tibetan

Tibetan is a Sino-Tibetan language spoken by approximately 6 million people, primarily in the Tibet Autonomous Region, Qinghai, and neighboring areas of China, as well as diaspora communities in India and Nepal. Lhasa Tibetan (Central Tibetan) is the most studied variety linguistically, though the term covers a wide dialect cluster. Tibetan has an unusually complex grammatical system for marking the relationship between the speaker, the information source, and the epistemic status of a claim — making it one of the most important case studies in evidentiality research.

## Key Facts

- **Elaborate evidential-egophoric interaction**: Tibetan does not separate evidentiality and epistemic modality cleanly. Its system encodes simultaneously (a) whether the speaker directly witnessed an event or is inferring/reporting it, and (b) whether the speaker is the *subject* of the event — the one whose inner experience is at stake. The egophoric distinction is grammaticalized into verb endings: the same event described by its experiencer versus by an outside observer requires different morphology, even in the present tense.
- **First-person direct experience as a distinct grammatical category**: in Tibetan, verbs divide into *voluntary* (actions the speaker controls) and *involuntary* (states and experiences). For involuntary predicates — emotions, sensations, perceptions — the first-person speaker uses a specific egophoric form that is unavailable to any third-party reporter. "I am cold" and "she is cold" require structurally different verb forms not because of person agreement in the standard sense, but because the speaker has privileged, non-inferential access to their own cold.
- **Central case for egophoricity research**: Tibetan (along with Quechua and certain Nakh-Daghestanian languages) is the primary empirical basis for the theoretical concept of egophoricity. Work by DeLancey (1986), Tournadre & LaPolla (2014), and others uses Tibetan data to argue that a new grammatical category — distinct from both person agreement and simple evidentiality — is necessary.

## Connection to Philosophy of Mind

Tibetan's system grammaticalizes a philosophically important asymmetry: the privileged access a subject has to their own mental states versus the inferential access available to any outside observer. This is the same asymmetry at the center of debates about introspection, phenomenal consciousness, and the hard problem of consciousness.
