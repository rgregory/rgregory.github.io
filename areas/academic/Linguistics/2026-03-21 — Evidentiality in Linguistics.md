# Evidentiality in Linguistics

## What Is It?

Evidentiality is a grammatical category that encodes **the source of information** a speaker has for what they are saying. In plain terms: when you make a claim, evidentiality marks *how you know it*.

Some languages make this obligatory — you **must** grammatically specify whether you witnessed something firsthand, heard it from someone else, or inferred it from evidence. English does this loosely through words and phrases ("apparently", "they say", "I saw that..."), but many languages bake it directly into verb morphology — you cannot form a grammatically correct sentence without choosing an evidential marker.

---

## The Core Distinction: Direct vs. Indirect Evidence

Most evidentiality systems start with a two-way contrast:

| Type | Meaning | Example gloss |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Direct / Visual** | Speaker personally witnessed it | "I saw it happen" |
| **Indirect / Reported** | Speaker heard about it, inferred it, or was told | "Apparently it happened" |

More complex systems add finer distinctions:

- **Visual** — seen with one's own eyes
- **Non-visual sensory** — heard, smelled, felt firsthand
- **Inferential** — concluded from physical evidence (tracks in the snow, smoke = fire)
- **Assumptive** — based on general knowledge or reasoning
- **Reportative / Hearsay** — told by another person
- **Quotative** — directly quoting a specific source

---

## Languages with Grammatical Evidentiality

Evidentiality as a **required grammatical system** appears in roughly 25% of the world's languages. Notable examples:

- **Quechua** (Andean South America) — one of the most studied systems; three-way distinction: direct, reported, inferred
- **Turkish** — two-way contrast built into past-tense verb endings: `-di` (witnessed) vs. `-miş` (heard/inferred). This is one of the most accessible examples for English speakers.
- **Bulgarian** — encodes evidentiality through verb forms in the past tense
- **Tibetan** — elaborate evidential system tied to epistemic modality
- **Hopi** (Uto-Aztecan, North America)
- **Cherokee**, **Cree**, **Yukaghir**, and many others

English is **not** a grammatically evidential language, but it lexicalizes the same distinctions: "I saw...", "I heard that...", "apparently...", "it seems like...", "they say..."

---

## Turkish: A Concrete Example

Turkish is a good entry point because the contrast is clean and documented:

- **-di past** (witnessed): *Ahmet geldi* — "Ahmet came" (I saw him come)
- **-miş past** (non-witnessed): *Ahmet gelmiş* — "Ahmet came" (I heard / it seems / apparently he came)

The second form is also used for:
- Things that happened while the speaker was asleep or absent
- Fairy tales and folklore (convention: the speaker didn't witness these events)
- Sarcasm and distancing in modern Turkish usage

---

## Why It Matters

**1. Epistemology embedded in grammar**
Evidentiality forces speakers to constantly mark the reliability of their own claims. Languages with obligatory evidentiality make speakers more explicit about the limits of their knowledge — a built-in epistemic hygiene.

**2. Cross-linguistic relativity**
Does speaking a language with obligatory evidentiality affect how you think about knowledge and certainty? This is an active research question in language-and-cognition studies (see Sapir-Whorf / linguistic relativity debates).

**3. Legal and journalistic relevance**
Evidential distinctions matter enormously in testimony, journalism, and reporting: "I saw X" vs. "I was told X" vs. "I inferred X" are very different epistemic claims — but English grammar doesn't force you to distinguish them.

**4. Translation problems**
Translating from an evidential language into English (or vice versa) requires decisions: what to do with information-source distinctions that English has no grammatical slot for?

---

## Evidentiality vs. Related Concepts

| Concept | What it encodes |
|---------|----------------|
| **Evidentiality** | Source of information (how you know) |
| **Epistemic modality** | Degree of certainty (how sure you are): "must be", "might be" |
| **Mirativity** | Whether information is new/surprising to the speaker |

These often overlap and interact — many languages bundle evidentiality with epistemic modality into a single system, which is why the terms are sometimes confused.

## Quick Summary

- Evidentiality = grammatical marking of **how you know something**
- Ranges from lexical (English: "apparently") to fully grammatical (Turkish, Quechua, Tibetan)
- Core types: direct/witnessed, inferred, reported/hearsay
- Touches on epistemology, cognitive science, and translation theory
- About 25% of world's languages have grammaticalized evidentiality
