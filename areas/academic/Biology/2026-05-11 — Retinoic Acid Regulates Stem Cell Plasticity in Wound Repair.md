# Retinoic Acid Regulates Stem Cell Plasticity in Wound Repair

A 2024 Rockefeller University study published in *Science* (vol. 383, no. 6687) establishes vitamin A — specifically its active metabolite retinoic acid — as a master regulator of stem cell plasticity during wound healing. The finding reframes vitamin A from a nutritional necessity into a molecular rheostat that controls how far stem cells are willing to deviate from their committed identity.

## The Core Discovery

Hair follicle stem cells are ordinarily locked into producing follicle tissue. When skin is wounded, these cells temporarily abandon that commitment: they enter a state of **lineage plasticity**, becoming capable of generating epidermal (surface skin) cells instead. This identity flexibility is essential for wound closure.

The Rockefeller team demonstrated that **retinoic acid governs the exit from plasticity** — it acts as a signal that tells stem cells when the crisis is over and it is time to return to their original identity. In the absence of retinoic acid signaling, stem cells linger in the plastic state; in its presence, they consolidate back into follicle-producing cells.

The researchers describe this as a **rheostat**, not a binary switch: the level of retinoic acid activity modulates the *degree* of plasticity, not merely its presence or absence.

## Why This Is Significant

Prior to this work, the factors controlling the re-commitment of transdifferentiated or plastic stem cells were poorly understood. Most research focused on what *triggers* plasticity; this study focuses on what *resolves* it. That mechanistic gap had direct consequences for regenerative medicine: if you want to harness stem cell flexibility therapeutically, you need to know how to turn it off as well as on.
