# Measuring Well-Being and Progress

**Authors**: Joseph Stiglitz, Amartya Sen, Jean-Paul Fitoussi (OECD Commission)  
**Year**: 2009+ (influential through 2016–2026)  
**Citations**: ~2,400+  
**Venue**: Oxford University Press

## Key Contribution

Proposes moving beyond GDP to multidimensional well-being measures: health, education, environmental quality, inequality, democratic participation. Argues GDP maximization ignores distribution and sustainability. Framework for national accounting beyond aggregate income.

## Intellectual Impact

Foundational for "beyond GDP" movement; influenced OECD, UN, and national statistics agencies. Cross-domain: cited in philosophy, environmental science, economics, policy. Informed sustainable development goals (SDGs) and well-being policy agendas.

### Philosophy & Epistemology

- **[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/|Amartya Sen's Capabilities Approach]]** — philosophical foundations of well-being; what people can do and be (not just what they consume)
- **[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-18 — Statistics as Philosophy|Statistics as Philosophy]]** — the fundamental epistemological question: what counts as progress? Who decides? How do we measure what matters?
- **[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-17 — Jean-François Lyotard — The Postmodern Condition|Lyotard on legitimation]]** — the loss of grand narratives about progress; whose well-being narrative gets privileged?
- **Justice and Distribution** — Rawlsian distributive justice; measuring not just aggregate well-being but fairness of distribution
- **Incommensurability and Measurement** — can health, education, environmental quality, and democratic participation be meaningfully compared in a single metric? Lyotard's concern about reducing incommensurable values to a common measure

### Methodological Foundations

- **Multidimensional Measurement** — how do you weight different dimensions? This is not a technical problem but a philosophical and political one
- **Reflexivity** — the act of measuring well-being changes what well-being means (similar to Goodhart's Law and measurement problems in economics)
