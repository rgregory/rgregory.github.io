# Apposition Eyes

The **apposition eye** is the dominant optical design in diurnal (day-active) insects — flies, bees, dragonflies, locusts. Each [[2026-04-04 — Ommatidia Units of the Compound Eye|ommatidium]] operates independently: pigment cells optically isolate it from its neighbours, so each unit samples only a narrow angular cone of the visual field directly in front of it.

The image is a mosaic — each ommatidium contributes one "pixel" to a representation assembled by the nervous system. Resolution is limited by the number and angular spacing of ommatidia (inter-ommatidial angle).

---

## Key Properties

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Light condition | Bright (diurnal) |
| Resolution | Higher (each unit = one pixel, many units) |
| Sensitivity | Lower (narrow acceptance angle per unit) |
| Temporal resolution | Very high — flies ~250 Hz |
| Pigment cell state | Fixed/closed — blocks lateral spread |

---

## Neural Superposition (flies)

In higher Diptera (*Drosophila*, housefly), a refinement called **neural superposition** boosts sensitivity without sacrificing resolution. Six ommatidia that view the same point in space (from slightly different positions) send their axons to the same cartridge in the lamina (first optic neuropil). The signals are summed neurally rather than optically, improving signal-to-noise while maintaining single-ommatidium resolution.
