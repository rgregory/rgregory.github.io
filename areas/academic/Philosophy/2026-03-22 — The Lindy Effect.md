# The Lindy Effect

The Lindy Effect: the future life expectancy of non-perishable things is proportional to their current age. A book in print for 100 years will likely remain in print for another 100. A technology existing for 50 years has a longer expected future than one that appeared last year.

## The Logic

For perishable things (humans, fruit, machines), age is a liability — they wear out. For non-perishable things (ideas, technologies, institutions, artistic works), survival is evidence of robustness. Every day something survives is a selection event. Things that have survived longest have been tested against the widest range of conditions. Survival is the signal.

## Implications

- Old books, tools, and institutions carry more demonstrated survival information than new ones — regardless of whether they seem outdated
- The newest technology or framework has passed almost no selection tests; the oldest have passed many
- Applied to advice: a 2,000-year-old piece of practical wisdom (Stoicism, Seneca) has survived more selection pressure than last year's business bestseller
- Applied to risk: fragile systems are those whose expected future life decreases with time; robust systems maintain or increase their expected future

## The Caveat

Lindy applies to *classes* of things, not individual instances. Any specific old book might go out of print tomorrow. The effect is about base rates and selection pressure, not guarantees.

## Origin

Popularised by Nassim Nicholas Taleb in *Antifragile* (2012), though the concept originates with Albert Goldman (1964) writing about Broadway shows.
