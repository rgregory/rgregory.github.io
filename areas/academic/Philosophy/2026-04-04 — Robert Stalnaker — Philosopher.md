# Robert Stalnaker — Philosopher

Robert Stalnaker is an analytic philosopher at MIT, working in metaphysics, philosophy of language, and philosophical logic. He is one of the founders of possible-worlds semantics for conditionals, and one of the most important critics of David Lewis's Modal Realism. His alternative view — that possible worlds exist as **abstract objects** rather than as concrete universes — is the leading form of ersatz modalism.

## Overview

Stalnaker's intellectual relationship with Lewis is one of the most productive philosophical dialogues of the late twentieth century. They share foundational commitments: both take possible worlds seriously as theoretical entities, both use them to analyze conditionals and modality, both are systematic philosophers of the first rank. But they disagree fundamentally about the **nature** of those worlds.

Lewis himself, in the preface to *On the Plurality of Worlds*, footnotes Stalnaker's label "extreme modal realism" for Lewis's own view — suggesting that Stalnaker's position was central enough to Lewis's self-understanding that he organized his book partly as a response to it.

## Key Works

- "A Theory of Conditionals" (1968) — *Studies in Logical Theory* — one of the two founding papers (alongside Lewis's "Counterfactuals") of possible-worlds semantics for conditionals
- *Inquiry* (1984) — book-length treatment of propositions, mental content, belief, and assertion
- *Our Knowledge of the Internal World* (2008) — epistemology and the internal/external distinction
- *Context and Content* (1999) — essays on context-dependence in language and thought
- "Possible Worlds" (1976) — *Noûs* — the canonical statement of ersatz modal realism
- "On What There Is" — engagement with Lewis on ontological commitment

## Core Arguments

### Ersatz Modal Realism — Worlds as Abstract Objects

Stalnaker holds that possible worlds exist but are **abstract objects** — specifically, **ways the world might have been**, construed as maximal properties or states of affairs, not as concrete, spatiotemporally extended universes.

On his view:
- There is exactly one concrete world — the actual world
- Possible worlds are abstract representations of how things could have been
- To say that P is possible is to say that some abstract world-object represents P as being the case
- To say that P is necessary is to say that all abstract world-objects represent P as being the case

This approach gives the semantic benefits of possible-worlds talk (systematic treatment of modality, conditionals, propositional attitudes) without Lewis's ontological extravagance — without committing to infinitely many concrete universes as real as our own.

Lewis, in Chapter 3 of *On the Plurality of Worlds*, classifies this as a form of **Magical Ersatzism**: Stalnaker's abstract worlds "represent" by some primitive, unexplained relation. They select one possibility over others — the actual world is distinguished not by its nature but by the fact that it, and not the abstract objects, is the one concrete reality. Lewis's objection: the representation relation is doing all the work and remains unanalyzed.

### Stalnaker's Reply to Lewis

Stalnaker argues that Lewis's critique misfires. The Lewisian demand for a non-primitive representation relation sets the bar too high. All theories must have primitives somewhere; the question is whether the primitives are theoretically tractable and well-motivated. Stalnaker holds that the notion of a world-state as an abstract object is no more mysterious than Lewis's notion of a concrete possible universe — and is far more parsimonious.

Moreover, Stalnaker argues that Lewis's Modal Realism faces its own unexplained primitive: the notion of **spatiotemporal isolation** that individuates worlds. What makes two things belong to different worlds rather than one very large world? Lewis must invoke a primitive of isolation that is no less mysterious than Stalnaker's representation relation.

### Conditionals

Stalnaker's 1968 paper, developed simultaneously and independently of Lewis's parallel work, provides a possible-worlds semantics for conditionals:

> "If it were the case that A, then it would be the case that C" is true iff at the closest possible world where A is true, C is also true.

Both Lewis and Stalnaker analyze conditionals via world-similarity, but differ on one key point:

- **Lewis**: for any two worlds W1 and W2, there may be no unique "closest" world — there can be ties in similarity, and Lewis allows for this by evaluating conditionals against a *set* of closest worlds
- **Stalnaker**: posits a **selection function** that picks a unique closest world for each antecedent and base world, using a **Limit Assumption** and a **Uniqueness Assumption**

This leads to a logical difference: on Stalnaker's semantics, the law of **conditional excluded middle** (CEM) is valid — for any A and C, either "if A then C" or "if A then not-C" must be true. On Lewis's semantics, CEM fails.

The debate about CEM continues in philosophy of language. Lewis rejected it; Stalnaker defended it; much subsequent work has been devoted to finding linguistic data that adjudicates between them.

### Propositions and Mental Content

In *Inquiry* (1984), Stalnaker develops a view of propositions as sets of possible worlds (functions from worlds to truth values). This allows a unified treatment of:
- The content of beliefs and desires (sets of possible worlds the agent would be happy with)
- The content of assertions (sets of possible worlds compatible with what the speaker represents as known)
- The context of conversation (a **context set** of worlds consistent with the common ground)

This **two-dimensional** approach to content is developed in opposition to both purely syntax-based views and purely causal theories of content. Stalnaker's view maintains that the internal/external distinction matters for how we characterize mental states.

## Relationship to David Lewis's Work

The Lewis-Stalnaker relationship is philosophically central:

1. **Conditionals**: They independently discovered the same possible-worlds framework for conditionals in the late 1960s. The differences are subtle but philosophically important (CEM, the Limit Assumption, the role of a selection function vs. a similarity ordering).

2. **Modal Realism vs. Ersatzism**: Stalnaker coined the term "extreme modal realism" for Lewis's position. Lewis devoted Chapter 3 of *OPW* to arguing that Stalnaker's ersatz programme, and programmes like it, cannot achieve genuine reduction of modal notions.

3. **Acknowledgment**: Lewis lists Stalnaker among those he is "greatly indebted to" in the preface to *OPW*, alongside Armstrong, Kripke, and others — a sign that the dialogue was genuine, not merely polemical.

4. **The core disagreement**: Lewis accepts the ontological cost (infinitely many concrete worlds) in exchange for a reductive analysis of modality — no primitive modal notions. Stalnaker pays less ontologically (only abstract objects) but accepts a primitive representation relation and does not claim a full reduction of modality. Each philosopher accepts a different kind of primitiveness.

5. **Shared framework**: Despite their disagreement, both operate within the same theoretical architecture — worlds, accessibility, similarity — and both are committed to systematic semantics for modal and conditional language. This makes their debate a precise internal dispute, not a clash of incommensurable frameworks.

## Key Terms

| Term | Stalnaker's use |
|------|----------------|
| **Extreme modal realism** | Stalnaker's label for Lewis's view — concrete, causally isolated possible worlds |
| **Ersatz worlds** | Possible worlds as abstract objects (world-states, maximal properties) rather than concrete universes |
| **Selection function** | A function picking the unique closest world to a given world relative to a given antecedent |
| **Conditional excluded middle** | For any A, C: either "if A, C" or "if A, not-C" — valid on Stalnaker's semantics, invalid on Lewis's |
| **Context set** | The set of worlds compatible with the conversational common ground at a given point in discourse |
| **Two-dimensionalism** | The view that content can be analyzed along two dimensions: what is said (primary intension) and how it is said (secondary intension) |
