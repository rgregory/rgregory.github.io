# Pilobolus — The Fungal Cannon

Pilobolus (from Greek: *pilos* = hat, *bolus* = throw) is a genus of zygomycete fungi that grows on the dung of herbivores, famous for one of the most sophisticated spore-dispersal mechanisms in biology.

---

## Life Cycle Context

Pilobolus is **coprophilous** — lives and fruits on herbivore dung (horse, cow, rabbit). Spores must complete a specific journey:
1. Land on grass **away** from the dung pile
2. Be eaten by a grazing herbivore
3. Survive gut passage
4. Germinate in fresh dung
5. Repeat

Problem: herbivores won't graze near their own dung. Spores must travel outward and upward, landing on clean grass at a distance.

---

## The Sporangiophore — A Precision Cannon

Structure:
- Transparent, fluid-filled stalk topped by a black **sporangium** (spore sac)
- Below the sporangium: a **subsporangial vesicle** — a swollen, pressurized bulb
- **Phototrophic** — tracks sunlight, orienting toward the light source precisely
- Base contains **β-carotene** acting as a lens to focus light and direct growth toward open pasture, away from the shaded dung pile

---

## The Launch

Turgor pressure in the subsporangial vesicle builds to ~**7 atmospheres** (~700 kPa), then ruptures:

- **Acceleration**: ~20,000 g (among the highest in biology)
- **Velocity**: ~25 km/h at launch
- **Range**: up to **2.5 meters**
- **Trajectory**: nearly horizontal, aimed at the light source
- Sporangium launched as a unit, held by a sticky pad that adheres to grass blades

---

## Why It's Remarkable

**1. The fungus built a lens**
The subsporangial vesicle acts as a **converging lens**, focusing incoming light onto the photosensitive stalk base — a passive, geometry-based targeting system using the physics of refraction. No nervous system required.

**2. Pressure as stored energy**
7 atm = pressure inside a car tire. Built hydraulically via osmotic water uptake — slow accumulation, instantaneous release. A biological capacitor.

**3. Optimized trajectory**
Phototropic orientation fires toward open sky and open pasture — not back toward the pile, not into shade.

**4. Whole sporangium travels**
Unlike fungi that launch individual spores, Pilobolus launches the entire spore packet as a coherent unit in sticky matrix — keeps spores together and aids adhesion.

---

## The 20,000 g Problem

For comparison:
- Fighter pilot max: ~9 g
- Mantis shrimp strike: ~10,000 g
- Pilobolus launch: ~20,000 g

The sporangium survives because the spores inside are dormant and protected. The structure destroyed (the vesicle) is the one that doesn't need to survive.

---

## Broader Significance

**Veterinary:** Pilobolus spores are a vector for **Dictyocaulus** lungworm larvae in horses and cattle. Larvae climb the sporangiophore and hitch a ride on the launched sporangium — a parasite riding a fungal dispersal system.

**Biomimetics:** Hydraulic pressure accumulation + phototrophic aiming + ballistic release — studied for micro-scale projectile systems and soft robotics.

**Philosophy of function:** No nervous system, no muscles, no eyes — yet Pilobolus tracks light, aims, builds pressure, and fires a ballistic projectile to a precise target. Sophisticated behavior emerging from local physics, chemistry, and geometry.

---

## Core Insight

Pilobolus solved an optimization problem — *how do I get my spores eaten by the right animal from the wrong starting location?* — using osmotic pressure, a refractive lens, and phototropism. No nervous system. No muscles. Pure physics executed in biological material.
