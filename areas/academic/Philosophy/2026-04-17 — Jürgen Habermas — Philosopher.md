# Jürgen Habermas — Philosopher

German philosopher and sociologist (b. 1929). Second-generation Frankfurt School. The most systematic defender of Enlightenment rationality in the postmodern era. Central project: rehabilitating reason — not as the monological reason of the subject, but as **communicative rationality** embedded in social practice.

---

## Core Framework: Communicative Action

Habermas distinguishes two rationality types:

| Type | Logic | Domain |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic/Instrumental** | Means-ends efficiency | Market, administration, technology |
| **Communicative** | Oriented toward mutual understanding | Lifeworld, discourse, democracy |

The modern crisis: **system** (economy + state, governed by money and power) **colonizes the lifeworld** (the pre-theoretical background of shared meanings, norms, and communicative practices). Pathology results when strategic rationality displaces communicative rationality in domains that require it.

---

## Discourse Ethics

Moral norms are valid if and only if all affected parties could agree to them under ideal discourse conditions. This gives a **procedural** rather than substantive ethics — legitimacy derives from the process of deliberation, not from any fixed content.

**Ideal Speech Situation**: hypothetical conditions under which discourse is free from domination, power asymmetries, and strategic distortion. Real discourse approximates this ideal to varying degrees.

---

## Theory of Communicative Action (1981)

Against Weber's diagnosis of modernity as iron cage:
- Rationalization is not inherently dominating
- The lifeworld has its own rationality (communicative), which can resist system colonization
- Democracy requires institutionalizing communicative rationality — deliberative democracy as the political form

---

## Habermas vs. Postmodernists

Key dispute with Foucault, Lyotard, Derrida:
- Habermas: postmodernism abandons emancipatory reason; it offers critique without normative foundation — you cannot argue that power is bad without implicitly appealing to standards that transcend power
- Foucault: reason itself is a form of power; there is no neutral standpoint
- Lyotard: the ideal of consensus suppresses dissent; difference (paralogy) not consensus should be the political goal

This debate is the defining controversy of late 20th-century political philosophy.
