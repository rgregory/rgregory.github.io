# Martin Heidegger — Philosopher

Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) is one of the most influential and controversial philosophers of the twentieth century. His influence extends across existentialism, hermeneutics, phenomenology, post-structuralism, and environmental philosophy. He is also one of the most politically compromised: his membership in the Nazi Party and his failure to adequately reckon with it remain a permanent stain on his legacy and an open philosophical question about the relationship between his thought and his politics.

Heidegger's central project, sustained across a lifetime of writing, is the "Question of Being" (die Seinsfrage): what does it mean for something to be? He argues that Western philosophy since the pre-Socratics has forgotten this question — it has asked about beings (what things are) but not about Being (the fact and manner that things are). The task of philosophy is to recover the question that has been covered over.

## Being and Time (1927)

*Sein und Zeit* is Heidegger's masterwork. It sets out to analyze the Being of Dasein — Heidegger's term for human existence — as the starting point for a fundamental ontology.

### Dasein
**Dasein** (literally "being-there") is the entity for whom Being is a question, the entity that has an understanding of Being even if it cannot articulate it explicitly. Dasein is not a subject (in the Cartesian sense of an inner, thinking thing) but a being-in-the-world: essentially, irreducibly embedded in a world of practical involvements, other Dasein, historical traditions, and existential possibilities.

The hyphenation is important: "being-in-the-world" is not three separate things (being + in + world) but a single, unitary structure. There is no Dasein outside a world; there is no world except as the world of Dasein. The Cartesian picture of an inner subject reaching out to an external world is a secondary, derivative construction — possible only because Dasein is always already out there, engaged with things.

### Thrownness and Projection
Dasein always finds itself already in a situation it did not choose: born into a particular historical period, language, social class, body, and set of possibilities. This is **thrownness** (Geworfenheit) — the facticity of existence, the "that-it-is" that Dasein cannot escape or choose away.

But Dasein is also **projection** (Entwurf): it is always oriented toward possibilities, always "ahead of itself" in its understanding. Dasein exists as care (Sorge): ahead of itself (projection), already in a world (thrownness), alongside things and other Dasein. Existence is always this temporal, tensed structure.

### The Ready-to-Hand and the Present-at-Hand
In Dasein's practical engagement with the world, things show up primarily as **ready-to-hand** (Zuhandenheit): equipment for purposes, gear transparent in use. The hammer is not an object I observe; it is an extension of my purposive activity, invisible in normal use. It becomes a **present-at-hand** (Vorhandenheit) object — an item with properties that can be described theoretically — only when it breaks down, becomes too heavy, or is missing.

This analysis is Heidegger's anti-Cartesian move: theoretical cognition (the scientific gaze on objects as present-at-hand) is not the primary mode of encountering reality. It is derivative from practical, pre-theoretical engagement. This was enormously influential on Kuhn (the paradigm as transparent-in-use background), Rorty (coping rather than knowing), and Dewey (transaction over representation).

### Temporality and Authenticity
Dasein's Being is fundamentally **temporal**: it is always stretched between its thrown past, its current situation, and its projected future possibilities. The most extreme and ownmost possibility — the one that cannot be transferred or avoided — is **death**. Dasein is "being-toward-death."

Most of the time, Dasein falls into **inauthenticity**: it loses itself in the "das Man" (the "they-self"), the anonymous public interpretation of existence in which no one in particular is responsible and everything is leveled to the average and mediocre. Authentic existence involves owning one's thrownness and mortality — resolving to take up one's ownmost possibilities in the face of death, rather than fleeing into comfortable anonymity.

**Conscience** and **anxiety** are the calls to authenticity: anxiety reveals the groundlessness of Dasein's existence (there is no ultimate ground, no God-given essence, no nature to fall back on); conscience calls Dasein back from the they-self to its own possibilities.

## The Turn (Kehre)

After *Being and Time* (which remained unfinished — only the first two divisions of the projected three were published), Heidegger underwent a significant shift — the **Kehre** — away from the analysis of Dasein and toward a direct meditation on Being itself, language, poetry, and the history of Being as metaphysics.

In the later work, the priority reverses: it is not Dasein that projects a world; it is Being that discloses itself through Dasein, which is the "clearing" (Lichtung) or "shepherd of Being." Language is the "house of Being" — not a tool Dasein uses to communicate, but the medium in which Being discloses itself and which is prior to any human activity.

The later Heidegger meditates on **the history of Being as metaphysics**: from the pre-Socratic poets who stood open to Being, through Plato's reduction of Being to ideas, through Descartes' reduction of beings to res extensa and res cogitans, through Nietzsche's will to power (the last metaphysics), to the modern **Enframing** of technology.

## Technology and Enframing

The **Enframing** (Gestell) is Heidegger's diagnosis of modernity. Modern technology is not merely a collection of instruments; it is a way of disclosing the world, a mode of revealing in which everything shows up as **standing-reserve** (Bestand) — resources to be calculated, optimized, and mobilized for human ends. The Rhine is not a river but a hydroelectric resource. A forest is not a dwelling but a timber reserve. Even human beings become human resources.

The danger of Enframing is not that machines are bad but that this mode of revealing crowds out every other mode. When everything shows up as standing-reserve, the possibility of other disclosures — poetic, meditative, questioning — is suppressed. The saving power (*das Rettende*) lies, Heidegger notoriously suggests, in art and poetry: the capacity to let beings show up in their Being, rather than reducing them to utility.

## Language as House of Being

In the later work, language is not an instrument for expressing pre-formed thoughts but the condition of possibility for any world-disclosure at all. "Language speaks, not man." It is in language — particularly in the language of genuine poetry (Hölderlin above all) — that Being discloses itself. Philosophy must listen to language rather than mastering it.

This claim influences Foucault (discourse as constitutive of its objects), Rorty (vocabularies as coping tools), Lyotard (language games as irreducible), and Derrida (deconstruction as close reading of what language does).

## Key Works

- **Being and Time** (1927) — The fundamental ontology; Dasein, care, temporality, authenticity, being-in-the-world.
- **What is Metaphysics?** (1929) — The famous analysis of anxiety and nothingness.
- **Introduction to Metaphysics** (1935, published 1953) — The question "Why is there something rather than nothing?"
- **The Question Concerning Technology** (1954) — Enframing, standing-reserve, and the essence of technology.
- **Poetry, Language, Thought** (1971) — Essays on dwelling, building, the fourfold, and Hölderlin.
- **Being and Time** remains unfinished; the later work is largely composed of lectures and essays.

## In Relation To

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Richard Rorty — Philosopher|Richard Rorty]]** — Rorty reads Heidegger as a fellow anti-essentialist and anti-foundationalist: Heidegger's critique of the metaphysics of presence — the tradition's tendency to ground Being in a foundational presence (God, substances, the cogito, the will to power) — is congenial to Rorty's anti-foundationalism. But Rorty rejects the mystical register of the late Heidegger (the meditative waiting for a new god), his political catastrophe, and his claim that his thought discloses something genuinely deep about Being rather than offering a useful redescription. Rorty wants to de-mystify Heidegger: to take the anti-essentialist insights and leave behind the quasi-religious framework.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Michel Foucault — Philosopher|Michel Foucault]]** — Foucault inherits from Heidegger the historicizing of the conditions of knowledge: just as Heidegger argues that Being is always disclosed within a historical horizon (the history of Being as metaphysics), Foucault argues that truth is always produced within a historical episteme. Foucault's "historical a priori" is a secularized, sociologized version of Heidegger's historical disclosure of Being. But Foucault replaces Heidegger's ontological framework with an analysis of concrete institutional and discursive practices — power rather than Being.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — John Dewey — Philosopher|John Dewey]]** — The Heidegger-Dewey comparison is one of the most productive and underexplored in twentieth-century philosophy. Both reject the Cartesian subject-object picture; both insist on the primacy of practical engagement over theoretical contemplation; both see the breakdown of absorbed coping (Dewey's "problematic situation," Heidegger's broken tool) as the origin of explicit, reflective inquiry. The differences: Dewey's account is naturalistic, democratic, and socially reconstructive; Heidegger's is ontological, politically catastrophic (Nazism), and nostalgic for a pre-modern relation to Being. Rorty's preference for Dewey over Heidegger is precisely here: Dewey offers the anti-Cartesian insights without the authoritarian politics and mysticism.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Thomas Kuhn — Philosopher of Science|Thomas Kuhn]]** — Kuhn's paradigm as a transparent background of practices that constitutes what can be seen, and that becomes visible only in crisis, is deeply consonant with Heidegger's analysis of the ready-to-hand. The normal scientist working within a paradigm is in the mode of absorbed coping; the scientist in revolutionary crisis is forced into the present-at-hand mode of explicit examination. This connection was noted by Hubert Dreyfus among others.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Jean-François Lyotard — Philosopher|Jean-François Lyotard]]** — Heidegger's critique of technology as Enframing is the direct ancestor of Lyotard's critique of performativity as the dominant legitimating norm of contemporary knowledge. Both argue that modern reason in its technological-economic form reduces everything to calculable resources and suppresses other modes of disclosure. Lyotard's "paralogy" — the disruptive, uncanny move that exceeds the system — resonates with Heidegger's emphasis on poetry and meditative thinking as saving powers.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Modal Realism — David Lewis|Modal Realism — David Lewis]]** — Lewis's modal realism is in a sense the opposite of Heidegger's fundamental ontology: Lewis gives a flat, non-hierarchical metaphysics of concrete possible worlds in which "existence" means the same thing for everything that exists; Heidegger's ontological difference between Being and beings insists that existence is not a predicate, not a univocal concept applicable across all entities. Lewis represents the analytic tradition's confidence in systematic metaphysics; Heidegger represents the continental tradition's suspicion of it.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Being and Event — Alain Badiou|Being and Event — Alain Badiou]]** — Badiou engages with Heidegger most directly through the question of the event: for Heidegger, the primordial disclosure of Being by the pre-Socratics was an event of unconcealment (aletheia) that has been progressively covered over by the metaphysical tradition. Badiou's events are secular and mathematical rather than poetico-ontological, but the structure — a rupture that cannot be recognized from within the existing situation — is parallel. Badiou explicitly rejects Heidegger's nostalgia for an originary disclosure.
