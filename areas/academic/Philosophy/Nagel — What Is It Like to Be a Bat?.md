# Nagel — What Is It Like to Be a Bat? (1974)

## Overview

Thomas Nagel's 1974 paper argues that **qualia — the subjective character of conscious experience — are epistemically privileged and structurally inaccessible to third-person scientific description.** No matter how complete a physical, functional, or behavioral account of an organism we obtain, something essential is left out: what it is *like*, from the inside, to be that organism.

**Why the bat?** Bats navigate via echolocation, a sensory modality so phenomenologically alien to humans that we cannot extrapolate from our own experience to theirs. We can know everything about bat neurophysiology, sonar mechanics, and behavior, and still not know what it is *like* to be a bat. The bat is a forcing example: it demonstrates that subjective character is not merely *unknown* but *structurally unknowable* from the third person.

## Key Claims

1. **Consciousness has an intrinsic subjective character.** "An organism has conscious mental states if and only if there is something it is like to be that organism — something it is like *for* the organism." This *for-ness* is not optional decoration on top of physical facts; it is constitutive of mentality itself.

2. **Reductionist physicalism fails.** Physical descriptions are necessarily third-person and objective. Subjective character is irreducibly first-person. Any reduction that translates "what it is like" into objective vocabulary either changes the subject or quietly smuggles the subjective back in.

3. **The explanatory gap.** Even granting that mental states *are* physical states, we lack any account of *why* a given physical configuration gives rise to a given phenomenal character — or any phenomenal character at all. This is the seed of what Chalmers (1995) later named the **hard problem of consciousness**.

## Philosophical Stakes

- **Challenge to materialism:** Nagel does not argue materialism is false; he argues we lack the conceptual resources to see how it could be true. Subjective and objective vocabularies do not currently mesh.
- **Foundation for the hard problem:** Chalmers' distinction between "easy problems" (functional explanation) and the "hard problem" (why there is experience at all) is a direct descendant of this paper.
- **Qualia debates:** The paper anchors decades of debate (Jackson's Mary, Chalmers' zombies, Dennett's denial of qualia) about whether phenomenal character is a real explanandum or an illusion.
- **Limits of objectivity:** More radically, Nagel suggests that the ideal of a fully objective worldview may be incoherent — there are facts (subjective facts) that no view-from-nowhere can capture.
