# David Lewis (1941–2001)

David Kellogg Lewis was one of the most influential analytic philosophers of the twentieth century. He held a position at Princeton University for most of his career and worked across metaphysics, philosophy of language, philosophy of mind, and philosophical logic. His signature contribution — Modal Realism — remains among the most discussed and most contested positions in contemporary metaphysics.

## Biographical Sketch

- **Born**: 28 September 1941, Oberlin, Ohio
- **Died**: 14 October 2001, Princeton, New Jersey
- **Education**: Oberlin College; Harvard University (PhD, under W. V. O. Quine)
- **Career**: UCLA (1966–1970); Princeton University (1970–2001)
- **Notable recognition**: The John Locke Lectures at Oxford, Trinity Term, 1984 — which formed the core of *On the Plurality of Worlds* (1986)

Lewis was known for his methodological commitment to philosophical systematicity: the goal of reducing the number of primitives in one's total theory, gaining theoretical economy at the cost of a large but homogeneous ontology.

## Core Philosophical Contributions

### 1. Modal Realism

Lewis's central thesis, defended most fully in *On the Plurality of Worlds* (1986), is that **possible worlds are concrete, real, spatiotemporally extended universes** — not abstract representations, not stories, not linguistic constructs, but things of the same ontological kind as the actual world.

> "I advocate a thesis of plurality of worlds, or modal realism, which holds that our world is but one world among many. There are countless other worlds, other very inclusive things." — *OPW*, p. 2

Key features of Lewis's possible worlds:

- **Isolation**: Worlds are spatiotemporally isolated from one another. There are no causal or spatiotemporal relations between things belonging to different worlds.
- **Concreteness**: Other worlds are not abstract objects. They are as real and as concrete as this world. The difference between "actual" and "merely possible" is indexical, not ontological — "actual" simply means *this* world, the one we are part of.
- **Plenitude**: Every way a world could possibly be is a way that some world is. The space of worlds is maximally rich — there are worlds with different laws, different physical constants, alien properties, no sentient beings, and so on.
- **No self-causation**: Worlds are not of our making. We make descriptions, stipulations, and representations of worlds, but not the worlds themselves.

**Why believe it?** Lewis's primary justification is theoretical utility — modal realism is *fruitful*. It provides the ontological basis for:
- The analysis of **necessity** as truth at all worlds
- The analysis of **possibility** as truth at some world
- The analysis of **counterfactuals** via closeness/similarity between worlds
- Grounding **properties** as sets of possible individuals
- Formulating **supervenience** theses cleanly (without distortion from sentential modal operators)
- Analysis of **causation**, **laws of nature**, **mental content**, and more

> "As the realm of sets is for mathematicians, so logical space is a paradise for philosophers." — *OPW*, p. 4

Lewis acknowledges this is a good reason, not a conclusive one. He treats the incredulous stare — the intuition that such ontological extravagance is simply too much — as a serious objection, but one outweighed by the systematic benefits.

### 2. Counterpart Theory

Within Modal Realism, Lewis resolves problems of **de re modality** (what properties an individual could have had, or must have) via **Counterpart Theory**, first developed in "Counterpart Theory and Quantified Modal Logic" (1968).

The core idea: individuals exist in only one world. When we ask whether Humphrey could have won the presidency, we are not asking about Humphrey himself at some other world — he is not there. Instead, we ask whether some **counterpart** of Humphrey at another world wins. A counterpart is an individual at another world that resembles the original in relevant respects.

> "Modality de re, the potentiality and essence of things, is quantification over possible individuals. Quantification over possible individuals is commonly restricted by counterpart relations. In both cases, the restrictive relations usually involve similarity." — *OPW*, p. 8

Key features:
- **No trans-world identity**: no individual exists in more than one world (contra Kripke's view that the same individual can exist in multiple worlds)
- **Counterpart relations are context-sensitive and indeterminate**: what counts as a counterpart depends on the conversational context and what respects of similarity are salient
- **Multiple counterparts**: an individual can have multiple counterparts in a single world, and zero counterparts in some worlds
- **Against haecceitism**: Lewis argues against the view that two worlds can differ purely in which individual plays which qualitative role, without any qualitative difference

Counterpart theory resolves the Humphrey problem: "Humphrey might have won" is true iff at some world, a counterpart of Humphrey wins. That counterpart satisfies 'x wins' directly, without any satisfaction *in absentia* problem.

### 3. Humean Supervenience

Lewis's metaphysical picture of the actual world is built on **Humean Supervenience**: the thesis that all facts about the world supervene on the spatiotemporal arrangement of local, intrinsic, qualitative properties at points (or point-sized regions).

> "The world has its laws of nature, its chances and causal relationships; and yet — perhaps! — all there is to the world is its point-by-point distribution of local qualitative character." — *OPW*, p. 14

This is a Humean view because it denies necessary connections between distinct existences. There is no ontological glue between events — only the pattern. Laws of nature, causal relations, dispositions, and probabilities are all higher-order patterns that supervene on this Humean mosaic. Lewis held this as a working hypothesis, not a demonstrated truth, but it shaped his entire philosophy of mind, causation, and laws.

### 4. Counterfactual Analysis of Causation

Developed in "Causation" (1973) and essays in *Philosophical Papers* Vol. II, Lewis analyses causation in terms of counterfactual dependence:

- C causes E iff, if C had not occurred, E would not have occurred
- "Would not have occurred" is analysed via the nearest-world semantics: look at the closest worlds where C does not occur and check whether E occurs there

This avoids the need for any primitive notion of causal power or necessity. Causation becomes a pattern in how events depend counterfactually on one another, which in turn is grounded in relations of world-similarity.

Lewis later refined this analysis to address preemption cases (where two causes compete and one "wins") and extended it to a framework of **causal influence**.

### 5. Ersatzism — The Rival Programme

Chapter 3 of *On the Plurality of Worlds* ("Paradise on the Cheap?") is a sustained critique of **Ersatz Modal Realism** — the programme of achieving the theoretical benefits of possible worlds while substituting cheaper, more intuitively acceptable entities for Lewis's concrete worlds.

Lewis distinguishes three main ersatzist programmes:

| Type | Worlds are... | Objection |
|------|--------------|-----------|
| **Linguistic Ersatzism** | Maximal consistent sets of sentences | Requires a primitive notion of consistency/possibility — does not reduce modality |
| **Pictorial Ersatzism** | Abstract pictures/representations isomorphic to possible worlds | Representation relation is unexplained; alien properties cannot be represented |
| **Magical Ersatzism** | Abstract objects that "represent" by brute selection | Representation is a primitive, magical relation — no explanation given |

Lewis's overarching objection: ersatzism does not achieve genuine reduction of modal notions. It presupposes primitive modality under some guise. Modal realism, by contrast, analyses modal idioms via quantification over a domain of concrete entities — an ontological commitment, not a conceptual primitive.

> "I urge that we must distinguish the different versions, since they are subject to different objections; it will not do to dodge trouble by favouring abstract ersatz worlds in the abstract, without giving any definite account of them." — *OPW*, Preface, p. viii

### 6. Actuality as Indexical

Lewis holds that "actual" is an **indexical** term, like "here" or "now". Every world is actual from its own point of view. When we say "the actual world," we mean the world we are part of — not a world with some special ontological status. This dissolves the asymmetry between the actual and the merely possible: there is no ontological difference, only a difference in perspective.

### 7. Against Overlap — The Problem of Trans-World Individuals

Chapter 4 addresses trans-world identity directly. Lewis argues against **overlap** (the view that the same individual can be a part of multiple worlds) for several reasons:

- **Intrinsic properties**: If Humphrey is part of two worlds, and is bent in one and straight in another, then he is both bent and straight — a contradiction unless properties are relativised to worlds, which Lewis finds unacceptably complex
- **Against Haecceitism**: Overlapping worlds could differ only in which individual "is" which, with no qualitative difference — Lewis rejects this as metaphysically idle
- **Against constancy**: an individual's intrinsic properties cannot be constant across worlds if worlds differ in their qualitative character

## Philosophical Method

Lewis was a thoroughgoing **systematic metaphysician**. His method was:

1. **Identify theoretical desiderata** — what analyses does philosophy need? (modality, causation, laws, mental content, etc.)
2. **Catalogue the ontological costs** of providing them
3. **Argue that a large but uniform ontology** (concrete worlds + mereology + set theory) delivers better theoretical economy than a cluttered ontology of primitives
4. **Reply to objections** honestly, conceding where arguments are not conclusive

He was explicitly not in the business of giving knock-down arguments. He offered reasons that favour a position over its rivals, while acknowledging the position's costs.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Lewis's meaning |
|------|----------------|
| **Modal Realism** | The thesis that there exist concrete possible worlds, equally real to the actual world |
| **Counterpart** | An individual in another world that resembles a given individual in relevant respects; substitutes for trans-world identity |
| **Humean Supervenience** | All facts supervene on the local, intrinsic, qualitative distribution of properties across spacetime |
| **Ersatzism** | Any programme that replaces Lewis's concrete worlds with abstract representations (linguistic, pictorial, or "magical") |
| **Isolation** | The thesis that worlds are spatiotemporally and causally isolated from one another |
| **Plenitude** | Every way a world could possibly be is a way some world is |
| **Actuality (indexical)** | "Actual" refers to the world the speaker inhabits — no world has privileged actuality |
| **De re modality** | Modal claims about what a particular individual could or must be or do |
| **Haecceitism** | The view that worlds can differ in which individual plays which qualitative role, with no qualitative difference — Lewis rejects this |
