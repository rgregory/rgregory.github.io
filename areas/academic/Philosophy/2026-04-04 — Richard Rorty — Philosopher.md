# Richard Rorty — Philosopher

Richard Rorty (1931–2007) is the central figure of American neopragmatism — the philosopher who spent a career arguing that the entire project of epistemology, the dream of grounding knowledge in something mind-independent and necessary, is a historical mistake we should drop rather than solve. Trained as an analytic philosopher, he became one of the most effective critics of the analytic tradition from within, eventually building a synthesis that drew equally on Dewey, Wittgenstein, Heidegger, and Foucault.

## Core Commitments

**Anti-foundationalism.** Rorty's central claim is that there is no neutral standpoint, no "mirror of nature," from which the mind can verify its representations against reality. Epistemology since Descartes and Locke has been obsessed with this fantasy: that knowledge is justified when mental representations accurately reflect the world as it really is. Rorty argues the entire framework should be abandoned, not reformed. There is no Archimedean point outside all vocabularies.

**Truth as what is good to believe.** Following Dewey, Rorty treats truth not as correspondence to reality but as whatever survives free and open inquiry — what it is good for us to believe, given our purposes. This is not relativism in the sense that anything goes; it is anti-realism in the sense that "truth" is a compliment we pay to beliefs that work, that cohere, that help us cope. The question "is it true?" collapses into "should we believe it? does it help?"

**Solidarity over objectivity.** Rorty argues that the appeal to objectivity — to something beyond human agreement — is a rhetorical move that actually disempowers democratic deliberation. The liberal hope should be solidarity: expanding the circle of "us," the community to whom we feel moral obligations, through narrative, sympathy, and imaginative identification with suffering strangers. This is the project of *Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity* (1989): the right, the true, and the just are not discovered but created through conversation and storytelling.

**The ironist liberal.** Rorty's ideal figure is the "liberal ironist" — someone who holds their own deepest beliefs and commitments with full awareness that they are contingent, that they could have been otherwise, but who remains committed to reducing cruelty and expanding solidarity nonetheless. The ironist does not need foundations to act; she acts because she cares, not because the universe commands it. This combines the liberal tradition's political commitments with a thoroughgoing Nietzschean acknowledgment of contingency.

**Ethnocentrism without apology.** Rorty accepts a qualified ethnocentrism: we can only justify our beliefs to those who share enough background assumptions to engage in conversation. There is no view from nowhere. But this does not prevent moral progress — it just means moral progress is a matter of conversational expansion, not of discovering pre-existing truths.

## The Linguistic Turn and Vocabularies

For Rorty, following the later Wittgenstein and Heidegger, everything is a matter of vocabularies — sets of descriptions we use to cope with the world. Different vocabularies are not better or worse at "carving nature at its joints"; they are more or less useful for different purposes. The history of human progress is the history of abandoning old vocabularies (Ptolemaic astronomy, phlogiston chemistry, divine-right monarchy) for new ones that serve us better. No vocabulary is final; every vocabulary is revisable.

This has direct implications for philosophy: philosophy is not a discipline that uncovers necessary truths. It is a genre of writing — one among others, alongside novels, poetry, and political manifestos — that sometimes helps us redescribe ourselves and our world in ways that turn out to be useful.

## Key Works

- **Philosophy and the Mirror of Nature** (1979) — The founding neopragmatist text. Dismantles the epistemological tradition (Descartes → Locke → Kant → Russell) by showing that the "mind as mirror" metaphor is historically contingent, not philosophically compulsory. Argues for "edifying philosophy" (Dewey, Wittgenstein, Heidegger) over systematic epistemology.
- **Consequences of Pragmatism** (1982) — Essays on the implications of anti-foundationalism for philosophy of language, metaphysics, and literary theory.
- **Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity** (1989) — The political and ethical complement to *Mirror of Nature*. Three interlinked arguments: the contingency of language, of selfhood, and of liberal community. The ironist liberal as a response to the challenge of combining private self-creation with public solidarity.
- **Objectivity, Relativism, and Truth** (1991) — Defends neopragmatism against the charge of relativism; argues that "solidarity" is a better regulative ideal than "objectivity."
- **Achieving Our Country** (1998) — Political essays; Rorty's warning about the left's retreat from reformist politics into academic cultural criticism — arguably prophetic.

## In Relation To

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — John Dewey — Philosopher|John Dewey]]** — Dewey is Rorty's explicit intellectual hero. Rorty inherits Dewey's anti-Cartesian naturalism, his pragmatist account of inquiry, and his democratic politics. The key difference: Rorty is more explicitly anti-metaphysical and more attentive to the linguistic turn. Rorty strips Dewey of residual naturalist metaphysics and rebuilds him as a post-analytic philosopher.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Thomas Kuhn — Philosopher of Science|Thomas Kuhn]]** — Kuhn's paradigm shifts are a central exhibit in Rorty's case against foundationalism. If even science — the supposed paradigm case of rational inquiry converging on truth — proceeds through incommensurable paradigm shifts rather than steady accumulation, then there is no reason to privilege "scientific" epistemology over other forms of inquiry. Rorty reads Kuhn as showing that truth is always relative to a vocabulary, never absolute.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Martin Heidegger — Philosopher|Martin Heidegger]]** — Rorty reads Heidegger as a fellow anti-essentialist: Heidegger's critique of the metaphysics of presence, his insistence that Being cannot be reduced to beings, his historicizing of philosophy — all of these Rorty finds congenial. But Rorty rejects Heidegger's mysticism (the quasi-religious tone of the late work, "waiting for a new god"), his political catastrophe (Nazism), and his view that philosophy discloses something deep about Being. For Rorty, Heidegger is useful but in need of de-mystification.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Michel Foucault — Philosopher|Michel Foucault]]** — Rorty and Foucault share a commitment to the contingency of truth: both argue that what counts as knowledge, reason, and rationality is historically produced rather than discovered. But they diverge sharply on politics. Foucault's genealogical critiques expose the power relations embedded in truth regimes without offering an alternative. Rorty sees this as politically irresponsible: without a positive vision of solidarity and democratic reform, critique becomes nihilism. Rorty wants to keep Foucault's genealogical insights within a liberal political framework Foucault himself would have resisted.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Jean-François Lyotard — Philosopher|Jean-François Lyotard]]** — Both oppose metanarratives: Lyotard declares "incredulity toward metanarratives" as the postmodern condition; Rorty argues that we should abandon the quest for foundations. But Rorty prefers solidarity to Lyotard's paralogy. Where Lyotard celebrates the proliferation of incommensurable language games and the "differend" — wrongs that cannot be phrased in the dominant idiom — Rorty worries that this gives up on the possibility of expanding moral community across difference. For Rorty, the goal is not to celebrate incommensurability but to imaginatively bridge it through narrative and sympathy.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Modal Realism — David Lewis|Modal Realism — David Lewis]]** — Rorty and Lewis represent opposed poles of analytic philosophy. Lewis is the arch-metaphysician, committed to modal realism and the literal existence of possible worlds — exactly the kind of robust mind-independent reality that Rorty's anti-realism denies. Rorty would treat Lewis's possible worlds as a useful vocabulary for certain purposes, not as a discovery about what fundamentally exists.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Being and Event — Alain Badiou|Being and Event — Alain Badiou]]** — Badiou is in many respects a direct antagonist of Rorty: where Rorty abandons the concept of truth as metaphysically weighty, Badiou develops a rigorous ontology of the event and a theory of truth-procedures that is explicitly universalist and anti-relativist. Their disagreement maps the divide between neopragmatism and post-Lacanian continental philosophy.
