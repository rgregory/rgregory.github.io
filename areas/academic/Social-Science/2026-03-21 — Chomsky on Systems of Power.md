# Chomsky on Systems of Power

Noam Chomsky — linguist, philosopher, and political theorist — argues that modern democratic societies maintain control not through overt force but through the management of public thought. His central claim is that powerful institutions — states and corporations above all — systematically shape what people believe, what they discuss, and what they consider possible.

---

## 1. The Propaganda Model

Developed with economist Edward S. Herman in *Manufacturing Consent* (1988), the propaganda model holds that mass media in capitalist democracies does not simply report the world — it filters it. Five structural filters determine what becomes news and how it is framed:

1. **Ownership** — major media outlets are owned by large corporations or wealthy individuals with direct interests in maintaining the status quo
2. **Advertising** — the primary revenue source is advertisers, who are themselves large corporations; content that threatens advertiser interests gets suppressed or softened
3. **Sourcing** — journalists rely on government officials and corporate PR departments for information; these sources set the terms of debate
4. **Flak** — organised criticism, lawsuits, and pressure campaigns punish coverage that powerful actors dislike, creating a chilling effect
5. **Ideology** — an overarching framework (historically "anti-communism"; today "the war on terror" or "free markets") defines the boundaries of acceptable thought

The result is not conspiracy but structure: editors and reporters internalise these filters through career incentives and professional norms. Dissent is not banned — it is marginalised, ridiculed, or simply crowded out.

---

## 2. Manufactured Consent

The phrase — borrowed from Walter Lippmann — captures Chomsky's core idea: in a society too complex to govern by brute force, elite consensus is manufactured among the population through media, education, and public discourse.

Key mechanisms:
- **Agenda-setting**: what gets covered determines what the public considers important
- **Framing**: the language and frame around an event shapes how it is understood (e.g., "collateral damage" vs. "civilian deaths")
- **Necessary illusions**: citizens are offered just enough information to feel informed, but within a range of opinion that never threatens fundamental power arrangements
- **Spectacle and distraction**: entertainment and celebrity culture absorb attention that might otherwise be directed at institutional accountability

Chomsky draws a sharp distinction between *concision* — the short, punchy soundbite that dominates broadcast media — and genuine understanding. Complex, subversive arguments require context; concision systematically favours conventional wisdom.

---

## 3. Institutional Power and the State-Corporate Nexus

Chomsky does not treat the state and corporations as separate power centres that balance each other. He argues they form an interlocking system:

- The state provides the legal framework, military protection, and subsidies that corporations require
- Corporations in turn fund political campaigns, rotate personnel with government ("revolving door"), and shape regulatory capture
- Together they form a **national security state** in which foreign policy serves the interests of investors, not citizens

This explains why, Chomsky argues, U.S. foreign policy consistently supports authoritarian regimes that are "open for business" and undermines democratic governments that challenge corporate access — regardless of which party holds power.

---

## 4. How Public Discourse Is Shaped

Chomsky identifies several techniques by which the range of public debate is narrowed without appearing censored:

- **The bounds of thinkable thought**: mainstream debate appears wide (left vs. right), but both positions accept premises that exclude fundamental challenges to power (e.g., the legitimacy of empire, the desirability of GDP growth at any cost)
- **Expert culture**: the credentialing of "authorised knowers" — economists, security analysts, policy wonks — whose authority rests on institutional affiliation, not demonstrated accuracy
- **Double standards**: actions condemned when performed by enemies are ignored or justified when performed by one's own state
- **Historical amnesia**: inconvenient historical facts (e.g., U.S. support for Saddam Hussein in the 1980s) are routinely omitted from mainstream coverage

---

## 5. The Role of Language

Chomsky's political theory is inseparable from his work as a linguist. Language is not neutral: the vocabulary chosen to describe events encodes power relationships. He is particularly attentive to:

- **Euphemism in official discourse** — "enhanced interrogation", "pacification", "surgical strikes"
- **Passive constructions that erase agency** — "mistakes were made" rather than "we killed civilians"
- **Abstract nouns that depersonalise violence** — "collateral damage", "soft power"

This connects his linguistics to his politics: if language structures thought (as his own generative grammar work implies at a cognitive level), then controlling language is a form of cognitive control.

---

## 6. What Should Be Done

Chomsky is often asked whether his analysis leads to nihilism — if power is so entrenched, why act? His answer:

- Change has always come from organised popular movements, not from enlightened elites (abolition, labour rights, women's suffrage, civil rights)
- The function of propaganda analysis is not despair but *intellectual self-defence* — understanding how consent is manufactured allows individuals to step outside the frame
- Small acts of solidarity, community organising, and supporting independent media all matter
- He is explicitly not a revolutionary in the vanguard sense; he favours incremental expansion of democratic participation and accountability

## Criticisms and Counterpoints

- **Too structurally deterministic**: critics (including fellow left-thinkers) argue Chomsky underestimates the genuine diversity and independence of journalists within mainstream institutions
- **No theory of change**: the propaganda model is a critique, not a programme; Chomsky's preferred alternatives (anarcho-syndicalism, worker self-management) remain underdeveloped
- **Selection bias**: some argue he cherry-picks examples of media failure while ignoring cases where the press has genuinely held power to account
- **Conflates intent and outcome**: the propaganda model does not require anyone to conspire, but critics argue this makes it unfalsifiable
