# Thomas Kuhn — Philosopher of Science

Thomas Kuhn (1922–1996) is the philosopher of science whose 1962 book *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* changed how we think about science — arguably the most influential work in the history and philosophy of science of the twentieth century. Kuhn showed that science does not progress by the steady accumulation of facts or the continuous application of fixed methods, but through discontinuous revolutions in which entire conceptual frameworks are replaced by incommensurable successors.

## The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

### Normal Science and Paradigms
Kuhn's central concept is the **paradigm**: a constellation of beliefs, values, techniques, and exemplary problems that defines "normal science" for a scientific community at a given time. A paradigm is not just a theory; it includes the community's shared assumptions about what questions are worth asking, what counts as a solution, what instruments and methods are appropriate, and what anomalies can be safely ignored.

**Normal science** is the routine, puzzle-solving activity that takes place within a paradigm. Scientists working within a paradigm are not testing its foundations; they take its basic framework for granted and work on extending and articulating it. The vast majority of scientific work, most of the time, is normal science.

Crucially, paradigms are defined as much by **exemplars** — concrete problem-solutions that students learn to apply by analogy — as by explicit rules. Kuhn's insight was that scientific practice is transmitted through apprenticeship into specific problem-solving traditions, not through the mastery of explicit methodological principles.

### Anomalies and Crisis
Not all puzzles within normal science are solved. When anomalies accumulate — results that resist solution within the current paradigm, that persistently refuse to fit — they eventually precipitate a **crisis**: a period in which confidence in the paradigm is shaken and scientists begin to explore alternatives.

Crucially, anomalies do not automatically trigger paradigm change. A single failed experiment does not refute a paradigm; Kuhn explicitly argues against Popperian falsificationism on this point. Scientists routinely save paradigms by adding auxiliary hypotheses, questioning instrument calibration, or simply setting the anomaly aside as a "known problem" to be solved later. It takes a very deep accumulation of anomalies, and often a younger generation not fully committed to the old paradigm, to force a crisis.

### Scientific Revolutions and Incommensurability
A **scientific revolution** occurs when a new paradigm displaces the old one. The Copernican revolution, the Newtonian revolution, the chemical revolution (Lavoisier displacing phlogiston), the Einsteinian revolution, the quantum revolution — all are Kuhn's examples.

The most controversial claim: paradigms before and after a revolution are **incommensurable**. They are not simply different theories about the same facts, such that one can be straightforwardly compared to the other and judged better. The standards of comparison, the definitions of key terms, even the observations themselves are partially constituted by the paradigm. The chemist before Lavoisier and the chemist after do not disagree about the same phenomenon; they inhabit, in a sense, different worlds.

The **duck-rabbit** gestalt switch — borrowed from Wittgenstein — is Kuhn's vivid illustration. The same visual stimulus can be seen as a duck or as a rabbit, but not both simultaneously. When a scientist undergoes a paradigm shift, she does not gradually accumulate evidence until the old view collapses; there is a sudden gestalt switch — the whole world looks different. This is why Kuhn speaks of scientists "working in a different world" after a revolution.

### The Role of the Scientific Community
For Kuhn, science is irreducibly a **social** enterprise. The paradigm is the possession of a community, not an individual. The authority to judge when an anomaly is serious enough to trigger crisis, and when a proposed successor paradigm is good enough to replace the old one, rests with the community — specifically with the mature professionals who have been trained in the tradition and have the most at stake in it.

This is a departure from the image of science as a purely logical enterprise in which individual scientists follow the evidence wherever it leads. The sociology of scientific communities — authority structures, training, peer review, disciplinary identity — is constitutive of science, not merely accidental.

## Kuhn versus Popper

Karl Popper argued that science progresses by **falsificationism**: a theory is scientific if and only if it makes predictions that could in principle be shown false; and when a prediction fails, the theory should be rejected. Science accumulates by ruthlessly eliminating false theories.

Kuhn's objections are devastating: (1) scientists in practice do not abandon theories when predictions fail; they protect paradigms by auxiliary adjustments. (2) There is no paradigm-neutral observation language against which theories can be tested; the observations themselves are theory-laden. (3) The history of science does not look like continuous falsificationist progress; it looks like periods of normal science punctuated by revolutions.

Popper accused Kuhn of making science irrational (a matter of sociological pressure, not logic). Kuhn replied that the norms of rationality are internal to paradigms — there is no super-paradigmatic Reason that governs paradigm choice from outside.

## Lakatos and the Research Programme

Imre Lakatos developed the "methodology of scientific research programmes" partly in response to Kuhn. A research programme has a "hard core" of assumptions protected by a "protective belt" of auxiliary hypotheses. The programme is progressive (rational to pursue) if it generates novel predictions that are confirmed; it is degenerating (rational to abandon) if it only adds epicycles to protect the core.

This is an attempt to give a more sophisticated account of paradigm-like structures while preserving a notion of rational theory choice that Kuhn seemed to undermine. Kuhn found Lakatos's account closer to his own than Popper's, but still too rationalistic about the timing of theory change.

## Key Works

- **The Structure of Scientific Revolutions** (1962, 2nd ed. 1970) — The paradigm-changing book on paradigm change.
- **The Essential Tension** (1977) — Essays on tradition and innovation in science; the productive tension between conservative puzzle-solving and revolutionary novelty.
- **The Road Since Structure** (2000, posthumous) — Later essays on incommensurability, the nature of paradigms, and responses to critics.

## In Relation To

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Richard Rorty — Philosopher|Richard Rorty]]** — Rorty cites Kuhn repeatedly as a key anti-foundationalist ally. If scientific truth is paradigm-relative — if there is no paradigm-neutral way to adjudicate between incommensurable frameworks — then the epistemological dream of grounding knowledge in mind-independent facts collapses. Rorty reads Kuhn as showing that "truth" is always relative to the practices and purposes of a community, never an absolute. Kuhn himself was uncomfortable with this reading, insisting he was not a relativist; Rorty insisted the implications were unavoidable.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Michel Foucault — Philosopher|Michel Foucault]]** — Foucault's epistemic ruptures and Kuhn's paradigm shifts are structurally parallel: both argue that knowledge is organized by frameworks that change discontinuously rather than accumulating steadily. The key difference is scope and mechanism: Kuhn focuses on scientific communities and disciplinary practices; Foucault's episteme encompasses the entire discursive formation of a culture. Foucault adds a genealogical question Kuhn does not ask: what power relations produce and maintain the current paradigm?

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — John Dewey — Philosopher|John Dewey]]** — Dewey's account of inquiry as social problem-solving anticipates Kuhn's emphasis on the community as the locus of scientific rationality. For Dewey, inquiry is always conducted within a social context of shared practices; the standards of good inquiry are internal to the community of inquirers. Kuhn's normal science is in some ways Dewey's "warranted assertibility" applied to scientific communities specifically.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Martin Heidegger — Philosopher|Martin Heidegger]]** — A less direct but interesting connection: Heidegger's analysis of the ready-to-hand (Zeug, equipment) in *Being and Time* describes how tools and practices are transparent in normal use and become visible only when they break down. Kuhn's normal science has a similar structure: the paradigm is invisible when functioning, visible only in crisis. Both are thinking about the pre-theoretical background of practices that makes inquiry possible.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Jean-François Lyotard — Philosopher|Jean-François Lyotard]]** — Lyotard in *The Postmodern Condition* draws explicitly on Kuhn's notion of incommensurability to argue that scientific knowledge operates within local language games that cannot be legitimated by a master narrative. Kuhn's paradigm incommensurability supports Lyotard's "paralogy" — the postmodern science that valorizes dissent and the production of new moves rather than consensus around a dominant paradigm.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Zermelo-Fraenkel Set Theory (ZF-ZFC)|Zermelo-Fraenkel Set Theory (ZF-ZFC)]]** — An interesting structural echo: just as different set-theoretic foundations can generate incompatible but internally consistent mathematical universes (with or without the Axiom of Choice, with or without large cardinal axioms), different scientific paradigms generate incompatible but internally consistent worlds of inquiry. The incommensurability of paradigms is analogous to the independence results in set theory: there are questions that cannot be decided from within the current axiom system.

**[[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-04 — Being and Event — Alain Badiou|Being and Event — Alain Badiou]]** — Badiou's theory of the event — a rupture in the situation that forces new truth procedures — is formally analogous to Kuhn's scientific revolution. For Badiou, a genuine event (like the French Revolution, or Cantor's creation of set theory) is precisely what cannot be recognized as such from within the existing "state of the situation." Kuhn's paradigm shift similarly cannot be fully articulated in the old paradigm's vocabulary.
