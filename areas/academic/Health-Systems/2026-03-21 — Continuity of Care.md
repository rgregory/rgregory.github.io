# Continuity of Care

Continuity of care is the degree to which a patient experiences coordinated, uninterrupted care over time and across different settings. It is the opposite of fragmented care — the frustrating experience of repeating your medical history to every new provider, getting contradictory advice, or falling through the cracks between a hospital discharge and a follow-up appointment. It is one of the oldest and most consistently studied concepts in health services research, and evidence links it to better outcomes, lower costs, and higher patient satisfaction.

---

## Definition

The most widely used definition comes from the work of Haggerty et al. (2003), published in the *BMJ*:

> Continuity is the experience of care over time by a patient with a health condition. It requires that care be coherent with the patient's medical history and personal circumstances.

In practice, continuity asks: does the care a patient receives today make sense in the context of everything that has happened before — and does it set them up well for what comes next?

---

## The Three Types of Continuity

The literature consistently distinguishes three distinct but overlapping dimensions:

### 1. Informational Continuity

The availability and use of information about past events and personal circumstances to make current care appropriate.

- Includes: medical history, test results, medications, allergies, prior diagnoses, patient preferences
- Enabled by: electronic health records (EHRs), shared care plans, discharge summaries, referral letters
- Breaks down when: records are siloed across systems, a patient sees a specialist who cannot access their GP's notes, or a hospital doesn't share a discharge summary in time

**Example**: A patient with diabetes sees an endocrinologist. Informational continuity means the specialist can see the GP's last six months of HbA1c results without the patient having to recall them from memory.

### 2. Management Continuity

A consistent and coherent approach to the management of a health condition that is responsive to the patient's changing needs.

- Includes: agreed care plans, consistent protocols across providers, smooth handoffs between settings
- Enabled by: shared clinical guidelines, structured transitions of care, case managers
- Breaks down when: different providers prescribe conflicting treatments, a patient is discharged from hospital without a clear follow-up plan, or a specialist's recommendations contradict a GP's ongoing treatment

**Example**: After a heart attack, management continuity means the cardiologist, rehabilitation team, pharmacist, and GP are all working from the same recovery protocol — not giving the patient four different versions of what to eat, what to avoid, and when to return to work.

### 3. Relational Continuity

An ongoing therapeutic relationship between a patient and one or more providers, characterised by accumulated knowledge of the patient and trust.

- Includes: seeing the same GP or specialist over time, the provider knowing the patient's life context (job, family, values), mutual trust
- Enabled by: long-term registered patient lists, appointment systems that support same-provider access
- Breaks down when: patients are assigned to whoever is available, frequent staff turnover, overly large panel sizes

**Example**: A GP who has known a patient for ten years recognises that a new complaint of fatigue is unusual for this patient and warrants investigation — where a new provider might dismiss it as normal variation.

## Why It Matters

Continuity of care is not primarily a quality-of-life nicety. It has direct clinical and economic consequences.

### For Patients
- Providers with longitudinal knowledge of a patient are better positioned to detect subtle changes, contextualise symptoms, and make accurate diagnoses
- Patients with a trusted regular provider are more likely to disclose sensitive information (mental health symptoms, risky behaviour, non-adherence to medication)
- Reduced duplication of tests and investigations

### For Health Systems
- Lower rates of unnecessary emergency department visits and hospital admissions
- Fewer medication errors at care transitions (one of the most common and dangerous points of failure in hospital care)
- Better management of chronic conditions — which are the primary driver of healthcare costs in all high-income countries. This challenge is intensifying as advanced therapies create new continuity demands: a patient who has received [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Health-Systems/2026-04-01 — How Does CRISPR Work|CRISPR-based gene therapy]] (such as Casgevy for sickle cell disease) requires long-term follow-up across haematology, primary care, and potentially reproductive medicine — providers who are unfamiliar with the intervention's mechanism and long-term unknowns cannot provide meaningful relational or management continuity for that patient

### For Safety
- Transitions of care (handoffs between providers or settings) are a leading cause of adverse events. Continuity of information and management directly reduces this risk.

---

## Evidence on Outcomes

The research base on continuity is large and consistent across health systems and countries.

| Outcome | Finding |
|---------|---------|
| All-cause mortality | Patients with higher relational continuity have significantly lower mortality. A 2018 meta-analysis (Pereira Gray et al., *BMJ Open*) found that 22 of 22 studies showed lower mortality in patients with higher continuity |
| Hospital admissions | Higher continuity associated with fewer avoidable admissions, particularly for chronic conditions |
| Emergency department use | Patients with a regular GP use emergency services less |
| Patient satisfaction | Consistently higher in patients with relational continuity |
| Preventive care uptake | Higher rates of vaccination, cancer screening, and health maintenance in patients with a regular provider |
| Medication safety | Continuity reduces polypharmacy errors and adverse drug events at transitions |

The effect sizes are clinically significant, not marginal. The 2018 Pereira Gray meta-analysis found continuity with a personal doctor was associated with a 25% reduction in mortality risk across included studies.

## Challenges to Continuity

Despite the evidence, continuity is under pressure in most health systems.

### Structural Challenges
- **Shift-based working**: Modern hospitals and primary care practices use rotas and shifts, meaning patients rarely see the same provider twice in an acute setting
- **Specialisation**: Complex patients are managed by multiple specialists who may not communicate effectively with each other or with the patient's GP
- **System fragmentation**: Health records are frequently not interoperable between hospitals, GP practices, pharmacies, and social care
- **Workforce shortages**: GP shortages mean panel sizes are too large to allow meaningful relational continuity; waiting times force patients to see whoever is available

### Patient-Side Challenges
- Geographic mobility — patients move and change providers
- Preference for convenience (same-day access) over provider continuity, particularly for acute problems
- Multiple comorbidities requiring multiple specialist relationships simultaneously

### Measurement Challenges
- Continuity is difficult to measure consistently. Common indices include:
  - **UPC (Usual Provider of Care)**: proportion of visits to the most frequently seen provider
  - **COC (Continuity of Care index)**: a distribution-based measure across all providers seen
  - **SECON (Sequential COC)**: measures whether consecutive visits are with the same provider
  - Different indices capture different dimensions and can give conflicting results for the same patient

---

## Continuity vs. Access: A Real Tension

One of the genuine dilemmas in primary care design is the trade-off between continuity and access. If a patient can only see their registered GP, wait times may be long. If they can see any available clinician today, continuity suffers.

Evidence generally suggests:
- For acute, minor illness: access matters more; relational continuity is less critical
- For chronic disease management, complex patients, mental health, and older patients with multiple conditions: relational continuity has substantially larger effects
- The optimal model varies by patient need and is not one-size-fits-all

Some health systems (notably the NHS in the UK, post-2023) have attempted to formalise this trade-off by distinguishing between "access appointments" and "continuity appointments" within the same practice.

## Summary

| Dimension | What It Means | Key Enabler | Key Failure Point |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Informational | Right information available at every contact | Interoperable EHR | Siloed records |
| Management | Coherent, coordinated treatment plan | Shared protocols, care transitions | Conflicting advice across providers |
| Relational | Ongoing relationship with a known provider | Long-term registration, low turnover | Rota-based access, large panel sizes |

Continuity of care is not a soft or secondary concern. It is a structural property of health systems that directly affects whether patients survive, recover, and are treated safely. The evidence base supporting it is unusually consistent.
