# Treatment of Hypothyroidism

## The Condition

Hypothyroidism is insufficient production of thyroid hormone (T4/T3) by the thyroid gland. It is classified by level of failure in the hypothalamic–pituitary–thyroid axis:

- **Primary** (most common): the thyroid gland itself fails. The leading cause is **Hashimoto's thyroiditis**, an autoimmune condition in which the immune system progressively destroys thyroid tissue.
- **Secondary**: the pituitary fails to produce TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone), so the thyroid receives no signal to produce hormone.
- **Tertiary**: the hypothalamus fails to produce TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone), disrupting the cascade upstream of the pituitary.

## Standard Treatment

**Levothyroxine** (synthetic T4, abbreviated LT4) is the standard of care. It is taken orally, once daily, on an empty stomach — ideally 30–60 minutes before food — to ensure consistent absorption.

T4 is a prohormone: peripheral tissues (liver, kidneys, muscle) convert it to the biologically active T3 via deiodinase enzymes. For the majority of patients, T4 monotherapy is sufficient because this conversion proceeds normally.

## Monitoring

**TSH** is the primary monitoring tool. The feedback loop is straightforward: low circulating T4 causes the pituitary to release more TSH; therefore TSH is **inversely correlated** with thyroid hormone levels.

- **High TSH** = undertreated (pituitary is compensating for insufficient T4)
- **Low TSH** = overtreated (pituitary is suppressed by excess T4)

### TSH Target Ranges

| Population | Target TSH (mIU/L) |
|---|---|
| Most adults | ~0.5–2.5 |
| Elderly | Higher end (less aggressive suppression) |
| Pregnancy (1st trimester) | ~0.1–2.5 (tighter control) |

Recheck TSH **6–8 weeks** after any dose change; lab values lag the clinical state.

## Drug Interactions — Reduced Absorption

The following substances reduce levothyroxine absorption and should be separated by at least 4 hours:

- Calcium carbonate
- Iron supplements
- Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)
- Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant)

## T3 Supplementation — Controversial

A subset of patients on T4 monotherapy have persistent symptoms (fatigue, cognitive slowing, mood changes) despite a normal TSH. Small RCTs suggest that a **T4+T3 combination** (adding liothyronine, synthetic T3) may benefit this subset. This is not standard practice.

Variants in the **DEIODINASE gene** (responsible for T4→T3 conversion) may predict which patients are poor converters and thus candidates for combination therapy — an emerging area of pharmacogenomics.

## Symptoms of Undertreated Hypothyroidism

Useful for clinical recognition and monitoring adequacy of treatment:

Fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation, bradycardia, dry skin, hair loss, myxedema (subcutaneous mucinous deposits), depression, cognitive slowing, menstrual irregularities.
