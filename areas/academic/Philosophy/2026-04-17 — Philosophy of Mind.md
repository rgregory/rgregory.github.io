# Philosophy of Mind

**Philosophy of mind** investigates the nature of the mind, mental events, mental functions, mental properties, consciousness, and their relationship to the physical body, particularly the brain. Central question: what is the relationship between mind and matter?

## Major Positions
- **Dualism** (Descartes): mind and body are distinct substances
- **Physicalism/Materialism**: mental states are physical states of the brain
- **Functionalism**: mental states are defined by their functional role, not physical substrate (enables multiple realizability)
- **Eliminativism** (Churchland): folk-psychological concepts like "belief" are false and will be replaced by neuroscience
- **Phenomenology** (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty): starts from the structure of conscious experience itself

## Hard vs Easy Problems
- **Easy problems** (Chalmers): explaining cognitive functions — attention, memory, integration
- **Hard problem**: why and how physical processes give rise to subjective experience (qualia)

- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Neuroscience/2026-05-16 — Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptors|Nicotinic Acetylcholine Receptors (nAChRs)]] — a concrete case for the substrate question: the same cognitive function (attention, working memory, sensory gating) is implemented via α4β2 and α7 nAChRs in mammals and via convergent receptor profiles in birds; nAChR pharmacology grounds functionalism's multiple realizability claim empirically — and the Alzheimer's connection (loss of cholinergic neurons → cognitive decline) shows that substrate is not fully irrelevant, contra strong functionalism
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Health-Systems/Neurology/2026-06-23 — Alzheimer's Disease|Alzheimer's Disease]] — AD is the paradigm case in philosophy of mind for the memory-continuity theory of personal identity (Locke/Parfit): episodic memory is the first and most catastrophically lost faculty, yet the patient persists as a biological entity for 4–10 years. Functionalism faces its sharpest challenge here — not from rapid substrate damage (as in VaD) or from fluctuation (as in LBD) but from the slow, irreversible unraveling of the very faculty most theories of personal identity treat as constitutive of selfhood.
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Health-Systems/Neurology/2026-06-23 — Lewy Body Dementia|Lewy Body Dementia]] — the progressive dissolution of personal identity in LBD is a philosophical test case: fluctuating cognition, loss of the capacity for continuous first-person experience, and vivid visual hallucinations that decouple the patient from shared perceptual reality. At what point does the neurodegenerative erosion of the substrate change *who* someone is? The cholinergic deficit that grounds functionalism's substrate question here is more severe in LBD than in Alzheimer's — a clinical instance where the irrelevance of substrate is hardest to maintain
- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Health-Systems/Neurology/2026-06-23 — Vascular Dementia|Vascular Dementia]] — raises a distinct functionalism challenge from LBD. VaD's hallmark is executive dysfunction from severed frontal–subcortical loops: a patient can retain episodic memory while losing planning, judgment, and deliberate agency. This is localized substrate damage with directly mappable functional consequences — harder to reconcile with strong functionalism's substrate-independence than diffuse protein aggregation is. It also inverts the personal-identity question: in VaD, the "self" that persists across time may remain in long-term memory but cannot deliberate or initiate — raising whether executive function, not memory, is the more fundamental substrate of personal identity

- [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Cognitive-Science/2026-06-23 — Memory Decay and Cognitive Decline|Memory Decay and Cognitive Decline]] — the reconsolidation finding (Nader, Schafe & LeDoux 2000) — that every retrieval re-opens a memory to modification before re-stabilization — directly challenges archival models of mind and has implications for Locke/Parfit memory-continuity theories of personal identity: if autobiographical memory is reconstructive rather than stored, then the continuity it is supposed to provide is itself a dynamic confabulation. This is the cognitive-science empirical complement to the philosophical critique AD already poses to personal-identity theory
