# Nietzsche — "God Is Dead"

## The Phrase and Its Source

The phrase comes from *The Gay Science* (1882), spoken by a madman who runs into a marketplace shouting "God is dead" — met with laughter, because the crowd has already stopped believing. The madman's point: they don't understand what they've done.

## What Nietzsche Actually Means

This is not an atheist announcement. It is a description of a cultural event with catastrophic consequences. "God" stands for the entire framework of transcendent values — the idea that there exists an objective moral order, that the universe has inherent purpose, that suffering has meaning, that truth is absolute. The Enlightenment and the rise of science killed that framework. But the West has not yet reckoned with what it lost.

## See also

- [[MOC/Self-Formation|Self-Formation MOC]] — this note sets the problem to which Nietzsche's self-formation ethics is the answer: with the transcendent framework gone, the self can no longer receive its shape from above and must create itself

## The Actual Problem — Nihilism

When the transcendent foundation collapses, so does everything built on it: objective morality, inherent human dignity, cosmic purpose. The danger is not disbelief in God — it's the vacuum. People will either face this honestly or unconsciously substitute new absolutes: nationalism, progress, science, political ideology. These are "God" in disguise — secular substitutes for transcendent certainty.

## The Übermensch and Value Creation

Nietzsche's response is not despair but a challenge: if values can no longer be received from above, they must be *created*. The Übermensch is not a superhuman — it is a person who can create and commit to values without needing cosmic backing. The alternative is the Last Man — comfortable, herd-following, meaning-avoiding.

## Will to Power

Not about domination. Nietzsche's account of what drives living things: the drive to express, expand, and overcome. Values, art, philosophy, and science are expressions of will to power — the question is whether they are life-affirming or life-denying (resentment disguised as morality).
