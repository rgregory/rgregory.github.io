# Lewy Body Dementia

Umbrella term for two closely related neurodegenerative diseases: **Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB)** and **Parkinson's Disease Dementia (PDD)**. Together the second most common cause of degenerative dementia after Alzheimer's disease — approximately 1.4 million Americans affected.

## Pathophysiology

Pathological hallmark: accumulation of **alpha-synuclein** (normally soluble presynaptic protein) into insoluble, misfolded aggregates forming **Lewy bodies** (eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions) and **Lewy neurites** within neurons.

**Propagation**: alpha-synuclein spreads via a **prion-like, transsynaptic mechanism** — misfolded seeds template normal protein into pathogenic conformations. Routes include ascending (brainstem → cortex), descending (olfactory bulb → brainstem), and peripheral (enteric nervous system → vagus → brainstem).

**Neuroanatomy**: Lewy bodies in deep cortical layers (anterior frontal/temporal lobes, cingulate gyrus, insula). Subcortical involvement includes substantia nigra (parkinsonism), locus coeruleus, and nucleus basalis of Meynert (severe cholinergic deficit — worse than Alzheimer's).

Co-pathology: concomitant Alzheimer pathology (amyloid-beta, tau) present in a significant proportion of DLB cases.

---

## DLB vs. PDD — The 1-Year Rule

The distinction is **purely clinical and temporal** — neuropathologically nearly indistinguishable at autopsy.

| Feature | DLB | PDD |
|---|---|---|
| Defining criterion | Dementia onset ≤1 year of parkinsonism | Dementia onset >1 year after established PD |
| Cognitive profile | Attention/executive deficits prominent early | Similar, often milder initially |
| Motor severity | Often milder, bilateral parkinsonism | More established classical PD motor features |
| Lewy body distribution | More cortical predominance | Brainstem → cortex progression |

Whether DLB and PDD are the same disease on a spectrum or distinct entities remains unresolved.

---

## Core Clinical Features (2017 DLB Consortium Criteria)

**Probable DLB** = ≥2 core features, OR 1 core feature + ≥1 indicative biomarker.

### 1. Fluctuating Cognition
Spontaneous, pronounced variation in attention and alertness; episodes lasting minutes to hours. "Glazed" look, staring spells, disproportionate daytime drowsiness. Frequently misidentified as TIA, epilepsy, or medication side effects.

### 2. Recurrent Visual Hallucinations
Present in ~**80%** of DLB patients. Vivid, detailed, persistent: people, animals, children. Patient often retains insight early. More common and more formed than Alzheimer's hallucinations.

### 3. REM Sleep Behavior Disorder (RBD)
Loss of normal muscle atonia during REM → physically acting out dreams (punching, kicking, yelling). **Highly specific (>98%)** for synucleinopathies (LBD, PD, MSA). Often precedes cognitive symptoms by **10–15 years** — strongest prodromal marker known. Diagnosed by polysomnography.

### 4. Parkinsonism
At least one of: bradykinesia, rest tremor, rigidity. Typically **bilateral, symmetric** at onset (vs. unilateral classical PD). Less tremor-dominant. Postural instability and falls common.

---

## Supportive Features and Biomarkers

**Supportive clinical features**: severe antipsychotic sensitivity, falls/syncope, severe autonomic dysfunction (orthostatic hypotension, constipation, urinary incontinence), hypersomnia, hyposmia, depression, apathy, anxiety.

**Indicative biomarkers (carry diagnostic weight)**:
- **DaTSCAN (SPECT/PET)**: reduced dopamine transporter uptake — separates DLB/PDD from Alzheimer's with high accuracy; FDA-approved for this indication
- **Polysomnography-confirmed RBD**
- **MIBG myocardial scintigraphy**: low uptake reflects cardiac sympathetic denervation; highly specific vs. Alzheimer's

**Emerging biomarkers (2025)**:
- **CSF alpha-synuclein seed amplification assay (SAA/RT-QuIC)**: 79% sensitivity, 97% specificity; ~100% sensitivity for neocortical-predominant disease
- **Skin biopsy for phosphorylated alpha-synuclein (P-SYN)**: peripheral, minimally invasive; high accuracy in recent-onset cognitive impairment
- **EEG**: posterior slow-wave activity; temporal lobe transient sharp waves

---

## Diagnosis

**Gold standard**: post-mortem neuropathology.

**Clinical workup**:
- DaTSCAN — dopaminergic deficit in striatum
- Polysomnography — RBD confirmation
- MRI brain — relatively preserved hippocampal volume (vs. AD); posterior cortical hypoperfusion
- Neuropsychological testing — attention/executive/visuospatial deficits; relatively preserved memory encoding

**Underdiagnosis**: only 50–80% of autopsy-confirmed DLB cases receive correct antemortem diagnosis.

---

## Prognosis

- Mean survival from symptom onset: **5–8 years** (range 2–20)
- More rapid cognitive decline than typical AD
- Nursing home placement typically earlier (behavioral symptoms, autonomic instability, falls)
- Death often from aspiration pneumonia, infections, cardiovascular events

---

## Treatment

No disease-modifying therapy exists. Management is symptomatic.

### Cognitive/Neuropsychiatric
- **Rivastigmine** (FDA-approved for PDD) and **donepezil**: first-line; cholinergic deficit in LBD is more severe than in AD, making this class particularly effective
- **Memantine**: modest benefit in some patients

### Parkinsonism
- **Levodopa**: modestly effective; start low, titrate slowly; can worsen hallucinations
- Dopamine agonists: generally avoided (psychosis risk, impulse control)

### RBD
- **Melatonin** (3–12 mg at bedtime): first-line
- **Clonazepam** (0.25–1 mg): effective but risk of falls/sedation

### Autonomic Dysfunction
- Orthostatic hypotension: fludrocortisone, midodrine, increased salt/fluid
- Urinary urgency: avoid anticholinergics (worsen cognition); consider mirabegron

### Antipsychotics — CRITICAL CONTRAINDICATION

> **~50% of DLB patients** exhibit **severe neuroleptic sensitivity reactions** — acute parkinsonism, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, loss of consciousness, death — even from a single dose.

- **Absolutely avoid**: haloperidol, olanzapine, risperidone
- **If unavoidable**: quetiapine (most commonly used) or clozapine (requires weekly CBC)
- **Best option**: **pimavanserin** (5-HT2A inverse agonist; no D2 activity; FDA-approved for PD psychosis; best safety profile)
- **First line for hallucinations**: optimize rivastigmine/donepezil — not antipsychotics

## Epidemiology

- 2nd most common degenerative dementia globally
- ~1.4 million Americans; 4–16% of dementia clinic diagnoses (underdiagnosed)
- Incidence: 0.5–1.6 per 1,000 person-years
- Peak onset: ages **70–85**
- **Male predominance**: ~1.5–2:1 ratio
- PDD: 25–30% of established PD patients develop dementia; ~14% per year in PD patients over 65

## Vs. Alzheimer's / Parkinson's

| Feature | DLB | Alzheimer's | Parkinson's |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary pathology | Alpha-synuclein (Lewy bodies) | Amyloid-beta + tau | Alpha-synuclein (brainstem-predominant) |
| Memory at onset | Relatively spared | Episodic memory early | Variable |
| Visual hallucinations | Early, vivid (~80%) | Late (~15–25%) | ~20–40% with dementia |
| Parkinsonism | Early, bilateral | Late if present | Defining feature |
| RBD | Core feature | Rare | Common (prodromal) |
| DaTSCAN | Abnormal | Normal | Abnormal |
| Antipsychotic risk | Severe | Moderate | High |
| Cholinesterase inhibitor | Strong response | Moderate | Strong (PDD) |
| Hippocampal atrophy | Relatively preserved | Disproportionate | Variable |

## Current Research

- **Alpha-synuclein SAA**: moving toward clinical implementation; CSF and skin achieving >90% sensitivity/specificity
- **Blood-based biomarkers**: plasma alpha-synuclein SAA and conformational analysis (distinguishing DLB from PD signatures) — in development
- **Anti-alpha-synuclein immunotherapy**: passive immunization trials; prion-like spread model informs mechanism
- **GBA1 variants**: ~10–15% of DLB patients carry glucocerebrosidase mutations; substrate reduction therapy being explored
- **Prodromal detection**: RBD cohorts followed prospectively for disease-modifying intervention before dementia onset (10–15 year prodromal window is the target)
- **Pimavanserin for DLB psychosis**: ongoing trials (currently FDA-approved only for PD psychosis)
