Lecture 1

The problem functionalism answers

  • Mind-brain identity theory says mental states are identical to specific brain states.
  • That works only if mental states require one physical substrate.
  • Functionalism enters because the substrate story looks too narrow.

Visual cueSimple fork diagram: dualism vs physicalism vs functionalism.

02
Lecture 1

Multiple realizability

  • A functional kind is defined by what it does, not what it is made of.
  • Same job, many possible physical realizations.
  • Examples: table, mouse trap, instructor, vending machine.

Visual cueOne role, many shapes and materials.

03
Lecture 1

The identity theory breaks here

  • If pain were identical to one human brain state, other creatures could not feel pain.
  • Octopuses are the pressure test.
  • They look different internally but still seem capable of pain.

Lecture 2

Mental states as causal roles

  • Functionalism defines mental states by inputs and outputs.
  • A state is mental because of its role in a system.
  • Causes become inputs; effects become outputs.

Visual cueInput → state → output flow diagram.

05
Lecture 2

The jealousy example

  • Input: seeing a photo of an ex plus a memory.
  • Output: whimpering, denial, defensive speech.
  • The substrate can vary; the causal pattern does the work.

Visual cueSocial-media post + memory → emotional reaction loop.

06
Lecture 2

The payoff

  • Functionalism keeps physicalism without tying mind to one material.
  • It explains why non-human or artificial systems might count as minded.
  • The question shifts from “what is it made of?” to “what role does it play?”.

Visual cueIf it can do the job, it can be the state.
