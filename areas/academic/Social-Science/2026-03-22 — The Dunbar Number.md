# The Dunbar Number

The Dunbar Number (≈150) is the cognitive limit on the number of stable social relationships a human can maintain. It was proposed by anthropologist Robin Dunbar in the 1990s, derived from a correlation between primate neocortex size and average social group size — scaled up to humans.

## Why It Matters

**Evolutionary baseline.** The number aligns with the size of hunter-gatherer bands, Roman military units (the maniple, ~120–130 soldiers), and village communities across recorded history. This convergence across unrelated contexts suggests the limit is neurological rather than cultural.

**Organizational design.** Companies such as Gore-Tex deliberately cap individual facility headcount at ~150. Below this threshold, social accountability — knowing everyone personally — replaces the need for formal hierarchy, rules, and enforcement mechanisms. Above it, institutions become necessary.

**Layered structure.** Dunbar identified nested concentric circles of social proximity, each roughly 3× the size of the previous layer:

| Layer | Size | Character |
|-------|------|-----------|
| Intimate | ~5 | Deep trust, emotional support |
| Close friends | ~15 | Active mutual care |
| Active friends | ~50 | Regular contact |
| Stable social group | ~150 | Known by name and relation |
| Extended network | ~500 | Recognized acquaintances |
| Meaningful contacts | ~1,500 | Faces you can name |

**Digital social networks.** Despite the premise that technology expands social capacity, studies of online behavior show that people actively maintain roughly ~150 meaningful relationships even when total connection counts run into the thousands. Platform affordances change reach, not cognitive bandwidth.

**Trust and coordination mechanics.** Below ~150, groups self-regulate through reputation and personal knowledge. Above it, anonymous coordination requires codified rules, dedicated enforcement, and hierarchical structure — a phase transition in organizational logic.

## Caveats

The precise figure is debated; plausible estimates range from 100 to 250 depending on methodology and population studied. The underlying principle — that there is a cognitive ceiling on meaningful social bonds, rooted in the size of the neocortex — holds up across evolutionary biology, anthropology, organizational theory, and network science.
