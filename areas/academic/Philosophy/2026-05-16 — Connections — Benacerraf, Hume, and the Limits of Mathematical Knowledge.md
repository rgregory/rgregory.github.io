# Connections — Benacerraf, Hume, and the Limits of Mathematical Knowledge

## The Parallel

Benacerraf's 1973 dilemma is Hume's fork applied to mathematics.

**Hume (1748)**: We cannot justify the inference from observed patterns to necessary connection. Causation is habit, not logic. The Uniformity of Nature is presupposed, not concluded. Every method has a horizon beyond which it cannot see — and it cannot see *that* it cannot see.

**Benacerraf (1973)**: If mathematical objects are abstract (causally inert, outside space-time), then on any causal/empiricist theory of knowledge we *cannot know them*. But if we abandon abstract objects to save epistemology, we lose a homogeneous semantics — mathematical truth becomes unlike truth everywhere else.

## What each illuminates about the other

Hume draws the limit *generally*: no method can validate its own presuppositions. Benacerraf shows the *specific* cost when you apply that limit to mathematics: you must choose between semantic adequacy and epistemological adequacy. You cannot have both.

The vault already pairs Hume and Gödel as structural parallels (both show that a system cannot fully account for itself). Benacerraf makes the triangle explicit:

- **Gödel** shows the limit from *inside* formal systems (incompleteness)
- **Hume** shows the limit from *inside* empirical reasoning (induction)
- **Benacerraf** shows the limit from *between* the two: the impossibility of bridging abstract truth and embodied knowledge

## The deeper pattern

All three are instances of a single structure: *the level of description determines what can be known*. This connects to [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-03-22 — The Aggregation Problem|The Aggregation Problem]] — the centrepiece vault note arguing that the choice of descriptive level is constitutive, not merely practical.

Benacerraf's dilemma is the Aggregation Problem applied to the philosophy/mathematics boundary: there is no meta-level from which both semantic and epistemological adequacy are simultaneously visible.
