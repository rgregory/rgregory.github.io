# Dual Process Theory — System 1 and System 2

## The Framework

Dual Process Theory holds that human cognition operates via two qualitatively different systems:

|                | System 1                          | System 2                              |
|----------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Speed**      | Fast (milliseconds)               | Slow (seconds)                        |
| **Effort**     | Effortless, automatic             | Effortful, deliberate                 |
| **Consciousness** | Largely unconscious            | Conscious, explicit                   |
| **Control**    | Difficult to control              | Intentionally controlled              |
| **Capacity**   | High capacity (parallel)          | Limited capacity (serial)             |
| **Error type** | Systematic (biases)               | Random (fatigue, distraction)         |

Most associated with Daniel Kahneman (*Thinking, Fast and Slow*, 2011), but the framework integrates work by Stanovich & West, Sloman, and Evans.

## System 1 in Practice

- Face recognition, language comprehension, emotional reactions, pattern matching
- Produces heuristics — fast rules that are often right but systematically wrong in predictable ways
- Produces intuitions that feel certain but may be inaccurate
- Cannot be easily turned off; generates answers even when System 2 is not activated to check them

## System 2 in Practice

- Formal reasoning, mathematical calculation, deliberate planning, rule-following in novel contexts
- Lazy by default — conserves cognitive resources; often endorses System 1 outputs without checking
- Can override System 1 but rarely does spontaneously
- Resource-limited: cognitive load, fatigue, and time pressure all reduce System 2 availability

## The Key Insight

The danger is not that System 1 is wrong — it is often right and is computationally indispensable. The danger is that System 2 is lazy and endorses System 1 outputs as if they were deliberate conclusions. Most cognitive errors occur not because System 1 fires, but because System 2 fails to intervene.

## Critiques

- The boundary between systems is not anatomically or computationally clean
- "System 2" may not be a unified system — it may be a family of control processes
- The framework can become unfalsifiable if any error is attributed to System 1 post-hoc
