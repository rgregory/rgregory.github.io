# Vascular Dementia

Cognitive decline caused by impaired blood supply to the brain — ischemic, hemorrhagic, or hypoperfusive injury — rather than by accumulation of a misfolded protein. The prototype of cognitive impairment with an identifiable, often **preventable**, cause. Second most common cause of dementia overall after Alzheimer's. Sits on a continuum: **vascular cognitive impairment (VCI) → vascular dementia**.

## Distinction from Neurodegenerative Dementias

| | Vascular Dementia | Neurodegenerative (AD, [[2026-06-23 — Lewy Body Dementia]], FTD) |
|---|---|---|
| **Cause** | Vascular brain injury (infarcts, white-matter ischemia, microbleeds) | Progressive neuronal loss from protein aggregates |
| **Course** | Stepwise or fluctuating, tied to discrete events | Gradual, insidious, continuous |
| **Cognitive signature** | Executive dysfunction + slowed processing early; memory encoding spared | AD: early episodic memory loss (hippocampal) |
| **Preventability** | Most preventable major dementia (BP, glucose, lipids, AF) | Not preventable in the same way |

The boundary is blurred — the two coexist frequently (mixed dementia), and vascular pathology lowers the threshold at which AD pathology becomes clinically expressed.

---

## Pathophysiology and Subtypes

**General mechanism**: cumulative loss of functional tissue or disruption of critical networks. Damage to **frontal–subcortical loops** (prefrontal cortex → basal ganglia → thalamus) explains the executive/attentional profile. Strategic location matters more than raw infarct volume.

- **Multi-infarct dementia (MID)**: multiple large/medium-vessel infarcts (embolic or atherosclerotic). Classic **stepwise** deterioration; focal deficits match infarct territories.
- **Strategic single-infarct dementia**: one small but strategically located infarct → disproportionate deficit. Key sites: thalamus (anterior/dorsomedial), caudate, genu of internal capsule, angular gyrus, basal forebrain, hippocampus. Abrupt onset.
- **Small-vessel disease / subcortical ischemic VaD (SIVD), incl. Binswanger's**: disease of penetrating arterioles → **lacunar infarcts** + diffuse white-matter ischemia (leukoaraiosis). Driven by chronic **hypertension** and **diabetes** (arteriolosclerosis, lipohyalinosis). **The most common form**; the most "gradual/insidious" subtype — executive dysfunction, psychomotor slowing, gait disorder, mood change.
- **Post-stroke dementia**: new dementia within **3–6 months** of clinical stroke. ~**20–30%** of stroke survivors; risk rises sharply with recurrence.
- **Hypoperfusion (ischemic-hypoxic)**: global/watershed injury from cardiac arrest, severe hypotension, heart failure. Damages watershed zones and selectively vulnerable regions (hippocampal CA1).
- **Hemorrhagic VaD**: intracerebral hemorrhage, recurrent microbleeds, or **cerebral amyloid angiopathy (CAA)** — amyloid-β in vessel walls causing lobar hemorrhages (overlap point with AD).
- **Mixed dementia (VaD + AD)**: very common — autopsy series show mixed pathology in roughly **40–50%** of dementia cases; the two act synergistically.
- **CADASIL**: most common **hereditary** small-vessel disease — **NOTCH3** mutation (chr 19), granular osmiophilic material (GOM) in vessel walls. Presents in **30s–50s** with migraine-with-aura, recurrent subcortical strokes, mood disturbance, progressive subcortical dementia; WMH classically in **anterior temporal poles + external capsule**. CARASIL is the recessive counterpart (HTRA1).

## Clinical Features

- **Decline pattern**: classic **stepwise** (abrupt worsening per event, plateaus between) in MID; **gradual** in small-vessel — so a gradual course does NOT exclude VaD
- **Executive dysfunction (hallmark)**: impaired planning, abstraction, set-shifting, working memory, judgment
- **Slowed processing / psychomotor slowing (bradyphrenia)**: often the earliest deficit
- **Memory**: relatively **preserved encoding**; deficit is **retrieval** (improves with cueing) — contrasts with AD's storage failure unhelped by cues
- **Gait disturbance**: small-stepped/shuffling ("marche à petits pas"), apraxic/magnetic gait, **early falls** — often precedes cognitive decline in SIVD
- **Focal neurologic signs**: hemiparesis, sensory loss, visual field cuts, dysarthria, hyperreflexia, Babinski
- **Pseudobulbar affect**: involuntary laughing/crying from bilateral corticobulbar damage
- **Depression (~20–30%+) and apathy**: strongly tied to subcortical/frontal disease
- **Urinary symptoms**: early urgency/frequency/incontinence disproportionate to cognitive stage (vs. late in AD)

## Risk Factors

**Modifiable (vascular)**:

| Factor | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Hypertension** | Single most important; midlife HTN drives small-vessel disease and WMH |
| **Diabetes** | ~Doubles VaD risk |
| **Atrial fibrillation** | Major embolic risk; anticoagulation reduces it |
| **Smoking** | Accelerates atherosclerosis / endothelial injury |
| **Hyperlipidemia** | Atherosclerosis; managed with statins |
| **Prior stroke / TIA** | Strongest clinical predictor; recurrence compounds |
| **Obesity / metabolic syndrome / inactivity** | Vascular risk cluster |

Others: excess alcohol, OSA, hyperhomocysteinemia, CKD. **Non-modifiable**: age (strongest), male sex, NOTCH3/CADASIL genetics, prior CVD.

---

## Diagnosis

**Core requirement**: (a) cognitive disorder + (b) cerebrovascular disease + (c) a **temporal/causal link** between them (onset ≤3 months post-stroke, stepwise course, or imaging burden sufficient to explain deficits in the right pattern).

**Clinical criteria**:
- **NINDS-AIREN** (1993): most used in research; highly *specific* but *insensitive* (misses gradual subcortical VaD)
- **VASCOG (2014)** / **DSM-5 Vascular Neurocognitive Disorder**: broader, cover the VCI spectrum incl. gradual subcortical forms; rely more on imaging
- **Hachinski Ischemic Score**: older bedside tool favoring vascular over degenerative etiology

**Neuroimaging (MRI central)**:
- **White-matter hyperintensities (WMH)** on T2/FLAIR — leukoaraiosis
- **Lacunes**, territorial cortical infarcts, strategic infarcts
- **Cerebral microbleeds** (SWI/GRE), enlarged perivascular spaces, cortical superficial siderosis (suggests CAA)
- **Fazekas scale** grades WMH severity: 0 none → 3 large confluent
- **STRIVE** consensus standardizes small-vessel-disease imaging reporting

**Adjuncts**: MoCA (more sensitive than MMSE for dysexecutive profile); vascular workup (carotid imaging, echo, ECG/Holter for AF, lipids, HbA1c, BP); amyloid PET/CSF to identify the AD component in suspected mixed dementia.

## Treatment

**No disease-modifying or specifically approved drug exists.** Management = secondary prevention (halt further vascular injury).

- **Blood pressure**: treat HTN, commonly target ~**<130/80** (individualized in elderly to avoid hypoperfusion); ACEi, ARBs, CCBs, thiazides
- **Glucose**: manage diabetes to individualized targets (often HbA1c <7%)
- **Lipids**: **statins** (atorvastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin)
- **Antiplatelets**: aspirin, clopidogrel, aspirin–dipyridamole for non-cardioembolic stroke/TIA prevention
- **Anticoagulation**: for AF — **DOACs** (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) preferred over warfarin
- **Lifestyle**: smoking cessation, exercise, Mediterranean/DASH diet, alcohol moderation, treat OSA
- **Cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, galantamine) + memantine**: only **modest, clinically small** benefit; not routinely recommended for *pure* VaD — appropriate when mixed (AD) disease suspected
- **Symptomatic**: SSRIs for depression; **dextromethorphan/quinidine** approved for pseudobulbar affect; PT for gait/falls; caregiver support

---

## Prevention — The Most Preventable Major Dementia

- **Treating midlife hypertension** is the highest-yield intervention — **SPRINT-MIND** showed intensive BP lowering reduced incident MCI
- Glycemic control, statins, smoking cessation, AF anticoagulation, and stroke prevention all lower incidence
- The **2020 Lancet Commission** flagged hypertension, diabetes, smoking, obesity, inactivity, excess alcohol as a large preventable fraction of all dementia — VaD the most directly preventable subtype
- "What is good for the heart is good for the brain"

---

## Prognosis

- Variable, lesion-dependent. Pure VaD often **stepwise/fluctuating**; subcortical SIVD more steadily progressive
- **Survival generally shorter than Alzheimer's** — median ~**3–5 years** post-diagnosis, reflecting cardiovascular comorbidity and elevated fatal stroke/MI risk
- **Course can be modified** — unlike neurodegenerative dementias, aggressive risk-factor control can slow or stabilize progression by preventing new events
- Recurrent strokes markedly worsen trajectory

---

## Epidemiology

- **~15–20% of all dementia cases** (some series 10–20%); higher relative prevalence in East Asian populations (stroke epidemiology)
- **Second most common dementia overall**, after Alzheimer's
- **Mixed dementia (VaD + AD) very common** — ~40–50% of dementia cases at autopsy; pure single-pathology dementia becomes rarer with age
- Incidence rises steeply with age; somewhat higher in men for pure VaD
- Strong geographic correlation with stroke/CVD prevalence

---

## Current Research

- **Small-vessel disease biomarkers/imaging**: refining WMH/lacune/microbleed/perivascular-space markers; advanced diffusion metrics (**PSMD**); STRIVE standardization for earlier detection
- **Blood–brain barrier dysfunction**: dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI showing BBB leakage as early, possibly causal mechanism and therapeutic target
- **Glymphatic / perivascular clearance**: impaired clearance of solutes (incl. amyloid-β) implicated in SVD and the AD–vascular overlap; sleep-dependent clearance research
- **CADASIL / monogenic SVD therapeutics**: NOTCH3-targeted approaches; CADASIL as a "pure" SVD trial model
- **Fluid biomarkers**: blood-based markers (**NfL**, endothelial/inflammatory) to track SVD and quantify the vascular contribution in mixed dementia
- **Prevention trials**: building on SPRINT-MIND; intensive BP control, lipid lowering, multidomain lifestyle (FINGER-style)
