# Neurolinguistic Programming Research Brief

User query: learn more about "neurolinquistic programming" — interpreted as **neurolinguistic programming / neuro-linguistic programming (NLP)**, not natural language processing.

## Bottom line

Neurolinguistic programming is a 1970s communication / psychotherapy / personal-development system associated with Richard Bandler and John Grinder. It proposes that subjective experience, language, sensory representational systems, and behavioral patterns can be modeled and changed through specific techniques.

The credible research picture is cautious-to-negative: NLP has cultural and coaching influence, but systematic reviews and older counseling-psychology reviews generally find **little reliable evidence** for its stronger therapeutic or health claims. The best stance is to study it as a historical influence on coaching, hypnosis, communication training, and psychotherapy-adjacent culture — while treating efficacy claims as unproven unless tied to specific techniques with independent evidence.

## What NLP claims

Common NLP ideas include:

- People use preferred representational systems: visual, auditory, kinesthetic, etc.
- Language predicates and eye movements allegedly reveal internal representational systems.
- Rapport can be improved by matching or pacing another person's language/body cues.
- Phobias, anxiety, habits, or performance patterns can allegedly be changed through methods such as anchoring, reframing, submodality shifts, and modeling.
- Exceptional performance can supposedly be modeled and transferred.

## Evidence snapshot

### Systematic review in health outcomes

Sturt et al. conducted a systematic review of experimental studies of NLP for health outcomes:

- Search found 1459 titles.
- 10 experimental studies were included.
- 5 were randomized controlled trials and 5 were pre-post studies.
- Conditions included anxiety disorders, weight maintenance, morning sickness, substance misuse, and claustrophobia during MRI scanning.
- Four RCTs found no significant between-group difference; one favored NLP.
- Risk of bias was high or uncertain across studies.
- Conclusion: little evidence that NLP improves health-related outcomes; insufficient evidence to support NHS resource allocation outside research.

Source: Sturt J, Ali S, Robertson W, Metcalfe D, Grove A, Bourne C, Bridle C. "Neurolinguistic programming: a systematic review of the effects on health outcomes." *British Journal of General Practice*. 2012;62(604):e757-e764. doi:10.3399/bjgp12X658287. PMID: 23211179. PMCID: PMC3481516.

### Earlier psychology reviews / critiques

Important critical reviews and evaluations repeatedly cited in overviews include:

- Sharpley, C. F. (1984). "Predicate matching in NLP: a review of research on the preferred representational system." *Journal of Counseling Psychology*, 31(2), 238–248. doi:10.1037/0022-0167.31.2.238.
- Sharpley, C. F. (1987). "Research findings on neurolinguistic programming: Nonsupportive data or an untestable theory?" *Journal of Counseling Psychology*, 34(1), 103–107. doi:10.1037/0022-0167.34.1.103.
- Druckman, D., & Swets, J. A. (eds.) (1988). *Enhancing Human Performance: Issues, Theories, and Techniques*. National Academies Press. Includes evaluation of methods for enhancing human performance.
- Witkowski, T. (2010). "Thirty-Five Years of Research on Neuro-Linguistic Programming. NLP Research Data Base. State of the Art or Pseudoscientific Decoration?" *Polish Psychological Bulletin*, 41(2). doi:10.2478/v10059-010-0008-0.

The recurring critique is that NLP's broad claims are underdefined, mechanisms are weakly specified, and evidence often fails to replicate or is methodologically weak.

## How to learn it usefully without swallowing the hype

1. **Separate techniques from theory.** Some practices, such as attentive listening, reframing, exposure-like imagery, or behavioral rehearsal, may overlap with evidence-based methods. That does not validate NLP's full theoretical package.
2. **Treat "representational systems" and eye-accessing claims skeptically.** These are central historical claims but have not fared well in empirical review.
3. **Compare with evidence-based neighbors.** For anxiety/phobias, compare NLP claims with CBT, exposure therapy, acceptance and commitment therapy, motivational interviewing, and clinical hypnosis literature.
4. **Notice commercial incentives.** NLP has a large training/certification ecosystem; claims may be stronger than evidence.
5. **Read primary sources historically, then read critiques.** Bandler/Grinder explain what NLP was trying to be; Sharpley/Sturt/Witkowski/Druckman help calibrate evidence.

## Suggested reading path

### Quick orientation

- Read a high-level overview to understand vocabulary: rapport, pacing/leading, anchoring, reframing, submodalities, modeling, preferred representational systems.
- Keep a note that NLP here is **not** natural language processing.

### Evidence check

- Start with Sturt et al. 2012 because it is a systematic review with full text available in PMC.
- Then read Sharpley 1984 and 1987 for the preferred-representational-system / predicate-matching evidence base.
- Use the National Academies *Enhancing Human Performance* chapter for a broader institutional evaluation lens.
- Read Witkowski 2010 for the strong pseudoscience critique, but treat its rhetoric separately from its evidence claims.

### Practical comparison

For any NLP technique that sounds interesting, ask:

- What is the precise intervention?
- What outcome is claimed?
- Has this exact intervention been tested independently?
- Is the same effect already explained by CBT, exposure, conditioning, hypnosis, motivational interviewing, or basic communication skills?
- Are there controlled trials, or only anecdotes/testimonials?

## Connection to drug treatment / substance-use treatment

There is a direct but weakly supported connection: NLP has been used or proposed in substance-misuse counseling and smoking-cessation contexts, but the evidence base is thin.

In the Sturt et al. 2012 systematic review, one included study was explicitly about **substance misuse**:

- Study: Gray, US, substance misuse.
- Design: pre-post, community setting, not randomized.
- Participants: substance misusers, with compulsory attendance through the criminal justice system.
- Baseline: 127 participants, 99 described as valid cases; 80 completed; 99 analyzed.
- Intervention: visualization, anchoring, and well-formed outcomes; 2-hour weekly group session plus two one-on-one sessions over 16 weeks.
- Outcome: urinalysis for illegal substances.
- Reported result: abstinence after program was 55% among completers versus 16% among non-completers, but the review reports a non-significant difference between completers and non-completers and treats the evidence as weak.

The same review noted NHS spending on NLP-related services included small amounts for **substance misuse counselling** and **smoking cessation services**, indicating real-world adoption, but not strong proof of effectiveness.

Interpretation:

- NLP may appear in addiction treatment under labels like visualization, anchoring, outcome formation, reframing, rapport-building, and motivational language.
- However, for substance-use disorder, the evidence-supported core treatments remain approaches such as motivational interviewing, CBT/relapse-prevention, contingency management, medication-assisted treatment where indicated, peer/community supports, and integrated mental-health care.
- If NLP-like techniques are used, they should be treated as adjunctive communication/coaching methods, not as a replacement for evidence-based addiction treatment.

Research caution: the substance-misuse evidence in the NLP literature is mainly low-quality, non-randomized, and confounded by completion/selection effects.
