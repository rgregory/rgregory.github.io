# Modal Realism — David Lewis

Modal Realism is Lewis's thesis that possible worlds are not abstract objects, linguistic constructions, or mental fictions — they are **concrete, real, spatiotemporally isolated universes**, as real as the actual world we inhabit.

---

## The Core Thesis

**Every way a world could be, a world is.**

There is a world where Napoleon won at Waterloo. A world where you never learned to read. A world where the laws of physics differ slightly. All of these worlds exist — not as representations or abstractions, but as genuinely existing physical universes.

What distinguishes the actual world is not special metaphysical status. "Actual" is an **indexical** — like "here" or "now." This world is actual because *we* are in it. From the perspective of inhabitants of other worlds, *their* world is actual. No world is privileged.

---

## Why Lewis Believed This — Theoretical Utility

Modal Realism is the best foundation for a wide range of philosophical analyses:

**Modality:** "Possibly P" = there is a world where P. "Necessarily P" = P is true in all worlds. No primitive modal operators needed — modality reduced to quantification over worlds.

**Counterfactuals:** "If the match had been struck, it would have lit" = in the closest worlds where the match is struck, it lights. (Lewis 1973)

**Propositions:** A proposition = a set of possible worlds (where it is true). Propositions are sets of concrete entities.

**Properties:** A property = a set of possible individuals (all individuals across all worlds that have it).

**Mental content:** What you believe = worlds compatible with your belief state.

**Causation:** "C caused E" = in the closest worlds where C doesn't occur, E doesn't occur.

Lewis's argument: all these analyses work, they are mutually reinforcing, and more cleanly than competing approaches. The theoretical cost (many concrete universes) is worth the philosophical payoff.

---

## Structural Claims (*On the Plurality of Worlds*, 1986)

**Worlds are maximal:** A world is a maximal connected spatiotemporal system. Everything spatiotemporally connected to you is part of your world. Different worlds have no spatiotemporal connections — causally and spatiotemporally isolated.

**No overlap:** No individual exists in more than one world. You are wholly in this world. (Necessitates Counterpart Theory.)

**Plenitude:** For every consistent combination of properties, laws, individuals — there is a world. Falls out of the analysis: if "possibly P" means "there is a world where P," we need a world for every genuine possibility.

**Indexical actuality:** The actual world is just the world we're in. No metaphysical privilege. "Actual" is fully indexical.

---

## Ersatz Modal Realism (The Competition)

Most philosophers accept worlds as theoretical tools but reject concreteness. Ersatz (substitute) options:

**Linguistic ersatzism:** Worlds = maximally consistent sets of sentences.
Problem: requires primitive modality to define "consistent."

**Propositional ersatzism:** Worlds = maximal consistent sets of propositions.
Problem: same — what makes propositions consistent?

**Pictorial ersatzism (Armstrong):** Worlds = structural combinations of universals.
Problem: can't represent alien possibilities (laws/properties that don't actually exist).

Lewis's critique of all ersatz views: they smuggle in primitive modality. "Consistent" or "possible" combination always presupposes the modal notion they were supposed to replace. Modal Realism avoids this — possibility is reduced to existence, no modal primitive required.

---

## Main Objections

**1. The incredulous stare**
Surely infinitely many concrete universes with talking donkeys violates parsimony.
Lewis: qualitative parsimony (fewer primitive kinds) matters more than quantitative (fewer entities). One kind of familiar concrete stuff > smaller ontology of mysterious abstract entities with unexplained modal properties.

**2. Epistemological isolation**
Worlds are causally isolated — how is modal knowledge possible?
Lewis: same as mathematical knowledge — rational reflection, a priori, no causal access required.

**3. Actuality**
If actuality is indexical, nothing is *really* actual.
Lewis bites the bullet: there is no "really actual" world. Our world is not privileged. Many find this deeply uncomfortable.

**4. Island universes**
Could a world consist of two disconnected spatiotemporal regions? Lewis's spatiotemporal criterion rules this out by definition — feels ad hoc.

---

## Legacy

Modal Realism remains the most philosophically rigorous possible worlds semantics. Not the majority view — ontological cost too high for most — but the benchmark against which alternatives are measured.

Influence:
- Lewis's counterfactual analysis is standard in causation theory
- Possible worlds framework used even by philosophers who reject concrete worlds
- Counterpart Theory extended by Hazen, Fara, Sider and others
- Set the terms for metaphysics of modality for four decades

Lewis regarded Modal Realism as a serious commitment, not a heuristic. He found the theoretical payoff compelling enough to accept worlds he never expected to visit.
