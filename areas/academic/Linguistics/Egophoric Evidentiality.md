# Egophoric Evidentiality

Egophoric evidentiality is a grammatical category found in several languages — most prominently Tibetan and Quechua — in which the first person speaker's direct, privileged access to their own mental or experiential states is encoded as a distinct evidential value. Where standard evidential systems ask *how* a speaker knows something (witness, inference, hearsay), egophoricity asks *who* the locus of experience is: the system grants the speaker a grammatically special position for reporting their own inner states that is inaccessible to any third party describing the same event. A parent cannot use the egophoric form to report that their child is in pain; only the child, speaking for themselves, can.

## Key Facts

- **Grammatical privilege of first-person experience**: the egophoric form is not simply a first-person agreement marker. It encodes the *kind* of epistemic access — direct, introspective, from the inside — that is structurally unavailable to any outside observer. This creates an asymmetry grammaticalized into the verb system.
- **Philosophical entanglement**: egophoricity is a linguistic instantiation of the epistemic asymmetry between first-person and third-person knowledge that dominates philosophy of mind. Thomas Nagel's "what is it like to be" framing captures exactly what the egophoric form marks: the phenomenal, subjective character of experience that cannot be exhausted by third-person description.
- **Interaction with epistemic modality**: in languages with egophoric systems (e.g., Tibetan), the same morphological territory covers both evidential source and epistemic certainty, making the two categories harder to disentangle than in purely evidential languages like Turkish.

## Philosophical Connections

The concept bridges directly into **First-Person Epistemology**: what is the epistemic status of introspective reports? If I say "I am in pain," am I *reporting* on an inner state or *constituting* one through language (Wittgenstein's challenge)? Egophoric marking encodes the assumption that first-person reports have a privileged ground — a claim that both Cartesian and post-Cartesian philosophy of mind have contested.

Nagel's argument in "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) is structurally identical to what egophoricity grammaticalizes: there is something it is like to be a subject, and no amount of third-person, objective description captures it. The egophoric form is, in miniature, the grammaticalized acknowledgment that Nagel was right.
