# Badiou — Being and Event: Set Theory and Ontology

**Full PDF extracted and processed: 270 pages, 1.1M characters**

---

## Core Thesis: Mathematics IS Ontology

Badiou's fundamental claim: **Ontology (the science of being qua being) is nothing other than mathematics itself.** Specifically, **set theory** — the discourse of pure multiples — pronounces being in its most essential form.

### Why Set Theory?

From the *Author's Preface*:

> "Situations are nothing more, in their being, than pure indifferent multiplicities. Consequently it is pointless to search amongst differences for anything that might play a normative role."

Set theory, through Cantor and post-Cantorian developments (Gödel, Cohen), provides the formal apparatus to think:
- **The pure multiple** — being stripped of all predication, identity, or structure
- **The void** — the empty set, which grounds all multiples
- **Infinity** — the infinite cardinals and transfinite hierarchies
- **Choice** — the axiom of choice and generic extensions

---

## Part I: Being — Multiple and Void (Plato/Cantor)

### 1. The One and the Multiple

**Problem**: Any situation presents itself as a "one" (unified, structured). But what is the being of this situation in itself, apart from the count, the presentation?

**Badiou's answer**: Being qua being is **pure multiplicity** — not constructed, not mediated by thought, but the raw multiplicity that Cantor's set theory discloses.

### 2. Theory of the Pure Multiple (Paradoxes & Critical Decision)

**Historical context**: Cantor's set theory generates paradoxes (Russell's paradox: the set of all sets that don't contain themselves). Traditional ontologies collapsed under these paradoxes.

**Badiou's move**: Rather than reject set theory, **make the axiomatization the solution**. The Zermelo-Fraenkel (ZF) axiom system explicitly avoids the paradoxes by legislating what counts as a valid multiple.

**Key axioms that do the work**:
- **Axiom of extensionality** — two sets are equal iff they have the same elements (identity follows from content)
- **Axiom of the void (empty set)** — there exists a set with no elements ∅
- **Axiom of union** — the union of any collection of sets is a set
- **Axiom of replacement** — images of sets under functions are sets
- **Axiom of foundation** — no infinite descending chains; every set is "built from" the void

**Philosophical significance**: ZF does not *prove* these axioms from pre-existing truths. They are *decisional* — a choice about what being (as multiplicity) must satisfy. Philosophy doesn't precede mathematics here; they are sutured together.

### 3. The Void: Proper Name of Being

The **empty set ∅** is the void — the point where mathematics touches pure being, unmarked, without quality.

> "The void is the proper name of being."

Why? Because:
1. Every set is constructible from the void (via ZF axioms)
2. The void carries **no predicates** — it is indifferent, minimal, pure multiple
3. Within any situation, the void is what is *not presented* — what exceeds the count, what the structure excludes
4. Being itself, anterior to any presentation or structuring, is void

**In logic**: The void is the ground from which the count-as-one (the state apparatus that structures a situation) operates, but it escapes that count. This is the seed of the *event*.

### 4. One, Count-as-One, and the Excess

**Crucial distinction**:
- **Being**: pure multiplicity, indifferent, the void
- **Appearing/Presentation**: the count-as-one that makes a situation intelligible

Any situation is a structured multiplicity. Its structure (the state) imposes a *count* that allows us to say "this situation is one unified whole." But:
- The void remains uncounted
- Sets of sets create a **point of excess** — an element of the presentation whose own presentation is problematic (e.g., a subset that is "too large")

This excess is the site of the **event**.

## Part II: Being and Excess — State and Metastructure

### The State, or Metastructure

Every situation has not just a structure (the count-as-one) but a **state of the situation** — a second-order apparatus that counts the parts.

**In set theory**: If *S* is a situation (a multiple), the state of *S* is the power set **P(S)** — the set of all subsets of *S*.

**Paradox**: The power set is "larger" than the original set (Cantor's theorem). This creates a gap:
- The situation presents multiples
- The state can *refer to* (re-present) these multiples as a unified set of parts
- But some parts are "indiscernible" — the state cannot tell them apart from certain perspectives

This **gap between presentation and re-presentation** is where truth-procedures eventually intervene.

## Part III: Being, Nature, and Infinity

### The Ontological Decision: There is Infinity

Badiou makes an ontological claim: **In the order of being (not of appearance), there exists infinity.**

This is not merely mathematical convenience. It is a statement about *what is*:
- Natural multiples (ordinals) form an infinite sequence
- Infinity is not a limit or potential, but actual — given in the axioms
- The infinite is the "second existential seal" of being

**Why?** Because finite systems collapse into contradiction (every finite structure admits a largest element, which violates the structure's own axioms). Only infinite multiplication escapes closure.

### Cardinals and Cardinality

Beyond ordinals (ordered sequences), there are **cardinals** — measures of the "size" of infinite sets.

**Crucial discovery (Cantor, developed by Gödel/Cohen)**:
- The continuum hypothesis (CH) — "Are there cardinals between ℵ₀ and 2^ℵ₀?" — is **undecidable** in ZF
- Cohen's forcing method shows that ZF + CH and ZF + ¬CH are both consistent

**Philosophical import**: Being itself admits a *gap* — an indecision at its heart. The question of how many infinities there are cannot be answered purely from being; it requires intervention (generic extension), analogous to how truth requires intervention in history.

## Part IV: The Event — Beyond Being Qua Being

### Being's Prohibition of the Event

Here is the paradox:
- **Being** (ontology, set theory) pronounces what is multiple, void, infinite
- But **truth** (the event, what ruptures a situation) belongs to "that-which-is-not-being-qua-being"
- An event is a **break with presentation** — it is not expressible within the language of multiples

**In ontology**: The event is literally *prohibited*. Set theory can describe multiples, but not what escapes presentation.

### The Event as Evental Site

Yet an event can *happen*. It occurs at an **evental site** — a multiple that is:
- Present in the situation (it appears)
- Singular and indiscernible (the state cannot entirely subsume it; it escapes complete re-presentation)

When such a multiple becomes the site of an event, something new emerges that was not previously sayable within the situation's language.

## Part V: Intervention and Fidelity

### The Intervention: Illegal Choice

An event cannot be "proven" to exist by the logic of the situation. Its occurrence requires **intervention** — an extra-logical decision that names the event, declares it has occurred.

This is why Badiou uses **Pascal's wager** and **axiom of choice** as models:
- **Pascal**: In the face of undecidability (does God exist?), one chooses to believe; this choice constitutes faith
- **Axiom of choice**: Given a collection of non-empty sets, one chooses an element from each; this choice is "illegal" (not constructive), yet it is the foundation of modern set theory

The intervention declares a subject where before there was only indifference.

### Fidelity and Deduction

Once an event is named, a **subject** emerges as the faithful process of tracing the consequences of that event. The subject works through **deduction** — reasoning that honors the event's novelty while remaining consistent.

**Four truth procedures** where this happens:
1. **Mathematics/Science** — systematic discovery of new theorems from axioms
2. **Politics** — emancipatory struggle based on a declared equality
3. **Art** — creation of new forms and sensibilities
4. **Love** — the becoming-Two that dissolves the illusion of the One

All are "generic" — they apply to everyone, to no specific predicate.

## Part VI: Quantity and Knowledge — Gödel and the Indiscernible

### The Problem of Infinite Cardinality

After Cantor, the question arises: How many *different* infinities are there? Gödel and Cohen showed this question is **undecidable** — indiscernible within set theory alone.

**Badiou's interpretation**: This is not a limitation but a truth about being itself. Being admits the indiscernible — states of affairs that cannot be entirely determined by the ontological language (set theory).

### The Indiscernible as Generic

Gödel's L (constructible sets) and Cohen's forcing define a **generic object** — a multiple that adds *new information* to the system without violating consistency.

**Generic = indiscernible = not yet named**

This is the formal parallel to an event: the generic object is to mathematics what the event is to history.

## Badiou's Four "Great" Philosophers

Badiou structures his argument around key historical figures:

- **Plato** — First to think the One and the Multiple; being as undetermined multiplicity
- **Cantor** — Mathematician who formalized the multiple; set theory as ontology
- **Hegel** — Dialectical infinity; the return and the nomination of being
- **Gödel/Cohen** — Modern infinities; undecidability; the generic

Each reveals a layer of what ontology can say. The event, however, belongs to poetry and history, not to mathematics alone.

## Philosophical Payoff: Why This Matters

1. **Against relativism**: Being is not indifferent to truth. While situations (multiples) are indifferent to predicates, truth procedures are *universal* and *generic*.

2. **Against foundationalism**: There is no Archimedean point outside the situation. But truth emerges *within* situations through intervention and fidelity.

3. **For emancipation**: Politics is a truth procedure. The event of a collective declaration (equality, justice) is not a psychological or social fact — it is a rupture with what *was sayable* before.

4. **Mathematics as ontology**: This reconciles the Anglo-Saxon formal tradition with continental thought. Both are necessary: mathematics says what is; poetry names what arrives.

## Badiou and Nietzsche: Contrasting Ontological Foundations

While both are radical ontologists, Badiou and [[02-Areas/Learning/Self-Study/Philosophy/2026-04-16 — Nietzsche and Freedom|Nietzsche]] offer opposing answers to the Being question. 

**Badiou's answer**: Being is the structure disclosed by ZF set theory — pure multiplicity, the void as the proper name of being. Ontology precedes truth; the void grounds all multiples; mathematics is the direct statement of what is.

**Nietzsche's answer**: Being is will to power — not a static structure but force, becoming, the creative discharge of energy. Ontology is not discovered but genealogized. There is no ground, only perspective; no fixed being, only eternal recurrence of forces organizing themselves into new forms.

**The philosophical difference**: Badiou uses set theory to escape contingency and genealogy — being is what the axioms state, universal and necessary. Nietzsche uses genealogy to dissolve foundations — "being" is always already a perspective, a creation of forces that could have organized differently. For Badiou, the void is the bedrock; for Nietzsche, there is no bedrock, only the play of forces. Yet both position themselves against liberal humanism: Badiou via universal truth procedures, Nietzsche via the aristocratic sovereign individual. Both demand that humans act at a level that exceeds ordinary being — Badiou through intervention and fidelity, Nietzsche through self-overcoming and the will to power.
