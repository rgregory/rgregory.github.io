# Tariana — Language of the Vaupés

Tariana is an Arawakan language spoken in the Vaupés River basin of the Brazilian Amazon, primarily in and around the town of São Gabriel da Cachoeira. It is critically endangered, with fewer than 100 fluent speakers remaining, all of whom are bilingual or multilingual in Tucano languages and/or Portuguese. Tariana is Alexandra Aikhenvald's primary fieldwork language and the empirical foundation for her 2004 typological monograph on evidentiality.

## Key Facts

- **Five-way obligatory evidential system**: Tariana has one of the most elaborate documented evidential systems, grammatically requiring speakers to choose among five distinct evidential categories on every main-clause verb:
  1. **Visual** — the speaker directly saw the event
  2. **Non-visual sensory** — the speaker heard, smelled, or otherwise sensed the event without seeing it
  3. **Inferred** — the speaker deduces from visible physical evidence (tracks, residue, aftermath)
  4. **Assumed** — the speaker concludes from general knowledge or reasoning, without specific physical evidence
  5. **Reported (hearsay)** — the speaker was told by someone else
  This five-way system is obligatory: omitting an evidential marker is ungrammatical.
- **Contact-induced grammatical borrowing**: the most striking feature of Tariana is that its evidential system was not inherited from Proto-Arawakan — it was *borrowed* from the Tucano language family through intense multilingual contact in the Vaupés region. Tucano languages have obligatory evidentiality; Tariana, under sustained contact, replicated the entire category. This is rare: grammatical categories (as opposed to vocabulary) are seldom borrowed. Tariana is the canonical case study in contact-induced grammatical replication.
- **Vaupés linguistic area**: the Vaupés region is one of the world's most striking examples of a linguistic area (Sprachbund) — a zone where genetically unrelated languages converge on structural features through long contact. The Tucano/Arawakan evidential convergence is the defining feature of this area. The community norm of linguistic exogamy (marrying outside one's language group) has maintained intense multilingualism for generations, creating the contact conditions for deep grammatical borrowing.

## Significance in Typology

Tariana demonstrates that evidentiality is not simply a property of a genetic language family — it can be acquired as a grammatical system through contact. This has major implications for how linguists model language change and the boundaries of what can be borrowed. Aikhenvald's Tariana data is the empirical spine of the argument that grammatical categories can diffuse across genetic lines under sufficient sociolinguistic pressure.
