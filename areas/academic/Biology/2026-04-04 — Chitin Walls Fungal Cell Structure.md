# Chitin Walls — Fungal Cell Structure

## What Chitin Is

Chitin is a long-chain polysaccharide of N-acetylglucosamine (a glucose derivative). It is the second most abundant natural polymer on Earth after cellulose.

Found in:
- Fungal cell walls (all true fungi)
- Arthropod exoskeletons (insects, crustaceans, spiders)
- Some fish scales and cephalopod beaks
- NOT in plant cell walls (cellulose) or animal cells (collagen/elastin)

## Why This Matters Biologically

Chitin is a structural polymer that provides rigidity and protection. In fungi, the cell wall (chitin + glucans + glycoproteins) serves the role that a skeleton serves in animals — it defines shape and resists osmotic pressure.

## Why This Matters Medically — Antifungal Targets

Because humans have no chitin, fungal chitin synthesis is a selective drug target. Two major antifungal mechanisms exploit uniquely fungal structures:

- **Echinocandins** (caspofungin, micafungin, anidulafungin) — inhibit β-1,3-glucan synthase (cell wall); fungicidal for Candida, fungistatic for Aspergillus
- **Ergosterol-targeting drugs** (azoles, polyenes like amphotericin B) — ergosterol is the fungal equivalent of cholesterol; humans use cholesterol, so these agents have selective toxicity

This is the same logic as penicillin targeting bacterial peptidoglycan: exploit structural differences between pathogen and host.
