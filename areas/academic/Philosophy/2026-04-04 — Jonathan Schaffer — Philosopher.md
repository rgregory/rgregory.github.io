# Jonathan Schaffer — Philosopher

Jonathan Schaffer is a contemporary analytic metaphysician best known for reviving and defending **Priority Monism** — the view that the whole cosmos is the one fundamental entity, and that all parts of the cosmos derive their existence from the whole. His work constitutes a direct challenge to the Humean, pluralist metaphysics of David Lewis.

## Overview

Schaffer works primarily in metaphysics, with significant contributions to the philosophy of causation, the metaphysics of grounding, and the theory of questions. He is a professor at Rutgers University and one of the most cited metaphysicians of his generation.

His central ambition is to restore **ontological priority** to the centre of metaphysics — the question of what is more fundamental than what — displacing the Quinean question of what merely exists.

## Key Works

- "On What Grounds What" (2009) — introduces grounding as a fundamental metaphysical relation; argues that ontology must be supplemented by a theory of priority
- "Monism: The Priority of the Whole" (2010) — *Philosophical Review* — the canonical defence of Priority Monism; argues that the cosmos is the one fundamental entity
- "The Internal Relatedness of All Things" (2010) — defends holism about relations; all things are internally related through the whole
- "Is There a Fundamental Level?" (2003) — challenges the assumption that there must be a bottom level of fundamental entities
- "Causation and Laws of Nature: Reductionism" (2008) — engages Lewis on causation

## Core Arguments

### Priority Monism

Schaffer distinguishes two questions:

1. **What exists?** (the Quinean question of existence)
2. **What is fundamental?** (the question of grounding and priority)

He accepts that particles, tables, and people all exist. But he argues that **the cosmos — the mereological sum of all there is — is the one fundamental entity**, and everything else (every part) exists only derivatively, as a mode or aspect of the whole.

This is **not** existence monism (the claim that only one thing exists). It is **priority monism**: many things exist, but only the whole is fundamental.

> "The whole is prior to its parts." — echoing Aristotle's *Metaphysics*

The key argument turns on **entanglement** in quantum mechanics: quantum states of composite systems cannot be decomposed into the states of their parts. The whole fixes the parts, not the other way around. This provides empirical motivation for the metaphysical priority claim.

### Grounding

Schaffer is one of the chief architects of the contemporary **grounding** research programme. Grounding is meant to capture a relation of metaphysical dependence stronger than supervenience:

- **Supervenience** (Lewis's tool): no difference of type A without a difference of type B — but this is a modal covariation claim, silent on explanation or direction
- **Grounding**: X grounds Y when Y *obtains in virtue of* X — a claim about explanatory and ontological priority, not merely modal correlation

On Schaffer's view, the grounded facts hold *because of* the grounding facts. The mental is grounded in the physical; the moral is grounded in the natural; the derived is grounded in the fundamental.

Grounding is:
- **Non-monotonic**: adding more grounds does not automatically yield more grounded facts
- **Irreflexive, asymmetric, transitive** (typically)
- **Hyperintensional**: two necessarily equivalent propositions can differ in what grounds them

### Against Lewisian Atomism

Schaffer's Priority Monism is in direct tension with Lewis's **Humean Supervenience**:

| Lewis | Schaffer |
|-------|----------|
| Fundamental entities are local, point-sized, intrinsic properties | Fundamental entity is the whole cosmos |
| Supervenience: whole depends on parts | Priority Monism: parts depend on whole |
| No necessary connections between distinct existences | Holism: parts are internally related through the whole |
| Pluralism: many fundamental entities | Monism: one fundamental entity |

Lewis builds from the bottom up — local qualities first, global structure as pattern. Schaffer builds from the top down — the whole is prior, parts are abstractions from it.

### The Layered World

Schaffer defends a picture of reality as **layered**: the fundamental level grounds higher levels, and grounding relations structure the hierarchy. But unlike Lewis, he does not require that the fundamental level consist of small, discrete, local entities. The fundamental might be one big thing.

## Relationship to David Lewis's Work

Schaffer's engagement with Lewis is deep and sustained:

1. **Humean Supervenience**: Schaffer's quantum-mechanical argument is one of the most discussed challenges to Lewis's picture. If quantum entanglement means that composite systems have intrinsic properties not fixed by the properties of their parts at spacetime points, then Humean Supervenience fails.

2. **Grounding vs. supervenience**: Schaffer argues that Lewis's reliance on supervenience leaves out the explanatory, directional dimension of metaphysical dependence. Supervenience is too coarse-grained a tool.

3. **Fundamental ontology**: Both Lewis and Schaffer are serious about fundamental ontology — but they reach opposite conclusions. Lewis: the fundamental consists of many small local things. Schaffer: the fundamental is one big thing.

4. **Modal Realism**: Schaffer operates within a broadly Lewisian framework when discussing modality but does not endorse Modal Realism. He uses possible worlds as an analytical tool while remaining agnostic about their concrete existence.
